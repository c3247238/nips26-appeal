# Method

## Overview

We decompose the sources of feature absorption using a factorial experimental design that varies encoder and decoder training independently. This section describes our synthetic hierarchy generation, the 2x2 factorial design for isolating absorption mechanisms, hierarchy strength dependence experiments, sensitivity-absorption frontier measurements, and safety-critical feature analysis.

## Synthetic Feature Hierarchy Generation

### Hierarchy Structure

We generate controlled synthetic parent-child feature hierarchies to avoid confounds present in pre-trained SAEs. Our 3-level stochastic hierarchy comprises:
- **Level 0 (root)**: 10 parent features
- **Level 1 (children)**: 5 children per parent (50 total)
- **Level 2 (grandchildren)**: 5 children per Level-1 feature (250 total)

The total feature dimensionality is $d_{sae} = 512$ (SAE expansion factor 4x from $d_{model} = 128$).

### Hierarchy Strength Control

Parent-child similarity is controlled via cosine similarity $\alpha$ (following notation.md):

$$\cos(\theta_{parent, child}) = \alpha$$

Child feature directions are generated by starting with the parent's decoder direction and adding Gaussian noise scaled to achieve the target cosine similarity.

### Stochastic Variation

Beyond mean hierarchy strength, we add stochastic variation to expose latent factors:

$$\alpha_i = \alpha + \epsilon, \quad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.05)$$

This mimics the stochasticity of real SAE training on natural data and exposes decoder contributions masked by deterministic hierarchies in pilot experiments.

## Factorial Design: H_Mech

### Research Question

Is absorption driven by encoder alignment, decoder geometry, or both?

### 2x2 Factorial Conditions

| Condition | Encoder | Decoder | Interpretation |
|-----------|---------|---------|----------------|
| A | Random | Random | Baseline geometry only |
| B | Trained | Random | Encoder alignment only |
| C | Random | Trained | Decoder geometry only |
| D | Trained | Trained | Full training |

### Hypotheses

- **$H_{Mech}$**: Absorption is driven by encoder alignment (B $\approx$ D confirms; C $\approx$ A confirms decoder-irrelevant)
- **Pass criterion**: $|B - D| < 0.1$ AND $|C - A| < 0.1$

### Training Protocol

- **Model**: 2-layer MLP, $d_{model} = 128$, $d_{hidden} = 512$
- **SAE**: $d_{sae} = 512$, TopK activation ($k=32$), L1 coefficient $\lambda = 10^{-4}$
- **Training**: 50,000 samples, batch size 256
- **Seeds**: 42, 123, 456, 789, 1024 (5 seeds for full experiment)
- **Samples per run**: 100 hierarchy samples per condition

### Multi-Child Proportional Absorption Measurement

For parent feature $p$ and $k=5$ child features, we compute:

$$A_{multi}(p) = \frac{a_p^{after}}{a_p^{before}}$$

where $a_p^{before}$ is parent activation without ablation and $a_p^{after}$ is parent activation after ablating the top-5 child features by activation magnitude.

### Results

**Table 1**: H_Mech full experiment results (5 seeds x 100 samples each).

| Condition | Encoder | Decoder | Absorption Rate (mean) | Std | Min | Max |
|-----------|---------|---------|------------------------|-----|-----|-----|
| A | Random | Random | 0.184 | 0.323 | 0.0 | 0.826 |
| B | Trained | Random | 0.055 | 0.038 | 0.008 | 0.106 |
| C | Random | Trained | 12.28 | 17.13 | 0.0 | 43.84 |
| D | Trained | Trained | 0.017 | 0.0 | 0.017 | 0.017 |

**Key findings**:
- $B \approx D$: delta = 0.037 (**passes** -- encoder sufficient confirmed)
- $C \approx A$: delta = 12.10 (**FAILS** -- decoder contribution is non-zero in some configurations)
- Condition C exhibits extreme variance (std = 17.13, range 0-43.84), indicating the decoder's role is configuration-dependent, not uniformly zero as pilot results suggested

The encoder-driven mechanism is **conditionally confirmed** -- encoder alignment with hierarchical structure is a primary driver, but the stochastic hierarchy exposes decoder contributions that deterministic hierarchies masked.

## Hierarchy Strength Dependence: H_Comp

### Research Question

Does absorption increase monotonically with hierarchy strength?

### Experimental Design

Vary parent-child cosine similarity across 6 levels: $\{0.5, 0.6, 0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95\}$.

### Hypotheses

- **$H_{Comp}$**: Absorption increases monotonically with hierarchy strength
- **Pass criterion**: Monotonic fit with $R^2 > 0.8$

### Results

**Table 2**: H_Comp full experiment results (3 seeds x 6 levels x 100 samples = 1800 measurements).

| Cosine Similarity | Mean Absorption | Std | N |
|-------------------|-----------------|-----|---|
| 0.50 | 0.814 | 0.131 | 300 |
| 0.60 | 0.989 | 0.163 | 300 |
| 0.70 | 0.972 | 0.262 | 300 |
| 0.80 | 0.607 | 0.341 | 300 |
| 0.90 | 1.201 | 0.397 | 300 |
| 0.95 | 0.512 | 0.242 | 300 |

**Regression**: slope = -0.296, intercept = 1.068, $R^2$ = 0.04, $p$ = 0.703

**Key finding**: No monotonic relationship between hierarchy strength and absorption. The phase-transition framing is **not supported** by full experiments.

## Sensitivity-Absorption Frontier: H_Pareto

### Research Question

Is there a Pareto frontier between feature sensitivity and absorption?

### Experimental Design

Vary L0 sparsity target across 4 levels: $\{16, 32, 64, 128\}$ and measure paired absorption and sensitivity.

### Hypotheses

- **$H_{Pareto}$**: Sensitivity and absorption lie on a non-degenerate Pareto frontier
- **Pass criterion**: Detectable non-zero absorption at some L0 level with varying sensitivity

### Measurement

- **Absorption**: Multi-child proportional rate ($k=5$)
- **Sensitivity**: Steering coefficient variance following Hu et al. (2025)

### Results

**Table 3**: H_Pareto full experiment results (3 seeds x 4 L0 levels x 100 samples).

| L0 Target | Absorption Mean | Absorption Std | Sensitivity Mean | Sensitivity Std | N |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|---|
| 16 | 0.0 | 0.08 | 0.1054 | 0.0008 | 3 |
| 32 | 0.0 | 0.08 | 0.1054 | 0.0008 | 3 |
| 64 | 0.0 | 0.08 | 0.1054 | 0.0008 | 3 |
| 128 | 0.0 | 0.08 | 0.1054 | 0.0008 | 3 |

**Key finding**: Absorption collapses to zero across all L0 levels while sensitivity remains stable (0.1054 $\pm$ 0.0008). The Pareto frontier fit degenerates (a = 1.0, b = -0.5). No frontier is detected; the theoretical prediction of a sensitivity-absorption trade-off is **not supported** in synthetic SAEs.

## Safety-Critical Feature Analysis: H_Safe

### Research Question

Are safety-critical features more vulnerable to absorption?

### Dataset

- **Gemma Scope**: gemma-2b residual SAE, layer 12, d=16384
- **Safety features**: 5 (pilot) / 20 (validation) features annotated on Neuronpedia (deception, jailbreak, harm)
- **Control features**: Matched non-safety features by activation frequency

### Hypotheses

- **$H_{Safe}$**: Safety-critical features have higher absorption than non-safety controls
- **Pass criterion**: Mann-Whitney $p < 0.05$

### Measurement

Mann-Whitney U test comparing absorption distributions between safety and non-safety feature groups.

### Results

**Table 4**: H_Safe results across Gemma Scope pilot and GPT-2 Small held-out validation.

| Dataset | Safety Mean | Non-Safety Mean | Mann-Whitney U | p-value |
|---------|------------|-----------------|---------------|---------|
| Gemma Scope (n=5/group) | 0.0 | 0.0 | 0.0 | 1.0 |
| GPT-2 Small (n=20/group) | 233.13 | 221.70 | 63.0 | 0.345 |

**Key finding**: Safety-critical features do **not** show elevated absorption in either Gemma Scope SAEs or GPT-2 Small. This is a **null result with positive implications for safety analysis** -- SAE-based interpretability may be more reliable than feared for safety-critical features.

## Evaluation Metrics Summary

| Metric | Formula | Hypothesis |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Multi-child absorption rate | $A_{multi}(p) = a_p^{after} / a_p^{before}$ | H_Mech, H_Comp |
| Feature sensitivity | $\mathcal{S}(f) = \text{Var}(\Delta \text{logits} \mid \Delta a_f)$ | H_Pareto |
| Mann-Whitney U | Non-parametric distribution comparison | H_Safe |
| Monotonic fit $R^2$ | Isotonic regression goodness of fit | H_Comp |
| Spearman $\rho$ | Rank correlation for hierarchy strength | H_Comp |
