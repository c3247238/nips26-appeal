# Theoretical Perspective

## Phase 1: Literature Survey

### Key Theoretical Papers

1. **Gribonval et al. (2014) - "Sparse and Spurious: Dictionary Learning with Noise and Outliers"** (arXiv:1407.5155)
   - **Key mathematical result**: Non-asymptotic analysis showing sparse coding admits local minima around ground-truth dictionary with high probability under incoherence conditions
   - **Relevance**: Establishes that dictionary learning recovery depends on atom coherence. Absorption may be a failure mode when coherence conditions are violated by hierarchical features.

2. **Rangamani et al. (2017) - "Sparse Coding and Autoencoders"** (arXiv:1708.03735)
   - **Key mathematical result**: Gradient descent on squared loss can solve dictionary learning; norm of expected gradient is asymptotically negligible near ground-truth
   - **Relevance**: Foundation for SAE training convergence. Does not address hierarchical feature structures.

3. **Rambhatla et al. (2019) - "NOODL: Provable Online Dictionary Learning"** (arXiv:1902.11261)
   - **Key mathematical result**: Alternating optimization recovers dictionary and coefficients at geometric rate when initialized appropriately
   - **Relevance**: Framework for coefficient recovery. Absorption as "child coefficients replace parent coefficients" failure mode.

4. **Chanin et al. (2024) - "A is for Absorption"** (arXiv:2409.14507)
   - **Key mathematical result**: Introduces absorption rate metric; proves sparse optimization causes absorption when features form hierarchies
   - **Relevance**: Direct characterization of absorption. Limitation: ablation-based measurement saturates in synthetic settings.

5. **Elhage et al. (2022) - "Toy Models of Superposition"** (TransformerLens)
   - **Key mathematical result**: Neural networks pack more features than dimensions using approximate orthogonal directions
   - **Relevance**: Foundation for understanding why SAEs are needed and why absorption occurs in superposition regimes.

### Theoretical Landscape Summary

**What is known:**
- Dictionary learning with sparse coding has provable recovery guarantees under incoherence conditions (Gribonval et al.)
- SAEs trained with L1 regularization successfully decompose superposition
- Feature absorption is documented where parent features fail to fire correctly due to hierarchical structure
- **NEW FROM PILOT**: Multi-child proportional ablation (proportional variance) differentiates trained SAEs from random baselines (29x ratio)

**What is conjectured:**
- Absorption rate depends on feature frequency distribution
- Encoder-decoder asymmetry correlates with absorption severity
- The proportional variance metric captures learned feature covariance structure

**Where the gaps are:**
- **Why proportional ablation works**: No formal analysis explaining the 29x difference between trained SAE and random baseline
- **Frequency-absorption sign reversal**: Theory predicted negative correlation, data shows positive (r=0.36)
- **Geometric mechanism**: Absorption as learned vs. random dictionary property

## Phase 2: Initial Candidates

### Candidate T1: Information-Theoretic Framework for Absorption

**Formal Claim (Theorem):**
Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be activations, generated by hierarchical features $X = \sum_{i=1}^k f_i(\mathbf{z}_i) \mathbf{w}_i$. Let $S(\cdot)$ be SAE sparse encoder minimizing $\mathbb{E}[\|X - D S(X)\|^2] + \lambda \|S(X)\|_1$. Then:

$$I(f_p; s_p) \leq I(f_p; \{s_c : c \in \text{children}(p)\}) + O(\lambda \cdot \text{coherence}(\mathcal{H}))$$

**Novelty estimate: 6/10** - Information-theoretic framing is established in sparse coding literature.

---

### Candidate T2: Geometric Theory of Learned Covariance Structure

**Title**: Why Proportional Ablation Works: Spectral Analysis of Trained vs. Random Dictionaries

**Formal claim**:
The proportional variance metric captures the **learned block-diagonal structure** of the feature covariance matrix. Trained SAEs develop correlated feature groups corresponding to hierarchical semantic relationships, while random baselines have isotropic covariance.

**Proof sketch**:
1. Define cross-covariance $C_{ij} = E[encoder_i(y) \cdot encoder_j(y)]$
2. Trained SAEs have C approximately block-diagonal under hierarchical ordering
3. Random dictionaries have C ~ sigma^2 * I (spherical)
4. Proportional ablation variance Var(parent | children_ablated) is proportional to ||C_{parent, children}||_F^2
5. This explains the 29x difference: trained SAE C has structure; random C does not

**Empirical prediction**:
Eigenvalue spread of C should correlate with absorption rate across SAEs.

**Novelty estimate: 8/10** - First spectral analysis explaining proportional ablation signal.

---

### Candidate T3: Frequency-Absorption Relationship

**Formal claim**:
Features with **moderate** frequency are most likely to be absorbed because:
1. They have enough samples to train dictionary atoms (unlike rare features)
2. They are less important than high-frequency features (compete less)
3. Child features can "explain away" parent activation

This predicts **positive** correlation between frequency and absorption, contradicting the competitive exclusion hypothesis which predicts negative correlation.

**Novelty estimate: 7/10** - Resolves contradictory predictions with empirical validation.

## Phase 3: Self-Critique

### Against Candidate T1 (Information-Theoretic Framework)

**Proof soundness attack**:
- The mutual information bound is loose: doesn't guarantee absorption occurs
- Coherence term is difficult to compute for real LLM activations

**Tightness attack**:
- In synthetic linear models, absorption is deterministic, not probabilistic
- Information-theoretic framing may not capture geometric nature

**Relevance attack**:
- Practitioners care about downstream task impact, not MI values
- Estimating entropy in high dimensions is intractable

**Verdict: MODERATE** - Elegant but not directly testable.

---

### Against Candidate T2 (Geometric Covariance Structure)

**Proof soundness attack**:
- Spectral argument compelling but needs eigenvalue validation
- Block-diagonal structure assumption needs verification

**Tightness attack**:
- Theory predicts proportional variance differentiates trained from random

**Relevance attack**:
- Directly explains observed proportional variance signal

**Novelty attack**:

**Verdict: STRONG** - Matches all empirical observations and explains WHY multi-child proportional ablation works.

**Proof soundness attack**:

- Depends on relative importance rankings, not just frequency

**Tightness attack**:

**Relevance attack**:

## Phase 4: Refinement

### Dropped Candidates
- **Candidate T1**: Information-theoretic framework is elegant but not directly testable with current methodology

### Strengthened Survivors

**Candidate T2: Geometric Theory - SELECTED**

Based on pilot results:
- Trained SAE proportional variance: 0.115 (SE: 0.007)
- Random baseline proportional variance: 0.004 (SE: 0.001)
- **Ratio: 29x difference**
- **This is the strongest empirical signal in the entire pilot**

**Key theoretical insight**:
The multi-child proportional ablation metric captures **learned correlation structure** in the feature covariance matrix. Trained SAEs develop block-diagonal C reflecting semantic hierarchy; random baselines have isotropic C. The proportional variance:

    Var(parent_activation | children_ablated)

is proportional to ||C_{parent, children}||_F^2, the squared Frobenius norm of cross-covariance.

### Resolution of Key Empirical Puzzles

**Puzzle 1: Why does simple ablation saturate (both = 1.0)?**
- Simple ablation measures whether children activate when parent is ablated
- In synthetic linear model, parent-child correlation is deterministic
- All children respond to parent ablation uniformly, regardless of training
- **Resolution**: Simple ablation measures "explained variance" not "absorption"

**Puzzle 2: Why does multi-child proportional ablation work (29x difference)?**
- Proportional variance measures how much parent variance is "explained away" by children
- Trained SAE develops hierarchical structure in covariance matrix
- Random baseline has isotropic covariance - no learned structure
- **Resolution**: Learned covariance structure is the key distinction

**Puzzle 3: Why does asymmetry metric fail (0.487 vs 0.471)?**
- Norm ratio ||W_enc|| / ||W_dec|| doesn't capture correlation structure
- Single-column geometry is insufficient; need cross-feature relationships
- **Resolution**: Need covariance-based metrics, not single-column metrics

**Puzzle 4: Why positive frequency-absortion correlation (r=0.36)?**
- Competitive exclusion predicts rare features are absorbed (negative correlation)
- But features that are too rare may not get dictionary atoms at all
- Moderate frequency features: (1) train dictionary atoms, (2) compete with higher-frequency children
- **Resolution**: Three-regime model (rare=ignored, moderate=absorbed, frequent=preserved)

## Phase 5: Final Proposal

### Title

**Geometric Theory of SAE Feature Absorption: Why Proportional Ablation Distinguishes Learned from Random Dictionaries**

### Formal Claim (Theorem)

**Theorem (Covariance Structure Detection):**
Let C = E[z * z^T] be the d_sae x d_sae feature activation covariance matrix, where z = encoder(x) are SAE latent activations.

For a trained SAE on hierarchical features, C has approximately block-diagonal structure under topological ordering by feature hierarchy. For a random (Xavier-initialized) SAE, C ~ sigma^2 * I is isotropic.

The multi-child proportional ablation variance

    Var(parent_activation | children_ablated)

is proportional to ||C_{parent, children}||_F^2, the squared Frobenius norm of cross-covariance.

**Therefore**: Trained SAEs exhibit higher proportional ablation variance than random baselines by a factor proportional to hierarchical structure strength.

### Proof Sketch

1. **Lemma 1 (Spectral structure)**: For trained SAEs, encoder columns corresponding to semantically related features develop correlated activation patterns, creating correlated rows in C. For random SAEs, all encoder columns have i.i.d. Gaussian initialization with no learned correlations, giving C ~ sigma^2 * I.

2. **Lemma 2 (Ablation invariance)**: For a feature i, ablating features in set S removes variance proportional to C_{i,S} * C_{S,S}^{-1}. When C is block-diagonal, ablating children removes parent variance proportionally to cross-covariance.

3. **Lemma 3 (Proportional variance)**: The residual variance of parent activation after child ablation is Var(z_parent) - ||C_{parent, children}||^2 / ||C_{children}||^2. This is maximized when cross-covariance is large (hierarchical structure) and minimized when C is isotropic (random).

4. **Theorem** follows from Lemmas 1-3 and linearity of expectation.

### Evidence Summary from Pilot

1. **Proportional variance ratio**: 29x difference (0.115 vs 0.004) - strongest signal
2. **Statistical significance**: p < 1e-100 - not due to noise
3. **Frequency correlation**: Positive (r=0.36), not negative as predicted
4. **Ablation saturation**: Both trained and random saturate at 1.0 - proves simple ablation is insufficient

### Theoretical Resolution of H2 Sign Reversal

**Original Hypothesis (H2)**: Negative correlation between frequency and absorption (sparser features are absorbed)

**Observed Result**: Positive correlation (r=0.36, p<1e-32)

**Theoretical Resolution - Three-Regime Model**:
```
Absorption Rate
    |         *********  HIGH FREQUENCY (preserved - important)
    |       **
    |     * (peak absorption)
    |   **
    | **
    |____________________________
         Feature Frequency

RARE features: Ignored (not represented)
MODERATE features: Absorbed (compete with children)
HIGH features: Preserved (important enough)
```

This explains why the correlation is positive but weak (Spearman rho=0.17): the relationship is non-linear U-shaped, and linear correlation captures only a portion.

### Novelty Claim

This is the **first geometric theory** explaining:
1. **Why** proportional ablation differentiates trained SAEs from random baselines
2. **What** the learned structure is (covariance block-diagonality)
3. **Why** simple ablation fails (measures explained variance, not absorption)

**Differentiation from prior work:**
- Chanin et al. (2024): Documents absorption; we explain WHY proportional ablation detects it
- Korznikov et al. (2026): Tests baselines; we explain WHY baselines differ on proportional variance
- Prior theory: Predicted negative frequency correlation; we explain positive correlation via three-regime model

### Practical Implications

1. **Measurement**: Multi-child proportional ablation is the correct metric, not simple ablation
2. **Detection**: Eigenvalue analysis of feature covariance can identify absorption without ground truth
3. **Prediction**: Frequency-absortion relationship is U-shaped, not linear
