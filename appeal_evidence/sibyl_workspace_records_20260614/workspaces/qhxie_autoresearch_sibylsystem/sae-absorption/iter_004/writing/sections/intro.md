# Introduction

Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) decompose neural network activations into interpretable latent directions, providing a scalable window into the computations of large language models (Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023). A persistent failure mode undermines this promise: *feature absorption*, where rare general latents---such as a "first letter = A" detector---are systematically suppressed by frequent specific latents---such as token-level features for "April" or "Amazon" that co-occur with the general concept (Chanin et al., 2024). On the canonical first-letter task, the reported absorption rate is 15--35% of letter features, measured via probe-directed comparison against ground-truth letter directions.

Three tensions in the absorption literature remain unresolved.

**Measurement versus phenomenon.** The 15--35% rate captures only what we term Type I (full, single-latent) absorption: cases where one child latent accounts for the majority of a parent's suppression. Our three-tier taxonomy (Section 3.3) classifies 24 of 26 letter features as exhibiting some form of absorption on GPT-2 Small (92.3% comprehensive rate), with 23 letters showing partial suppression (Type II) where the parent latent activates at less than 50% of its expected magnitude. Even accounting for likely inflation in the Type II metric---parent features were identified by a selectivity heuristic rather than `sae-spelling` ground truth---the gap between the canonical rate and the partial absorption rate is substantial. The true failure scope of SAEs is plausibly 2--6$\times$ larger than the headline figure suggests.

**Mechanism versus intervention.** Architecture papers---JumpReLU SAEs (Rajamanoharan et al., 2024), Matryoshka SAEs (Bussmann et al., 2024), OrtSAE (Bussman et al., 2025), masked regularization, ATM SAE---report absorption reductions without a unified mechanistic account. We test whether a Lotka-Volterra (LV) competitive exclusion framework provides one. In ecology, when two species share a niche and one has a higher carrying capacity, the weaker species is excluded (Lotka, 1925; Volterra, 1926). By analogy, we define a competition coefficient $\alpha_{ij} = \sigma_{ij} \cdot (f_j / f_i)$, where $\sigma_{ij}$ is the normalized co-activation rate between SAE latents $i$ and $j$, and $f_i, f_j$ are their activation frequencies. The LV theory predicts a sharp absorption transition at $\alpha_{ij} \approx 1$. This prediction fails: on GPT-2 Small, the LV detector achieves test F1 = 0.128 (ROC-AUC = 0.148), underperforming a decoder cosine-similarity baseline (F1 = 0.165, ROC-AUC = 0.201), and a sharpness test finds a linear fit marginally preferred over a sigmoid (AIC difference $< 0.2$). The competition coefficient captures relevant factors---co-activation geometry and frequency imbalance---but the threshold-based competitive exclusion framing is not supported by the data.

**Metric versus impact.** DeepMind deprioritized SAE research after finding dense probes outperform SAEs on safety-relevant tasks, casting doubt on whether reducing absorption actually improves downstream utility. No prior work directly tests the assumed causal chain from absorption to downstream performance. We provide this test using SAEBench (Karvonen et al., 2025) data across 54 Gemma Scope SAEs (Gemma 2 2B, layers 5/12/19, widths 16k/65k/1M). Absorption score correlates negatively with sparse probing F1 ($r = -0.595$, $p < 0.001$), SCR ($r = -0.431$, $p = 0.002$), and RAVEL TPP ($r = -0.454$, $p < 0.001$); all three survive Bonferroni correction at $\alpha = 0.0125$. Unlearning shows no significant correlation ($r = -0.175$, $p = 0.280$). Partial correlations controlling for SAE width, layer, and architecture class *strengthen* these results (partial $r = -0.661$ for sparse probing, $-0.677$ for SCR, $-0.492$ for RAVEL), indicating that absorption captures genuine quality variation beyond confounding structural factors. This falsifies our pre-registered null hypothesis (H3: $|r| < 0.2$) and provides the first direct evidence that absorption reduction translates to downstream SAE improvement.

A complementary hypothesis also fails: corpus co-occurrence statistics (pointwise mutual information between letter categories and tokens) do not predict which features are absorbed. The PMI coefficient in a regression controlling for SAE width, $L_0$, and layer is $\beta_4 = -0.0063$ ($p = 0.593$), with partial $R^2 = 0.0006$. The dominant predictors of absorption rate are layer ($p < 0.0001$) and $\log(L_0)$ ($p = 0.012$), suggesting that absorption is primarily driven by the SAE's optimization objective and sparsity penalty, not by training data co-occurrence patterns. This has practical implications: corpus curation is unlikely to reduce absorption; architectural and regularization interventions remain the correct approach.

This paper makes three contributions:

1. **An unsupervised LV-based absorption detector** that requires no probe directions (Section 3). The detector does not achieve usable accuracy (test F1 = 0.128), establishing a concrete negative result: absorption in SAEs is not well-described as binary competitive exclusion with a sharp threshold.

2. **A three-tier absorption taxonomy** that classifies features as Type I (full), Type II (partial), or Type III (distributed) (Section 3.3). Applied to GPT-2 Small, the comprehensive absorption rate (92.3%) reveals that partial suppression affects the vast majority of letter features, far exceeding the canonical 15--35% Type I rate.

3. **The first systematic test of absorption's downstream impact** across 54 Gemma Scope SAEs (Section 5.4). The strong negative correlations ($r = -0.43$ to $-0.60$) between absorption and SAEBench task performance, surviving Bonferroni correction and strengthening under partial correlation, validate the research community's motivating assumption that absorption matters for interpretability quality.

All experiments are training-free, operating on pre-trained SAEs via SAELens and TransformerLens. The primary model is GPT-2 Small for probe-based experiments; the downstream correlation analysis uses Gemma Scope SAEs via pre-computed SAEBench results.
