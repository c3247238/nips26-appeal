# 8 Conclusion

This paper presents a three-part empirical study of feature absorption in sparse autoencoders, testing an unsupervised detector derived from the Lotka-Volterra competitive exclusion framework, a corpus-level predictor based on pointwise mutual information, and the first systematic evaluation of whether absorption scores predict downstream SAE quality.

**Detection (H1, negative).** The competition coefficient $\alpha_{ij} = \sigma_{ij} \cdot (f_j / f_i)$ does not produce a usable probe-free absorption detector. On GPT-2 Small with the `gpt2-small-res-jb` 24k SAE, the LV detector achieves test F1 = 0.128 at calibrated threshold $\tau = 0.5$, with ROC-AUC = 0.148 --- below the cosine-similarity baseline (F1 = 0.165, AUC = 0.201). The sharpness test finds no phase transition near $\alpha_{ij} \approx 1$: a linear fit (AIC = $-61.05$) marginally outperforms the sigmoid (AIC = $-60.95$). Cross-architecture validation on v5-32k and v5-128k SAEs shows complete failure (test F1 = 0.009 and 0.0, respectively), with mean F1 degradation of 12.6 percentage points. The LV competitive exclusion analogy, while conceptually appealing, does not describe a threshold phenomenon in SAE decoder space.

**Corpus prediction (H2, null).** Corpus PMI has no predictive power for absorption. In a regression over 806 observations across 31 SAE configurations and 26 letters, the PMI coefficient is $\beta_4 = -0.006$ ($p = 0.593$, HC3 robust standard errors), with partial $R^2 = 0.0006$ --- three orders of magnitude below the 0.10 success criterion. The strongest predictors of absorption are layer ($\beta_3 = -0.012$, $p < 0.0001$) and $\log(L_0)$ ($\beta_1 = 0.013$, $p = 0.012$). Absorption decreases in later layers and increases with the sparsity penalty. This null result has a practical implication: corpus curation and co-occurrence disruption are unlikely to reduce absorption. Architectural and regularization interventions remain the correct targets.

**Downstream impact (H3, falsified --- strong positive).** The paper's strongest finding is that absorption score correlates meaningfully with downstream SAE quality across 54 Gemma Scope SAEs from SAEBench. Pearson correlations with three of four downstream tasks survive Bonferroni correction ($\alpha = 0.0125$): sparse probing F1 ($r = -0.595$, $p < 0.001$), RAVEL TPP ($r = -0.454$, $p < 0.001$), and SCR ($r = -0.431$, $p = 0.002$). Unlearning shows no significant relationship ($r = -0.175$, $p = 0.280$). Partial correlations controlling for width, layer, and architecture class strengthen for sparse probing (partial $r = -0.661$) and SCR (partial $r = -0.677$), indicating that absorption captures genuine quality variation beyond confounds. This provides the first direct empirical validation that absorption reduction translates to improved downstream interpretability performance, confirming the assumption that motivates ongoing architectural research into absorption-resistant SAE designs.

**Absorption taxonomy.** On the GPT-2 Small 24k SAE, our three-tier classification identifies 1/26 letters as Type I (full) absorption (3.8\%), 23/26 as Type II (partial) absorption (88.5\%), 0/26 as Type III (distributed), and 2/26 as unabsorbed (7.7\%). The comprehensive absorption rate of 92.3\% exceeds the canonical 15--35\% Type I rate by 24$\times$. This result should be interpreted with the caveat that the Type II rate is likely inflated: parent features were identified by a selectivity heuristic rather than `sae-spelling` ground-truth labels, and the magnitude comparison uses a global mean fallback that systematically underestimates expected activation (Section 5.3).

**Width paradox (H4, partial support).** Mean DAS($k=1$) shows no clear monotonic trend across widths (0.105, 0.104, 0.119 for 24k, 49k, 98k), while DAS($k=3$) decreases from 0.320 to 0.227 before rebounding to 0.260 --- neither metric follows the predicted pattern. Per-letter analysis shows 57.7\% of letters with non-positive DAS($k=1$) slope (close to the 60\% target) but only 42.3\% with positive DAS($k=3$) slope (far from the 80\% target). The width paradox receives partial support for single-child non-monotonicity but the distributed absorption prediction is not confirmed.

**Limitations.** All probe-based experiments use GPT-2 Small as an open-model anchor because Gemma-2-2B requires gated HuggingFace access. The downstream correlation analysis uses pre-computed SAEBench scores on Gemma Scope SAEs, creating a cross-model gap between the detection and impact analyses. The first-letter task is the only absorption ground truth available, limiting generalization to other feature hierarchies. The safety probe experiment (3 SAE configurations, 100 prompts) is underpowered for detecting monotone trends.

**Future work.** Three directions follow from these results. First, replication on Gemma-2-2B would unify the detection and downstream analyses on a single model. Second, causal intervention experiments --- ablating absorbed features and measuring downstream task change --- would test whether the correlational evidence from H3 reflects a causal mechanism. Third, extending the taxonomy beyond the first-letter task to safety-relevant feature hierarchies would determine whether the 92.3\% comprehensive rate generalizes or is specific to the highly structured letter domain.
