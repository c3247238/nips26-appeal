# 2. Background and Related Work

## 2.1 Sparse Autoencoders for Mechanistic Interpretability

Transformer-based language models encode many more concepts than they have dimensions, representing features as overlapping linear directions in a phenomenon called *superposition* \cite{elhage2022toy}. Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) decompose these entangled residual stream activations $x \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$ into sparse combinations of learned features by optimizing the sparse dictionary learning (SDL) loss, which has a biconvex structure:
$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_x \left[\|x - \hat{x}\|^2 + \lambda \|z\|_1\right] \quad \text{subject to} \quad \|d_j\| = 1 \ \forall j$$
where $z = \text{ReLU}(W_e x + b_e) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{SAE}}}$ is the sparse latent activation, $\hat{x} = W_d z$ is the reconstruction, and $\lambda$ controls the sparsity--fidelity trade-off. The biconvex SDL loss is convex separately in the encoder parameters $W_e$ and the decoder parameters $W_d$, but not jointly, producing a landscape of partial minima that underlies several SAE failure modes \cite{tang2025unified}.

SAEs applied to MLP activations in a single-layer transformer produced the first demonstration of approximately monosemantic features recovered by dictionary learning \cite{bricken2023monosemanticity}. Scaling to frontier models---Claude 3 Sonnet \cite{templeton2024scaling} and GPT-4 \cite{gao2024scaling}---yielded SAEs with millions of latents encoding safety-relevant concepts (deception, sycophancy, bias). Architectural variants followed: Gated SAEs decouple feature detection from magnitude estimation to reduce $L_1$-induced shrinkage \cite{rajamanoharan2024gated}; JumpReLU SAEs train $L_0$ directly via the straight-through estimator, achieving state-of-the-art reconstruction on Gemma 2 9B \cite{rajamanoharan2024jumprelu}; and TopK SAEs impose fixed sparsity with clean scaling laws up to 16M latents \cite{gao2024scaling}. The Gemma Scope suite provides 400+ pre-trained SAEs across layers and widths for Gemma 2 2B and 9B \cite{lieberum2024gemma}, and SAEBench standardizes evaluation across eight metrics---including absorption---on 200+ open-source SAEs spanning eight architectures \cite{karvonen2025saebench}.

These advances in architecture and scale coexist with mounting evidence that SAEs face fundamental reliability limits beyond reconstruction quality. Cui et al. \cite{cui2025limits} provide the first closed-form analysis showing SAEs generally fail to recover ground-truth features unless features are extremely sparse. Korznikov et al. \cite{korznikov2026sanity} find that trained SAEs recover only 9% of true features in synthetic settings, with random baselines matching on interpretability, sparse probing, and causal editing tasks. Song et al. \cite{song2025consistency} report that SAE features are inconsistent across training runs: TopK SAEs achieve only 0.80 PW-MCC reproducibility. Feature absorption---the focus of this paper---is arguably the most consequential of these reliability failures, because it creates systematic false negatives in feature activations without any visible degradation in reconstruction metrics.


## 2.2 Feature Absorption: Definition and Prior Work

Chanin et al. \cite{chanin2024absorption} define feature absorption as the systematic failure of a parent latent to activate when its child latent is active, even though the parent concept is present in the input. The canonical example: a latent detecting "starts-with-A words" (the parent feature) has zero activation $z_p = 0$ on inputs containing proper nouns starting with A, because a more specific "starts-with-A proper nouns" latent (the child feature) absorbs the parent's reconstruction contribution. The child latent's decoder direction captures enough of the parent's variance that the sparsity penalty eliminates the redundant parent activation---saving one unit of $L_0$ at the cost of a systematic false negative in the parent's recall. Chanin et al. validate this mechanism on a toy model with hierarchical features and measure absorption rates of 15--35% across Gemma Scope SAEs at widths 16k and 65k. Their supervised metric requires pre-specified probe directions and activation data: it identifies false-negative tokens where all split latents fail to activate, then detects absorption when the highest-ablation-effect latent has cosine similarity $> 0.025$ with the probe direction and exceeds the second-highest by $\geq 1.0$. SAEBench \cite{karvonen2025saebench} incorporates this metric, confirming absorption across Gemma Scope, Llama 3.2, and Qwen2 SAEs---every tested architecture exhibits the phenomenon.

Tang et al. \cite{tang2025unified} provide a theoretical foundation by characterizing absorption as a consequence of the biconvex SDL loss landscape. At partial minima, the encoder direction $w_{e,j}$ for an absorbed parent latent shifts away from its decoder direction $d_j$ by incorporating child decoder components---a geometric signature that our Encoder-Decoder Alignment (EDA) metric formalizes. Chanin et al. \cite{chanin2024toymodels} informally note on LessWrong that comparing top activations via encoder versus decoder directions may reveal absorption; we formalize this intuition into a computable scalar metric with a theoretical lower bound (Theorem 1 in Section 3).

A third relevant theoretical result is the amortization gap proved by O'Neill et al. \cite{oneill2025amortization}: the linear-ReLU encoder used in standard SAEs is provably suboptimal for sparse code recovery. This means encoder-decoder misalignment can arise not only from absorption (our signal of interest) but also from the encoder's architectural inability to compute the optimal sparse code. The amortization gap constitutes a third confounder for the EDA signal, alongside polysemanticity---an issue we acknowledge in our theoretical analysis (Section 3.2) and partially address through the D-EDA decomposition (Section 3.3).

Several architectural responses aim to reduce absorption at training time. Matryoshka SAEs \cite{bussmann2025matryoshka} train nested dictionaries of increasing size simultaneously, organizing features hierarchically so that smaller inner dictionaries learn general parent concepts; they reduce absorption on SAEBench while achieving the best scores on RAVEL and sparse probing. OrtSAE \cite{korznikov2025ortsae} enforces pairwise decoder orthogonality via a cosine similarity penalty, reducing absorption by 65% with linear computational overhead. Narayanaswamy et al. \cite{narayanaswamy2026masked} propose masked regularization that disrupts co-occurrence patterns during training, reducing absorption across SAE architectures. Costa et al. \cite{costa2025mpsae} introduce Matching Pursuit SAE (MP-SAE), an iterative encoding scheme where features are selected sequentially via residual-guided pursuit, allowing hierarchically related features to coexist. Hierarchical SAE designs with explicit structural constraints \cite{muchane2025hierarchical, luo2026hsae} model parent-child feature relationships directly.

All of these mitigations require retraining the SAE from scratch. For the hundreds of SAEs already deployed in interpretability pipelines, no post-hoc diagnostic exists that operates without activation data or known probe directions. Equally, all existing detection methods require running the model forward on evaluation data---precluding systematic auditing of the $d_{\text{SAE}} = 65{,}536$ latents in a typical Gemma Scope SAE. This gap motivates EDA: a weight-only screening metric computable from $W_e$ and $W_d$ alone.


## 2.3 RAVEL and Entity-Attribute Hierarchies

The RAVEL (Relational Attribute Verbalization for Entity) dataset \cite{huang2024ravel} provides structured entity-attribute pairs for probing factual representations in language models. City-continent, city-country, and city-language hierarchies define natural parent-child feature structures: the parent feature (e.g., "continent = Europe") subsumes multiple child features (individual cities), analogous to how the first-letter parent ("starts with A") subsumes specific token children. RAVEL has been adopted in SAEBench as a separate evaluation metric for attribute disentanglement \cite{karvonen2025saebench}, but no prior work has used it to measure feature absorption in entity-attribute hierarchies.

The structural parallel to the first-letter task is precise: both define a parent feature class whose activation should persist when any member child feature is active. The key difference is hierarchy breadth---city-continent has 6 parent classes, city-country has approximately 100, and the first-letter task has 26---providing variation in the frequency imbalance that the sparsity-pressure absorption mechanism predicts should modulate absorption rates. Our cross-domain experiments (Section 5) exploit this variation to test whether absorption generalizes beyond the first-letter task and whether hierarchy structure predicts absorption severity.
