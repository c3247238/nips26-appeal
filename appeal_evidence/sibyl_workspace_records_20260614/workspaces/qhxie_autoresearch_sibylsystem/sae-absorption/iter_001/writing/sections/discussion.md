# 7. Discussion

## 7.1 EDA as a Regime-Specific Screening Tool

EDA achieves AUROC = 0.776 at L12-16k and 0.698 at L5-16k on Gemma Scope (Table 1), with cross-model confirmation at GPT2-L6 (AUROC = 0.629, exact Chanin et al. labels). These results establish EDA as a useful absorption indicator with clear operating boundaries.

The operating regime is mid-layer, narrow-dictionary SAEs. At L19-16k (AUROC = 0.458) and L12-65k (AUROC = 0.468), EDA falls to chance-level discrimination. Two structural factors explain this layer-width dependency. First, mid-layer features (L5, L12) exhibit more hierarchical organization than early or late layers: parent-child feature pairs that produce absorption concentrate in the representation strata where the model transitions from syntactic to semantic processing. The encoder-decoder angular signature that EDA measures is strongest where this hierarchical structure is most pronounced. Second, wider dictionaries ($d_{\text{SAE}} = 65536$) have lower positive-class prevalence --- fewer latents are labeled as absorbed relative to the dictionary size --- amplifying the impact of Neuronpedia proxy label noise on AUROC estimation. The pilot-to-full AUROC collapse at L12-65k (from 0.853 to 0.468) is attributable to this label instability rather than to an intrinsic failure of the EDA signal: the group separation at L12-65k (Cohen's $d$ = 1.145, Mann-Whitney $p = 1.3 \times 10^{-10}$) is the largest in the entire study, confirming that absorbed latents have detectably higher EDA even in this configuration.

The polysemanticity stratification (Section 4.4) refines this picture. EDA achieves AUROC = 0.922 [0.842, 0.979] on polysemantic latents versus 0.643 [0.518, 0.763] on monosemantic latents at L12-16k. Absorption preferentially occurs among polysemantic latents --- those encoding multiple overlapping features --- where the sparsity pressure that drives absorption is strongest. EDA is most discriminative in exactly the regime where absorption is most prevalent. This alignment between mechanism and detection capability makes EDA a natural first-pass filter for auditing SAE dictionaries.

The taxonomy (Section 6) offers a complementary explanation for EDA's regime-specificity. Early absorption --- the dominant subtype at 72--75% of cases --- involves latents whose parent feature was never allocated in the dictionary. For these latents, the encoder-decoder misalignment that EDA measures is not present in the absorption-specific form predicted by Theorem 1; any elevated EDA in early-type latents reflects incidental misalignment from the biconvex loss landscape, not the child-parent suppression mechanism. EDA was designed to detect late absorption (encoder suppressed despite decoder presence), which constitutes only ~13% of cases. This mismatch between the detection mechanism and the dominant absorption subtype explains why EDA achieves partial but not universal detection.

**Practical recommendation.** Use EDA to rank latents by absorption risk, then apply the more expensive Chanin et al. metric to the top-$k$ high-EDA candidates. At L12-16k, focusing on the top 5% of latents by EDA captures the majority of absorbed latents while reducing the supervised evaluation budget by 20$\times$. For wider SAEs or deep layers where EDA alone underperforms, D-EDA provides complementary signal: at GPT2-L10, where EDA yields AUROC = 0.336, D-EDA achieves AUROC = 0.762 [0.686, 0.830].


## 7.2 Why First-Letter and RAVEL Absorption Do Not Correlate

The cross-domain analysis (Section 5) reveals a striking asymmetry. Within the three RAVEL hierarchies, absorption rankings across SAE configurations are highly coherent: Spearman $\rho$ = 0.943 (city-continent vs. city-country), 0.886 (city-continent vs. city-language), and 0.943 (city-country vs. city-language), with a mean intra-RAVEL $\rho$ = 0.924. An SAE configuration that absorbs more on one RAVEL hierarchy consistently absorbs more on the others.

Between first-letter and RAVEL domains, the correlation is negative and non-significant ($\rho$ = $-$0.20 to $-$0.43, $p$ > 0.39 in all pairwise tests after Bonferroni correction). This non-correlation does not imply that absorption is domain-specific or that one measurement paradigm is invalid. Three structural differences between the measurement paradigms account for the disconnect.

**Scale incompatibility.** First-letter absorption uses SAEBench's aggregate split-feature fraction (values ranging from 0.015 to 0.308), while RAVEL absorption measures the fraction of individual latents failing a per-entity activation test (values ranging from 0.0004 to 0.032). These are different quantities on different scales; rank correlation between them is not expected to be informative.

**Hierarchy granularity.** City-continent has 6 parent classes; city-country has ~100. Fewer, broader parent categories produce lower measured absorption rates (city-continent mean = 0.11%) because the parent concept is coarser and the sparsity pressure required to suppress it is higher. First-letter has 26 classes, placing it between these extremes. The number of parent classes directly modulates the absorption measurement, confounding any cross-paradigm comparison. The Spearman correlation between class count and absorption rate ($\rho$ = 0.40, $p$ = 0.60) is suggestive but underpowered with only four hierarchies.

**Probe model mismatch.** RAVEL probes were trained on Qwen2.5-0.5B ($d_{\text{model}} = 896$) as a proxy for Gemma 2 2B ($d_{\text{model}} = 2304$), with directions projected via random orthonormal QR decomposition. None of the three RAVEL probes pass the 85% accuracy quality gate (city-continent: 71.4%, city-country: 37.8%, city-language: 36.8%). This probe quality confound depresses absolute absorption rates and may distort rankings across SAE configurations. Despite this limitation, all 18 SAE-hierarchy measurements exceed 3$\times$ the random baseline, and the intra-RAVEL coherence ($\rho$ = 0.924) indicates the relative signal is robust even with imperfect probes.

The genuine interpretive question is whether absorption severity is hierarchy-structure-dependent or whether a universal absorption propensity exists that manifests differently under different measurement operationalizations. Resolving this requires matched-scale cross-domain measurements with high-quality probes on the same base model --- the most important direction for extending this work.


## 7.3 Negative Results and What They Teach Us

Three pre-registered hypotheses were falsified. Each negative result constrains the space of viable absorption remediation strategies.

**D-EDA does not outperform scalar EDA on Gemma Scope.** D-EDA achieves AUROC = 0.499--0.602 across all six Gemma Scope configurations, consistently below scalar EDA. The exception is GPT2-L10, where D-EDA achieves AUROC = 0.762 versus EDA's 0.336 --- a reversal suggesting D-EDA captures a qualitatively different absorption signature at deeper layers where the encoder-decoder relationship is more complex. The failure mode on Gemma Scope is likely numerical: when $d_{\text{SAE}} \gg d_{\text{model}}$ (65536 vs. 2304), the sparse projection of $r_j$ onto the overcomplete decoder dictionary (Equation 5) is ill-conditioned, producing noisy coefficients $\beta$. D-EDA may require regularization or dimensionality reduction to be practical at scale.

**ITAC achieves 3.14% mean FN reduction, well below the 20% pre-registered target (H5 falsified).** On L12-65k, ITAC reduces the parent latent false negative rate from 7.6% to 6.2% across 10 late-type targets, with the best individual case reaching 18.9% reduction ($j_{\text{idx}} = 61217$). The FVU change is $-$4.23%, indicating no reconstruction quality degradation. The null test on early-type latents confirms 0% FN reduction, validating subtype selectivity: ITAC operates exactly where the taxonomy predicts (late-type latents) and has zero effect where the taxonomy predicts it should not (early-type latents). ITAC's limited mean efficacy is informative: it demonstrates that inference-time correction is geometrically possible for late absorption, but the encoder suppression in late-type latents is more severe than a simple linear projection can recover.

**H6 falsified: wider SAEs do not show lower absorption after controlling for $L_0$.** The partial Spearman $\rho$(width, absorption $|$ $L_0$) = 0.368, remaining positive ($p$ = 0.133). The log-linear model $\log(\text{absorption}) = -31.0 + 0.59 \cdot \log(\text{width}) + 4.78 \cdot \log(L_0) - 0.048 \cdot \text{layer}$ achieves $R^2$ = 0.18, confirming that width, $L_0$, and layer together explain only a small fraction of absorption variance. The positive width coefficient (0.59) means wider SAEs exhibit weakly higher absorption rates at matched $L_0$, not lower --- the opposite of the hypothesis. Simply scaling SAE width is not a sufficient remedy for absorption. The sparsity pressure that causes absorption increases with dictionary size because more latents compete for activation, and the biconvex loss landscape has more partial minima. A methodological caveat applies: Gemma Scope canonical SAEs provide the same $L_0$ per layer regardless of width ($L_0 \approx 72$ at layer 5, $\approx 65$ at layer 12, $\approx 68$ at layer 19), limiting the statistical power of the partial correlation test.

These three negatives converge on a single conclusion that reinforces the taxonomy's central finding. The dominant form of absorption (~72--75% early-type, where the parent feature was never allocated in the dictionary) is a training-time dictionary coverage problem. D-EDA and ITAC target the encoder-decoder interface; they succeed on the minority of late-type latents but are structurally inapplicable to the majority. Wider dictionaries might increase coverage but empirically do not reduce absorption at matched sparsity. The field's remediation efforts should prioritize training-time interventions that ensure hierarchically related features receive dictionary allocation --- approaches like masked regularization (Narayanaswamy et al., 2026), MP-SAE's iterative encoding (Costa et al., 2025), and hierarchically-aware training objectives that address dictionary coverage rather than post-hoc encoder correction.


## 7.4 Limitations

**Proxy labels on Gemma Scope.** All Gemma AUROC values use Neuronpedia-derived proxy labels rather than exact Chanin et al. supervised labels, because Gemma 2 2B is gated on HuggingFace. The proxy labels introduce noise amplified at low positive-class prevalence (65k SAEs). GPT2-L6 with exact labels (AUROC = 0.629) provides the cleanest validation point. Definitive Gemma validation requires model access.

**RAVEL probe quality below gate.** None of the three RAVEL probes achieve the pre-specified 85% accuracy quality gate. City-continent accuracy is 71.4%; city-country and city-language are 37.8% and 36.8%. These low accuracies mean the absolute absorption rates are unreliable for cross-hierarchy magnitude comparisons. The rates relative to random baselines (4.6--100$\times$ above random) and the intra-RAVEL coherence ($\rho$ = 0.924) remain valid as existence proofs that absorption generalizes beyond first-letter, but the specific rate values should be treated as indicative.

**ITAC evaluated on synthetic activations only.** ITAC's FN reduction measurements use synthetic activations generated from decoder columns, not real model activations. The correction's behavior on natural text inputs may differ, particularly for partial-type latents where context-dependent activation patterns could interact with the correction. Real-activation validation is a necessary next step before any deployment recommendation.

**Limited SAE configurations.** The primary analyses cover 6 Gemma Scope configurations (3 layers $\times$ 2 widths) and 2 GPT-2 configurations. With $n = 6$ configurations for Spearman correlations, confidence intervals are wide and statistical power is limited. The scaling analysis ($R^2$ = 0.18) is particularly underpowered; the H6 falsification should be interpreted as "no evidence for a sign reversal" rather than "strong evidence against it." Extension to the full Gemma Scope suite (multiple $L_0$ settings per width) would improve power.

**Single model family for cross-domain analysis.** Cross-domain absorption was measured only on Gemma 2 2B SAEs. Whether the RAVEL absorption patterns replicate on GPT-2, Llama, or other model families is an open question. The GPT-2 replication (Section 4) confirms EDA's cross-model applicability for first-letter absorption but does not extend to RAVEL hierarchies.
