# Introduction

Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) decompose dense neural network activations into sparse, interpretable features, enabling mechanistic interpretability at scale (Cunningham et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023). The promise is straightforward: if each SAE feature responds to a single, coherent concept (monosemanticity), then the sparse decomposition provides a human-readable vocabulary for understanding model internals. Anthropic's circuit tracing in Claude 3.5 Haiku demonstrates that when features are reliable, they enable powerful mechanistic understanding of model behavior (Anthropic, 2025).

Feature absorption undermines this promise. When a child feature (e.g., "the word Saturday") is active, the parent feature (e.g., "starts with S") systematically fails to fire -- not because the parent concept is absent, but because the SAE's encoder allocates the activation budget to the more specific child (Chanin et al., 2024). The parent feature appears monosemantic when inspected in isolation, yet it silently fails on a structured subset of its domain. Google DeepMind deprioritized SAE research partly because absorption degraded safety-relevant feature detection by 10--40% (Karvonen et al., 2025). For safety-critical applications that depend on feature reliability, absorption is not a minor artifact -- it is a systematic failure mode.

The field's understanding of absorption rests on a narrow empirical base. Every published absorption measurement uses a single proxy task: first-letter spelling on GPT-2 Small (Chanin et al., 2024). This task has an unnaturally clean hierarchy -- 26 letters, near-uniform distribution, 100% parent-child co-occurrence by construction. Real knowledge hierarchies are imbalanced (the United States has 176 cities in RAVEL while Chad has 5), multi-level (city $\to$ country $\to$ continent), and semantically rich. Whether the 15--35% absorption rates observed for first-letter spelling transfer to the entity-attribute hierarchies that underpin factual reasoning is unknown.

We present the first systematic cross-domain absorption characterization, extending measurement from spelling to entity-attribute knowledge hierarchies -- city-country, city-continent, and city-language -- on Gemma 2 2B with Gemma Scope SAEs (Lieberum et al., 2024). Using the RAVEL dataset (Huang et al., 2024) as a source of validated entity-attribute pairs and training linear probes across four transformer layers, we measure absorption rates on four distinct feature hierarchies spanning syntactic (first-letter) and semantic (entity-attribute) domains.

Three findings anchor the paper:

**Absorption rates vary 4$\times$ across hierarchies and concentrate at the final prediction layer.** At layer 24 with 16k SAEs, absorption rates range from 11.6% (city-language) to 45.1% (city-country), with first-letter at 27.1% and city-continent at 31.4% (Kruskal-Wallis $H$ = 299.95, $p$ = 7.4 $\times 10^{-66}$, $N$ = 3566). No simple semantic-versus-syntactic ordering holds: city-country exceeds first-letter, while city-language falls far below it. The pattern is hierarchy-specific, not category-specific. Absorption is negligible at early layers (0.7--1.0% at layer 6) and rises sharply to 11.6--45.1% at layer 24 -- a concentration at the model's final prediction stage that implicates task-specific computation rather than generic feature representation. **Activation patching causally confirms competitive exclusion for first-letter but not for semantic hierarchies.** Zeroing child features in the SAE latent space recovers parent probe predictions for 32.5% of false negatives in first-letter (control: 1.5%, Wilcoxon signed-rank $p$ = 0.000218, Cohen's $d$ = 1.33, $n$ = 25 words) -- the first interventional evidence for competitive exclusion in SAEs. For city-continent, however, child zeroing recovers only 0.05% (control: 14.5%, $n$ = 93 entities), with the effect reversed. This divergence suggests two distinct absorption mechanisms: concentrated (single-feature competitive exclusion for first-letter) and distributed (multi-feature absorption for semantic hierarchies where no single child captures the parent information).

**Absorption is 100% pathological.** The hypothesis that absorption might faithfully represent computational redundancy -- that the model does not independently use the parent feature when the child is active -- is decisively falsified. Ablating the parent direction from child feature decoder vectors causes a mean absolute logit change of 3.98 nats ($n$ = 1471 instances from 50 entities), approximately 1000$\times$ the control perturbation (0.004 nats). Zero of 1471 instances qualify as benign at any threshold tested (0.05, 0.1, or 0.2 nats). Every absorption instance degrades model output.

These findings have direct implications for SAE deployment. First, single-task absorption benchmarks are insufficient: architecture comparisons on first-letter alone can be misleading because hierarchy type, not SAE architecture, determines absorption severity (ANOVA $p$ = 0.50--0.75 for architecture effect vs. $p$ = 0.005--0.063 for hierarchy effect). Second, the universal pathological nature of absorption establishes urgency for detection and mitigation methods. Third, the failure of five correlational approaches to predict absorption -- including the Geometric Absorption Score ($\rho$ = 0.116), conditional mutual information ($\rho$ = 0.044), the Absorption Tax ($\rho$ = $-$0.20), and a rate-distortion predictor model ($R^2$ = 0.088) -- motivates a shift from correlational to causal methods in SAE analysis. These negative results, reported transparently alongside the positive findings, delineate the boundary between what current methods can and cannot capture about absorption.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews sparse autoencoders, feature absorption, and the RAVEL evaluation framework. Section 3 describes our measurement methodology: probe training with quality gates, the adapted absorption pipeline, activation patching protocol, and the benign-versus-pathological diagnostic. Section 4 presents cross-domain absorption results. Section 5 analyzes the causal mechanism and hedging decomposition. Section 6 tests architecture invariance. Section 7 discusses implications and limitations. Section 8 concludes with contributions and future directions.
