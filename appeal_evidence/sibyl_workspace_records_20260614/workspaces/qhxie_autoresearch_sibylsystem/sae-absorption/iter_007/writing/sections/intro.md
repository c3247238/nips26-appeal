# 1 Introduction

Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) decompose the polysemantic activations of large language models into sparse, interpretable features---the primary unsupervised tool for mechanistic interpretability at scale (Cunningham et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023; Templeton et al., 2024). Feature absorption undermines this decomposition: a general "parent" SAE latent silently fails to fire on inputs where a more specific "child" latent is active, even though the parent concept is present (Chanin et al., 2024). The failure is invisible to standard evaluation---the parent feature achieves high precision on its activating examples but has systematic, undetected recall holes.

Chanin et al. (2024; NeurIPS 2025 Oral) measured absorption rates of 15--35% across hundreds of SAEs on first-letter spelling using GPT-2 Small with L1-ReLU SAEs. This finding triggered an architectural mitigation wave: Matryoshka SAE (Bussmann et al., 2025; ICML 2025), OrtSAE (Korznikov et al., 2025), ATM-SAE (Li et al., 2025), and masked regularization (Narayanaswamy et al., 2026) all propose encoder modifications to reduce absorption. Every mitigation benchmarks against the Chanin metric and assumes it measures competitive exclusion---a causal mechanism where child latents actively suppress parent latents under sparsity pressure. No study has validated this assumption on JumpReLU SAEs, the architecture that dominates the Gemma Scope ecosystem with 400+ pretrained SAEs (Lieberum et al., 2024). JumpReLU SAEs impose hard per-latent activation thresholds $\theta_j$ rather than soft L1 penalties, creating a binary activation regime (fire or zero) that could alter both the mechanism and measurement of absorption. The Chanin metric's thresholds---cosine $\geq 0.025$ for candidate identification, magnitude gap $\geq 1.0$ for absorption confirmation---were calibrated on L1-ReLU SAEs and have never been tested on JumpReLU.

Three open questions motivate our study:

**Q1 -- Metric validity.** Does the Chanin absorption metric transfer from L1-ReLU SAEs on GPT-2 Small to JumpReLU SAEs on Gemma 2 2B? What fraction of measured "absorption" reflects genuine competitive exclusion versus hedging---information spreading across many latents due to insufficient capacity (Chanin & Garriga-Alonso, 2025)---or metric artifact?

**Q2 -- Sparsity dynamics.** How does absorption scale with the $L_0$ operating point (the configured number of active SAE latents per forward pass)? Is there a sparsity threshold below which absorption becomes negligible?

**Q3 -- Theoretical criterion.** Can conditional mutual information (CMI) predict which parent-child feature pairs are susceptible to absorption, and at what sparsity level absorption becomes information-theoretically unavoidable?

We audit the Chanin metric on Gemma 2 2B with Gemma Scope JumpReLU SAEs across five hierarchy domains (first-letter spelling, city-country, city-continent, city-language, animal-class) and report three findings.

**1. The absorption metric does not transfer to JumpReLU SAEs (Section 4).** Shuffled-label controls produce higher "absorption" than true labels in all five tested domains: 74.6% shuffled versus 15.96% measured on first-letter (4.7$\times$), 45.2% versus 6.49% on city-continent, 39.3% versus 1.43% on animal-class, 18.0% versus 6.56% on city-language, and 10.3% versus 0.0% on city-country. The structural cause is candidate explosion in high dimensions: at cosine $\geq 0.025$ in $\mathbb{R}^{2304}$, a random unit vector identifies 3,766 of 16,384 decoder columns (23.0%), making the candidate identification step vacuous ($P(\text{at least 1 candidate fires}) = 1.0$ at $L_0 = 82$ for all probe types). The metric effectively reduces to the false-negative rate, which is higher for shuffled labels (bad probes) than true labels (good probes). This control failure persists across all 20 threshold combinations in a 5$\times$4 parameter sweep (CV = 0.077), confirming it is structural rather than an artifact of threshold choice. Confound decomposition at $L_0 = 22$---where all 25 probes achieve F1 = 1.0---classifies 98.6% of 656 false negatives (FNs) as hedging under a permissive definition (any token that ceases to be a FN at a higher $L_0$). A strict parent-latent check, requiring that at least 1 of the $k = 5$ parent-associated latents fires at $L_0 = 176$, reduces the hedging rate to 6.2% (95% bootstrap CI: [4.4%, 8.2%]; significantly above shuffled control of 3.4%, $z = 3.51$, $p < 0.001$). Activation patching on 8 persistent core words---tokens that remain FN at all four tested $L_0$ values $\{22, 41, 82, 176\}$---finds 0/8 parent recovery after child zeroing (0/65 random-feature controls also show no recovery), ruling out competitive exclusion for these tokens.

**2. Absorption declines monotonically with $L_0$, exhibiting a phase transition (Section 5).** On L12-16k, the absorption rate drops from 42.85% at $L_0 = 22$ to 37.49% at $L_0 = 41$, 14.39% at $L_0 = 82$, and 0.84% at $L_0 = 176$ (Spearman $\rho_s = -1.0$; bootstrap CIs at each $L_0$ do not overlap). A sharp transition occurs between $L_0 \approx 40$ and $L_0 \approx 80$, where absorption drops from $>$35% to $<$15%. This profile is stable across layers 10, 12, and 20 at $L_0 = 82$ (13.88%, 14.39%, 13.55%; CV = 8.6%), establishing $L_0$---not encoder architecture---as the primary control parameter for absorption severity.

**3. The CMI-absorption association does not replicate with perfect probes (Section 6).** At $L_0 = 82$, where probe quality varies across letters (mean F1 = 0.817), CMI at subspace dimension $d' = 10$ shows a marginal negative correlation with absorption ($\rho_s = -0.383$, $p = 0.059$; Cohen's $d = -0.924$). After Bonferroni correction the $p$-value is 0.236. Replication at $L_0 = 22$---where all 25 probes achieve F1 = 1.0, eliminating the probe quality confound---yields $\rho_s = 0.044$ ($p = 0.835$), with the sign reversed. Partial correlation controlling for probe F1 at $L_0 = 82$ weakens the signal to $\rho_s = -0.328$ ($p = 0.118$); restricting to probes with F1 $> 0.85$ ($n = 10$) further reduces it to $\rho_s = -0.113$ ($p = 0.757$). The correlation between absorption and probe F1 ($\rho_s = -0.69$, $p < 0.001$) is the driver, not rate-distortion theory.

These results recommend validating absorption metrics on new SAE architectures before building mitigations, and indicate that hedging---not competitive exclusion---is the dominant false-negative mechanism on JumpReLU SAEs at typical $L_0$ operating points.
