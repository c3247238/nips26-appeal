# 7 Discussion

## 7.1 Summary of Contributions

Three findings emerge from this audit. First, the Chanin absorption metric does not transfer from L1-ReLU Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) on GPT-2 Small to JumpReLU SAEs on Gemma 2 2B. Shuffled-label controls exceed measured absorption in all five hierarchy domains (Table 2), candidate explosion in $\mathbb{R}^{2304}$ renders the candidate identification step vacuous (Figure 1), and strict hedging accounts for only 6.2% of false negatives at $L_0=22$ (Table 4). Activation patching on the 8 persistent core words yields 0/8 parent recovery (Table 5), ruling out competitive exclusion for these tokens.

Second, absorption declines monotonically from 42.85% at $L_0=22$ to 0.84% at $L_0=176$, with a phase transition in the $L_0 \approx 40$--80 range that is stable across layers 10, 12, and 20 (CV = 8.6%; Figure 3). The $L_0$ operating point, not SAE architecture or layer position, is the primary determinant of measured absorption.

Third, the CMI-absorption association observed at $L_0=82$ ($\rho_s = -0.383$, $p = 0.059$) does not replicate at $L_0=22$ where all 25 probes achieve F1 = 1.0 ($\rho_s = 0.044$, $p = 0.835$; Bonferroni $p = 1.0$). The sign is reversed, and the correlation at $L_0=82$ was driven by probe quality ($\rho_s$(absorption, probe F1) $= -0.692$, $p < 0.001$), not by rate-distortion theory. Partial correlation controlling for probe F1 reduces the signal further ($\rho_s = -0.328$, $p = 0.118$), and restriction to the 10 letters with F1 $> 0.85$ eliminates it entirely ($\rho_s = -0.113$, $p = 0.757$).

## 7.2 Two Interpretations of the Core Findings

The data admit two readings that we cannot fully disambiguate.

**Interpretation A (metric miscalibration).** The Chanin metric is fundamentally miscalibrated for JumpReLU SAEs. At the standard cosine threshold $\tau_{\cos} = 0.025$ in $\mathbb{R}^{2304}$, a random unit vector identifies 3,766 of 16,384 decoder columns as candidates (23.0%), and a true $k$-sparse probe identifies 3,478 (21.2%). With $L_0 = 82$ active latents per token, the probability that at least one candidate fires is 1.0 for random, true, and shuffled probes alike (Table in Section 4.2). The candidate identification step provides no discriminative power, and the metric reduces to the false-negative rate of the probe. Threshold sensitivity analysis confirms this: absorption rate varies by only CV = 0.077 across a $5 \times 4$ grid of cosine thresholds and magnitude gaps (Table 3), and shuffled-label controls exceed measured absorption at all 20 parameter combinations. Under this reading, the measured 15.96% absorption rate at $L_0 = 82$ is a probe artifact, and the 0/8 activation patching result indicates that the model simply does not encode first-letter information via parent SAE latents for these tokens.

**Interpretation B (genuine low absorption).** JumpReLU SAEs at $L_0 \geq 82$ genuinely exhibit low competitive exclusion. The metric works correctly in the sense that it detects few false negatives, and few false negatives exist. The universal control failure reflects that shuffled labels produce worse probes (lower F1), which generate more false negatives, which the metric faithfully detects. Under this reading, the 0/8 activation patching result confirms that competitive exclusion is rare, and the $L_0$ phase transition reflects a real decrease in information loss as capacity increases.

Both interpretations agree on the empirical facts: absorption is low at $L_0 \geq 82$, the metric cannot distinguish hedging from competitive exclusion, and the CMI diagnostic does not replicate with perfect probes. They disagree on whether the low measured rate reflects a measurement failure or a genuine property of JumpReLU SAEs. The activation patching result (0/8 recovery) cannot fully resolve this ambiguity: the 8 persistent core words may be unrepresentative of the broader false-negative population. Scaling activation patching beyond these 8 words is the clearest path to disambiguation.

## 7.3 Implications for the Mitigation Wave

Matryoshka SAE (Bussmann et al., ICML 2025), OrtSAE (Korznikov et al., 2025), ATM-SAE (Li et al., 2025), and masked regularization (Narayanaswamy et al., 2026) all benchmark their absorption reduction against the Chanin metric. Our findings raise two concerns for this mitigation literature.

First, if the metric measures false-negative rate rather than competitive exclusion (Interpretation A), then absorption reductions reported by these methods may reflect changes in probe behavior rather than changes in the underlying feature dynamics. The metric would need to be recalibrated---or replaced with a causal diagnostic such as activation patching---before mitigation benchmarks on JumpReLU SAEs can be interpreted.

Second, the $L_0$ phase transition (42.85% at $L_0 = 22$ to 0.84% at $L_0 = 176$, cross-layer CV $< 10\%$) identifies the $L_0$ operating point as the dominant control parameter. Architectural interventions that do not modify the sparsity-fidelity tradeoff may be secondary to simply operating at a higher $L_0$. The sharp transition between $L_0 \approx 40$ and $L_0 \approx 80$ suggests a critical capacity threshold: below it, the dictionary is too sparse to represent both parent and child features simultaneously; above it, sufficient capacity exists for separate encoding. Mitigation methods should report their effective $L_0$ alongside absorption measurements to disentangle architectural effects from sparsity effects.

## 7.4 Negative Results as Contributions

Four of seven hypotheses were falsified: H2 (hierarchy-driven dominance: predicted $>80\%$ hierarchy-driven, observed 1.4%), H5 (cross-domain rates: all domains below shuffled controls), H6 (cross-domain entity matching: zero matching pairs), and H7 ($L_1$ vs. JumpReLU bimodality: both distributions bimodal). Each was reported with pre-registered targets and observed values.

The CMI non-replication is instructive for the broader mechanistic interpretability community. The marginal signal at $L_0 = 82$ ($\rho_s = -0.383$, uncorrected $p = 0.059$) appeared consistent with rate-distortion theory and had a large effect size (Cohen's $d = -0.924$). Leave-one-out analysis showed no single influential letter (max $|\Delta\rho_s| = 0.088$, letter V), and the jackknife bias-corrected estimate was $\rho_s = -0.397$. The signal appeared robust by standard criteria. It failed to replicate because it tracked probe quality, not information-theoretic structure: at $L_0 = 22$, where all probes are perfect, the correlation vanishes ($\rho_s = 0.044$). At higher subspace dimensions ($d' = 30$: $\rho_s = 0.410$, $p = 0.042$; $d' = 50$: $\rho_s = 0.483$, $p = 0.014$), the sign reverses to positive at $L_0 = 22$, contradicting the theoretical prediction. This outcome illustrates three lessons: (a) marginal signals require replication across operating regimes, (b) confounders must be controlled before theoretical interpretation, and (c) dimension sensitivity analysis is essential for $k$-NN mutual information estimators.

The strict-versus-permissive hedging discrepancy (6.2% vs. 98.6%) provides a methodological warning. The permissive definition---any token that ceases to be a false negative at a higher $L_0$---is trivially satisfied when $L_0 = 176$ resolves nearly all false negatives (only 9/1,196 remain). The strict definition---requiring at least 1 of the $k = 5$ parent-associated latents to fire at $L_0 = 176$---yields a dramatically lower rate. 93.8% of false-negative tokens at $L_0 = 22$ have none of their 5 parent-associated latents firing even at $L_0 = 176$, indicating that these tokens are not resolved by the specific parent latents but by entirely different features. Letter G is an outlier (90.5% strict hedging, 19/21 false-negative tokens), accounting for 46% of all strict hedging cases. The strict rate of 6.2% is significantly above the shuffled control (3.4%, $z = 3.51$, $p < 0.001$), confirming a real but small signal.

## 7.5 Limitations

**Single model family.** All primary experiments use Gemma 2 2B with Gemma Scope JumpReLU SAEs. The control failure, hedging dominance, and $L_0$ phase transition have not been validated on other model families (Llama, Mistral) or other SAE training methods. The confounded cross-architecture comparison with GPT-2 Small ($L_1$-ReLU, 61.65%--67.29% absorption) cannot isolate the effect of SAE architecture from model size (768 vs. 2,304 hidden dimensions), vocabulary, and training data.

**First-letter spelling as primary hierarchy.** First-letter features are weakly represented graphemic information with unusual hierarchy properties: depth-2, branching factor $\sim 40$, and near-perfect co-occurrence between parent and child tokens. This may overestimate absorption severity relative to semantic hierarchies. Cross-domain measurements (city-country, city-continent, city-language, animal-class) were intended to address this concern, but universal control failure in all four additional domains prevents their interpretation.

**Small activation patching sample.** The 0/8 parent recovery result, while consistent across three patching methods (primary decode-reencode, all-children zeroed, residual subtraction) and validated by 10 control features per word (0/80 spurious recoveries), is based on 8 words---the persistent core tokens that are false negatives at all four tested $L_0$ values. These words may represent a special subset (e.g., high-frequency function words: *other*, *often*, *under*, *until*, *offer*) that are not representative of the broader false-negative population at any single $L_0$.

**CMI estimation fragility.** The $k$-NN mutual information estimator operates in a $d'$-dimensional decoder subspace, and results depend on the choice of $d'$. At $L_0 = 82$, the sign reverses at $d' \geq 20$ (Figure 5). At $L_0 = 22$, the correlation is positive at all tested dimensions ($d' \in \{10, 20, 30, 50\}$) and reaches $\rho_s = 0.483$ ($p = 0.014$) at $d' = 50$---opposite to the theoretical prediction. The instability across both $L_0$ and $d'$ undermines any theoretical interpretation of the CMI diagnostic.

**Training-free constraint.** This study evaluates pretrained SAEs only. It cannot assess whether retraining SAEs with explicit hierarchy-aware losses, modified regularization, or different sparsity schedules would change absorption dynamics.

## 7.6 Future Directions

**Activation patching at scale.** The most direct extension is scaling activation patching beyond 8 words. Patching the full set of 186 false negatives at $L_0 = 82$ (or the 656 at $L_0 = 22$) would establish the prevalence of competitive exclusion across the entire false-negative population, resolving the central ambiguity between Interpretations A and B.

**Decoder geometry analysis.** The candidate explosion finding (23.0% of decoder columns identified as candidates by a random vector) suggests that unsupervised absorption detection may be possible through decoder geometry alone, without requiring probes. Analyzing the angular structure of decoder columns---identifying clusters of near-parallel columns that may share parent-child relationships---could provide a metric-independent characterization of absorption susceptibility.

**Cross-model validation.** Replicating the $L_0$ phase transition on Gemma 2 9B, Gemma 2 27B (which have Gemma Scope SAEs available), and Llama 3.1 family models with independently trained SAEs would establish whether the transition zone ($L_0 \approx 40$--80) is universal or model-dependent. The cross-layer stability (CV $< 10\%$) observed at layers 10, 12, and 20 within Gemma 2 2B is suggestive but insufficient.

**SAE retraining experiments.** Training SAEs with explicit hierarchy losses (e.g., penalizing parent-child decoder cosine similarity or incentivizing parent latent firing on child-positive inputs) would test whether absorption is an inherent consequence of the SAE training objective or a fixable artifact. The finding that absorption is primarily controlled by $L_0$ suggests that sparsity schedule modifications may be more effective than architectural changes.

**Immunological imprinting analogy.** An analogy from immunology generates a distinct testable prediction: if absorption operates through feature-level "original antigenic sin," then semantically unrelated features that frequently co-occur during training should exhibit cross-reactive absorption. Testing this prediction requires identifying co-occurring but semantically independent features and measuring whether one absorbs the other---a qualitatively different mechanism from the hierarchical absorption studied here.
