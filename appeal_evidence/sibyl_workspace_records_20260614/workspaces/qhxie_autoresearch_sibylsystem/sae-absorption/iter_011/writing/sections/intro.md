# Introduction

Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) decompose dense neural network activations into sparse, interpretable features (Cunningham et al., 2024; Bricken et al., 2023). When SAE features reliably represent their target concepts, they enable mechanistic analysis of language model internals: Anthropic's circuit tracing in Claude 3.5 Haiku uses SAE features to identify planning-ahead behavior and jailbreak mechanisms (Lindsey et al., 2025). Google DeepMind's Gemma Scope provides over 400 open pre-trained SAEs for Gemma 2 models (Lieberum et al., 2024), and SAEBench standardizes evaluation across eight metrics and seven architectures (Karvonen et al., 2025).

Feature absorption undermines this reliability. When a child feature (e.g., "the city Paris") fires in the SAE encoder, the corresponding parent feature (e.g., "in Europe") systematically fails to activate---even though a linear probe correctly classifies the raw activations for the parent concept (Chanin et al., 2024). The parent feature appears monosemantic from its activation examples, but it has systematic gaps in recall that are invisible without ground-truth supervision. Chanin et al. prove in a two-layer toy model that absorption arises from the sparsity objective: the SAE achieves lower $L_0$ by encoding parent information into child decoder vectors rather than activating a separate parent feature. Google DeepMind deprioritized SAE research partly because absorption degraded safety-relevant feature detection by 10--40% (Smith et al., 2025).

Every published measurement of feature absorption uses a single proxy task: classifying the first letter of a word (Chanin et al., 2024; Karvonen et al., 2025). This task has properties that may not generalize: 26 equiprobable parent classes, near-perfect probe accuracy ($F_1 = 1.0$), and 100% parent-child co-occurrence by construction. Safety-relevant features live in knowledge and reasoning space, not spelling space. Whether the 15--35% absorption rates measured on first-letter spelling transfer to semantic hierarchies---where probes are imperfect, class distributions are imbalanced, and parent-child relationships are contextual---is unknown.

Two compounding problems motivate this work. First, no study measures absorption on semantic knowledge hierarchies, leaving the field unable to assess whether first-letter results are typical or anomalous. Second, imperfect probes are themselves a potential confound: if lower probe $F_1$ inflates measured absorption rates, then cross-domain comparisons are unreliable without controlling for probe quality. These problems are entangled---one cannot interpret cross-domain absorption variation without first quantifying the probe quality confound.

We address both problems using RAVEL (Huang et al., 2024), a dataset of entity-attribute pairs with validated probes for city-country, city-continent, and city-language relationships, combined with first-letter spelling as a control task. All experiments use Gemma 2 2B with Gemma Scope JumpReLU SAEs across four transformer layers and two dictionary widths. Three findings emerge.

**Probe quality is a major confound in absorption measurement.** A probe degradation ablation---intentionally degrading first-letter probe quality via weight noise injection to seven $F_1$ levels from 0.70 to 1.0---establishes a monotonic relationship between probe $F_1$ and measured absorption rate (Spearman $\rho = -1.0$, linear $R^2 = 0.777$, $p = 0.009$). Overlaying RAVEL hierarchies on this curve decomposes their absorption rates into probe-explained and residual components. City-continent absorption (31.4% at $F_1 = 0.87$) falls within 0.6 percentage points (pp) of the curve prediction, indicating its elevated rate is fully explained by probe quality. City-language (11.6% at $F_1 = 0.82$) sits 21.3 pp *below* the curve prediction---a genuine hierarchy-specific outlier that probe quality alone cannot explain (Figure 1).

**Activation patching confirms a universal competitive exclusion mechanism.** Zeroing the identified child feature in the SAE latent space and measuring parent probe recovery provides interventional evidence for competitive exclusion across all tested hierarchy types: first-letter (Cohen's $d = 1.33$, $p = 0.000218$), city-continent ($d = 1.50$, $p < 10^{-20}$), and city-language ($d = 0.75$, $p < 10^{-18}$). Control features produce 1.5--6.8% recovery. The mechanism is universal, not specific to the first-letter domain, though recovery magnitude varies across hierarchies.

**Cross-domain absorption rates span a quality-gated 2.7$\times$ range.** At layer 24 with 16k-feature SAEs, absorption rates are: city-language 11.6%, first-letter 27.1%, city-continent 31.4%, and city-country 45.1%. Within-RAVEL variation is statistically significant (Kruskal-Wallis $p = 7.4 \times 10^{-66}$). After accounting for probe quality, city-language vs. first-letter remains significant ($p_{\text{Bonf}} = 0.003$), while city-continent vs. first-letter does not ($p_{\text{Bonf}} = 1.0$). The quality-gated range between the two hierarchies that survive confound analysis (city-language 11.6% vs. city-continent 31.4%) is 2.7$\times$. First-letter (27.1%) falls within this range, not at either extreme---it is neither a best-case nor worst-case proxy.

Four correlational approaches to predicting which features will be absorbed---the Geometric Absorption Score (GAS, $\rho = 0.12$), conditional mutual information (CMI, $\rho = 0.044$, $p = 0.84$), the Absorption Tax framework ($\rho = -0.20$), and a rate-distortion three-factor model ($R^2 = 0.104$, all individual predictors in the wrong direction)---all fail. These negative results are reported transparently in the appendices.

Our contributions are:

1. **Probe degradation methodology for absorption measurement.** We introduce a probe degradation ablation that quantifies the relationship between probe $F_1$ and measured absorption ($R^2 = 0.777$, Spearman $\rho = -1.0$, $p = 0.009$), establishing probe quality as a previously unquantified confound. This methodology produces a three-way decomposition---probe-explained, residual, and genuine hierarchy effect---that is directly reusable in future absorption studies (Section 4.6).

2. **Universal causal mechanism via activation patching.** We provide the first interventional evidence that competitive exclusion drives absorption across all tested hierarchy types ($d = 0.75$--$1.50$, all $p < 10^{-3}$). A tightened hedging classification (strict 0--22.6% vs. loose 92.6%) shows that the original Chanin et al. (2024) classification is near-tautological (Section 5).

3. **Cross-domain absorption characterization with confound decomposition.** We report the first absorption measurements beyond first-letter spelling, covering four hierarchy types across four layers and two SAE widths. The probe degradation ablation decomposes the observed variation, identifying city-language as a genuine hierarchy-dependent outlier ($\Delta = -21.3$ pp from the degradation curve) while showing that city-continent absorption is explained by probe quality alone (Section 4).
