# 6. Discussion

Our experiments reveal a nuanced landscape for inference-time scaling in masked diffusion language models: cross-step information transfer has substantial value, but the optimal mechanism for achieving it depends critically on the task structure, the intervention space (token vs. parameter), and the computational budget. In this section, we analyze why confidence-based remasking consistently underperforms, characterize DTA's task-dependent effectiveness, highlight DMI as a practical contribution, and distill the broader lessons from our 18-iteration research trajectory.

## 6.1 Why Remasking Fails on Reasoning Tasks

The full-scale Countdown-500 results provide definitive evidence that pure remasking strategies---ReMDM-conf (4.4%) and RCR (5.7%)---offer negligible improvement over vanilla denoising (4.7%). Our token-level diagnostics (Section 5.2) reveal the root cause: **confidence-based remasking suffers from a fundamental signal quality problem**.

ReMDM-conf achieves only 31.3% correction precision, meaning that nearly 70% of the tokens it remasks were already correct. The model's softmax confidence, while well-calibrated for distinguishing masked from unmasked positions, is poorly calibrated for distinguishing *semantically correct* tokens from *semantically incorrect* ones. In constrained reasoning tasks such as Countdown, where a single arithmetic error invalidates the entire solution, the cost of disrupting correct tokens far exceeds the benefit of occasionally correcting wrong ones.

The trajectory instability analysis further quantifies this problem. ReMDM-conf creates an average of 94.8 unstable positions per sample---approximately 37% of the generation area---where tokens change multiple times across denoising steps. This "token churning" degrades the conditioning context for subsequent predictions: when the model must predict token $y_i$ at step $t$, its context $\mathbf{y}^{(t)}_{-i}$ contains tokens that may be overwritten at step $t-1$, introducing a form of label noise into the denoising process. By contrast, both vanilla denoising and DTA produce zero unstable positions, since neither involves remasking already-revealed tokens.

The contrast with SCP is instructive. SCP achieves 76.9% correction precision---substantially higher than ReMDM-conf---by using leave-one-out probing to identify genuinely self-contradictory tokens rather than relying on confidence scores. However, this precision comes at approximately 7$\times$ the computational cost of vanilla denoising, and SCP's full-scale accuracy ($\sim$8.4%) is comparable to DMI (9.3%) rather than substantially exceeding it. This suggests that even with perfect error detection, token-space correction alone may be insufficient for reasoning tasks where errors reflect structural misunderstanding rather than local prediction mistakes.

**The deeper issue is that token-space operations cannot propagate structural understanding across denoising steps.** Remasking and re-predicting individual tokens treats the denoising process as a sequence of independent local corrections. But reasoning tasks require global coherence---an arithmetic expression must satisfy a numerical constraint across all its terms simultaneously. The Information Island problem \citep{xia2026metastate} is not merely about losing information between steps; it is about the inability of discrete token-level interventions to build and maintain a persistent "understanding" of the problem structure. This is the fundamental motivation for parameter-space adaptation: LoRA updates encode structural patterns learned from the evolving sequence into the model's weights, making them available at every subsequent prediction.

## 6.2 DTA's Task-Dependent Effectiveness

A central finding of this work is that DTA's effectiveness is strongly task-dependent (Figure 7). On MBPP code generation, DTA achieves the strongest positive signal among all methods (+12.5 percentage points over vanilla in the pilot, 37.5% vs. 25.0%). On GSM8K multi-step mathematics, DTA underperforms both vanilla and ReMDM-conf. On Countdown constrained arithmetic, the full-scale DMI results (9.3%) represent the clearest improvement, while DTA full-scale results remain pending.

We attribute this task dependence to the nature of DTA's self-supervised learning signal---the masked language modeling (MLM) loss computed on revealed tokens during the M-step.

**Why DTA excels on code generation.** Code has rich local structure: syntactic patterns, variable naming conventions, and function signatures create strong token co-occurrence regularities. When DTA masks and re-predicts revealed code tokens, the MLM loss captures these regularities and encodes them into the LoRA parameters. At subsequent denoising steps, the adapted model can leverage these learned patterns to make more contextually appropriate predictions for remaining masked positions. The pilot results support this interpretation: DTA correctly solved tasks requiring pattern recognition (symbol negation, minimum finding, tuple sorting) that vanilla failed on, while maintaining the structural integrity of the generated code (distinct-2 $\geq$ 0.978, rep-3 $\leq$ 0.007).

**Why DTA struggles on constrained arithmetic.** Countdown requires generating an arithmetic expression that evaluates to a specific target number. The MLM self-supervision signal teaches the model about token co-occurrence patterns (e.g., which numbers tend to appear near operators), but it does not teach arithmetic correctness. The loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{MLM}} = -\sum_{i \in S} \log p_{\theta + \Delta\theta}(x_i \mid \text{mask}(x_i, S))$ measures whether the model can predict a token given its context, not whether the resulting expression satisfies a numerical constraint. This is a fundamental limitation of self-supervised adaptation for tasks requiring verification against an external criterion.

**The GSM8K mixed signal.** On GSM8K (pilot), ReMDM-conf outperforms vanilla by +12.5 percentage points while DTA underperforms by $-$12.5pp. This pattern is surprising given the Countdown results where remasking is ineffective. We hypothesize that GSM8K's multi-step reasoning structure creates a different error profile: errors in intermediate reasoning steps may be more amenable to token-level correction (remasking) because they manifest as locally implausible token sequences, whereas DTA's global parameter updates may be too diffuse to target specific reasoning errors. The longer generation length (512 tokens vs. 256 for Countdown) may also amplify parameter drift, as the LoRA adapter accumulates updates over more denoising steps.

## 6.3 DMI as a Practical Contribution

Perhaps the most practically significant finding of this work is the effectiveness of Diffusion Memory Injection (DMI), the simplest method in our information augmentation spectrum. On the full-scale Countdown-500 evaluation, DMI achieves 9.3% mean accuracy across three seeds---approximately 2$\times$ the vanilla baseline (4.7%)---with near-zero computational overhead ($\sim$1.05$\times$ vanilla).

DMI's mechanism is straightforward: at each denoising step, it injects a soft embedding vector computed from the previous step's logits into the current step's input. This single modification breaks the Information Island barrier by providing the model with a continuous-valued "memory" of its previous predictions, without requiring any backward pass, parameter update, or architectural change.

The practical appeal of DMI lies in its simplicity and universality. Unlike DTA, which requires LoRA adapter initialization, gradient computation, and careful hyperparameter tuning (rank, learning rate, decay factor, warmup fraction), DMI involves a single embedding injection operation that can be implemented in fewer than 10 lines of code. Unlike SCP, which requires $O(L)$ additional forward passes for leave-one-out probing, DMI adds negligible computation. This makes DMI an attractive candidate for deployment as a default enhancement in any DLM inference pipeline.

The information augmentation spectrum ablation reveals a diminishing-returns pattern: DMI ($\sim$1.05$\times$ cost, 9.3% accuracy) achieves nearly the same performance as SCP ($\sim$7$\times$ cost, $\sim$8.4% accuracy) at a fraction of the computational budget. This suggests that the marginal value of cross-step information is highest at the lowest level of the spectrum (embedding-level memory), with progressively smaller gains from more sophisticated mechanisms. Whether this pattern holds at full scale for DTA and across additional benchmarks remains an open question.

## 6.4 Lessons from 18 Iterations of Negative Results

This paper emerges from 18 iterations of experimentation spanning six months, the majority of which produced negative or null results. We distill four methodological lessons that we believe are broadly applicable to inference-time scaling research.

**Lesson 1: Perplexity is unreliable as a quality metric for DLMs.** Our Phase 1 experiments demonstrated that ReMask-Retry on a 0.6B model can reduce GPT-2 perplexity by 47.5% while producing degenerate text on 71% of prompts. At the 7B scale, PPL variance is so large that genuine 5% improvements are undetectable without $N > 200$ samples. We recommend that DLM research adopt task-specific accuracy metrics (Countdown, GSM8K, MBPP) as primary evaluation criteria, with perplexity reported only as a secondary reference jointly with diversity metrics.

**Lesson 2: Pilot results systematically overestimate effect sizes.** Across all methods and benchmarks, 16-sample pilot results exhibited a mean effect size inflation of approximately 24 percentage points relative to full-scale evaluation. The most dramatic example: DMI showed 0% accuracy in the pilot (0/16 correct) but achieved 9.3% at full scale (47/500 mean across seeds). Conversely, ReMDM-conf showed 25% in one pilot but only 4.4% at full scale. The stochastic nature of DLM generation, combined with small sample sizes, creates high variance that can produce misleadingly positive or negative signals. We advocate for a minimum of 200 samples across 3 random seeds for any reported result, with pilot-scale experiments ($N < 50$) explicitly labeled as hypothesis-generating.

**Lesson 3: DLM inference-time scaling faces challenges distinct from autoregressive models.** The success of Best-of-N, self-refinement, and chain-of-thought in AR models does not transfer to DLMs. AR models benefit from sequential coherence checking and well-calibrated sequence-level likelihoods; DLM models operate in a parallel, bidirectional regime where token probabilities are entangled across positions and denoising steps. Methods that work by "asking the model to evaluate its own output" (remasking, trajectory consistency) fail because the model lacks the self-evaluative signal that AR's autoregressive structure provides for free. Successful DLM scaling methods must either introduce external information (verifiers, reward models) or modify the generation process itself (temperature scheduling, parameter adaptation).

**Lesson 4: Implementation bugs can produce effects comparable to genuine method improvements.** Our temperature annealing experiments initially showed a $-$16.5% PPL improvement, later revealed to be an artifact of a bug that made the annealing schedule ineffective (passing `temperature=0.0`, which triggers argmax decoding regardless of the annealing hook). After correction, the true effect was $-$11% to $-$16% median PPL improvement. This episode---where a bug produced a "positive result" of comparable magnitude to the corrected method's genuine effect---underscores the importance of implementation verification (checking that different hyperparameter settings produce distinct outputs) as a standard part of experimental protocols.

## 6.5 Limitations

Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged:

1. **Incomplete full-scale evaluation.** At the time of writing, full-scale results for DTA and DTA+ReMDM on Countdown-500 remain pending. The full-scale DMI and SCP results establish the value of cross-step information transfer, but the question of whether parameter-space adaptation (DTA) provides additional benefit beyond embedding-level memory (DMI) on Countdown cannot yet be answered definitively. We report pilot-scale DTA results ($N = 16$) with explicit caveats about their reliability.

2. **Pilot-scale cross-benchmark and cross-model results.** The GSM8K, MBPP, and LLaDA-8B results are based on 16-sample pilots. Given the documented pilot-to-full-scale discrepancies (Lesson 2), these results should be interpreted as directional signals rather than definitive conclusions. DTA's strong MBPP pilot signal (+12.5pp) and weak GSM8K signal ($-$12.5pp) require full-scale validation to confirm.

3. **Self-supervision signal quality.** DTA's M-step uses masked language modeling loss on revealed tokens---a proxy for generation quality that does not directly measure task correctness. For tasks with verifiable constraints (arithmetic, code execution), this misalignment between the self-supervised objective and the evaluation metric is a fundamental limitation. Structured self-supervision losses (e.g., contrastive objectives, consistency verification) could address this gap but are beyond the scope of the current work.

4. **Computational overhead.** DTA incurs approximately 4--5$\times$ the computational cost of vanilla denoising due to the backward pass required for LoRA updates. While this is modest in absolute terms ($\sim$15s vs. $\sim$4s per Countdown sample on an RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell), it makes DTA impractical for latency-sensitive applications. DMI's near-zero overhead makes it the more deployment-friendly option when absolute accuracy gains are comparable.

5. **Hyperparameter sensitivity.** DTA introduces several hyperparameters (LoRA rank $r$, learning rate $\eta$, decay factor $\gamma$, warmup fraction, number of LoRA layers) that require tuning. While our ablation studies (Section 5.3) show that the default configuration ($r = 4$, $\gamma = 0.95$) is numerically stable across all tested values, the accuracy impact of these hyperparameters could not be resolved at pilot scale. The optimal configuration may be task-dependent, adding complexity to deployment.

6. **Theoretical assumptions.** The VDTA framework's ELBO monotonicity guarantee (Proposition 1) relies on the assumption of strong convexity of the MLM loss with respect to $\Delta\theta$, which may not hold exactly in practice with non-convex neural network loss surfaces. The theoretical analysis provides a useful conceptual framework and correctly predicts the monotonic information accumulation observed empirically (Section 5.4), but should be understood as an idealization rather than a strict guarantee.

## 6.6 Future Directions

Our results suggest several promising avenues for future work:

**Structured self-supervision.** The central limitation of DTA's M-step is that the MLM loss teaches token co-occurrence rather than task correctness. Replacing or augmenting the MLM loss with structured objectives---contrastive losses that distinguish correct from incorrect partial solutions, verification-based losses that check constraint satisfaction, or consistency losses that penalize self-contradicting predictions---could substantially improve DTA's effectiveness on reasoning tasks. The SCP framework provides a natural starting point: its leave-one-out probing identifies self-contradictions with 76.9% precision, and incorporating this signal into the DTA gradient could yield a more task-aware adaptation mechanism.

**DMI as a universal DLM enhancement.** DMI's near-zero-cost embedding injection provides $\sim$2$\times$ improvement on Countdown with no hyperparameter tuning. Validating this benefit across a wider range of models (LLaDA, Dream variants, future DLMs) and tasks (summarization, translation, dialogue) would establish whether DMI represents a general-purpose improvement to the DLM inference pipeline. If confirmed, DMI could be incorporated as a default inference-time option in DLM frameworks, analogous to nucleus sampling in autoregressive models.

**Hybrid DTA + external verifier approaches.** DTA operates in parameter space; remasking operates in token space; external verifiers provide task-specific correctness signals. These three axes of intervention are largely orthogonal. A system that uses an external verifier (e.g., a code executor for MBPP, a calculator for arithmetic) to provide reward signals for DTA's M-step---replacing the MLM loss with a task-specific objective---could combine the best aspects of all three approaches. Recent work on PG-DLM \citep{zhang2025pgdlm} and DiFFPO \citep{chen2026diffpo} demonstrates the potential of reward-guided DLM inference, but these methods require pre-trained reward models; DTA's training-free LoRA framework could serve as a lightweight alternative.

**Theoretical analysis of token-space vs. parameter-space interventions.** Our empirical results reveal that token-space interventions (remasking) and parameter-space interventions (DTA) have complementary strengths and weaknesses that vary by task. A formal analysis of when each type of intervention is optimal---for example, characterizing the conditions under which parameter updates can propagate information that token-level corrections cannot---would provide principled guidance for method selection. The information augmentation spectrum offers a starting point for such an analysis, with its four levels providing a controlled framework for measuring the marginal value of increasing intervention expressivity.
