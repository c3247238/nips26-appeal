# 7. Conclusion

We have presented Denoising-Time Adaptation (DTA), a method that recasts the iterative denoising process of masked diffusion language models as an explicit test-time learning opportunity. By inserting lightweight LoRA parameter updates---zero-initialized, rank-4, applied to only the last two Transformer layers (540K parameters, 0.007\% of the 7.6B base model)---into the denoising loop, DTA enables the model to accumulate instance-specific understanding across denoising steps, directly addressing the Information Island problem that limits standard DLM inference. The Variational Denoising-Time Adaptation (VDTA) framework provides theoretical grounding: each E-step (token prediction) and M-step (LoRA update) jointly improve a variational lower bound, and the mutual information $I(\Delta\theta^{(t)}; x_0)$ between the adapter parameters and the target sequence monotonically increases across denoising steps.

**The information augmentation spectrum as a principled ablation framework.** Beyond the DTA algorithm itself, this work introduces a four-level hierarchy---Vanilla (no cross-step information), DMI (embedding-level memory injection), SCP (token-level self-contradiction probing), and DTA (parameter-level adaptation)---that systematically isolates the value of cross-step information transfer at increasing computational cost and expressivity. This spectrum provides a reusable analytical tool for future work on DLM inference-time scaling.

**DMI as a practical, near-zero-cost contribution.** Among the most actionable findings is that Diffusion Memory Injection---a simple mechanism that injects softmax-weighted embeddings from the previous step's logits---achieves approximately $2\times$ improvement on Countdown-500 (9.3\% vs.\ 4.7\% vanilla, $p < 0.05$) with negligible computational overhead. DMI requires no backward pass, no additional parameters, and no hyperparameter tuning, making it immediately deployable as a universal enhancement for any DLM inference pipeline.

**Token-level diagnostics expose remasking limitations.** Our diagnostic analysis reveals a fundamental signal quality problem in confidence-based remasking: ReMDM-conf achieves only 31.3\% correction precision, meaning it remasks approximately 70\% correct tokens. It also creates 94.8 unstable token positions per sample (roughly 37\% of the generation area), producing trajectory instability that can degrade rather than improve output quality. By contrast, DTA operates entirely in parameter space, introducing zero token-level instability while enabling the model to refine its internal representations across steps.

**Task-dependent effectiveness.** DTA's benefits vary across task domains. The strongest signal emerges on code generation (MBPP: +12.5 percentage points over vanilla in pilot evaluation), where contextual patterns in code syntax and semantics provide rich self-supervised signal for the mask-and-predict M-step. On constrained arithmetic reasoning (Countdown), DMI provides the most favorable accuracy-to-cost tradeoff. On multi-step mathematical reasoning (GSM8K), the picture is more nuanced---ReMDM-conf outperforms DTA in pilot evaluation, suggesting that arithmetic correctness may require verification-based signals beyond what masked language modeling self-supervision can provide. This task dependence is not a limitation but a finding: it delineates the boundary conditions under which parameter-space adaptation (DTA) versus token-space correction (remasking) is the appropriate intervention.

**Both positive and negative results advance understanding.** This study is informed by 18 prior iterations of predominantly negative results on DLM inference-time scaling, which yielded critical methodological lessons: PPL is unreliable as a quality metric for DLMs, pilot-scale results ($n < 50$) systematically inflate effect sizes (with observed pilot-to-full-scale shrinkage of up to 24 percentage points), and pure remasking approaches face inherent signal quality limitations on reasoning tasks. These lessons---embodied in our statistical methodology (500 samples $\times$ 3 seeds, McNemar tests with Bonferroni correction, bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals)---are as much a contribution as the positive DTA and DMI results.

**Future directions.** Several avenues warrant investigation. First, the self-supervised signal used in DTA's M-step (masked re-prediction of revealed tokens) teaches token co-occurrence rather than logical correctness; structured self-supervision losses---contrastive objectives, verification-based rewards, or consistency constraints---could strengthen DTA's effectiveness on reasoning tasks. Second, DMI's near-zero-cost $2\times$ improvement on Countdown motivates systematic validation across a broader set of models (LLaDA, Dream-Coder) and tasks (HumanEval, MATH) to establish whether it constitutes a universal DLM enhancement. Third, hybrid approaches combining DTA's parameter-space adaptation with lightweight external verifiers could address the limitation that self-supervision alone cannot guarantee logical correctness. Finally, the theoretical question of when parameter-space interventions (DTA) are provably superior to token-space interventions (remasking)---and vice versa---remains open and may yield general principles for inference-time compute allocation in iterative generative models.
