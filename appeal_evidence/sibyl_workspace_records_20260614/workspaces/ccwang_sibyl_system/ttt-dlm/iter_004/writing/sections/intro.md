# 1. Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) have traditionally relied on autoregressive (AR) generation, producing tokens sequentially from left to right. Masked diffusion language models (MDLMs) offer a compelling alternative: they generate text through iterative denoising of a fully masked sequence, enabling parallel token prediction, arbitrary generation order, and inherent self-correction capabilities (Sahoo et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024). Recent scaling efforts — LLaDA at 8B parameters (Nie et al., 2025) and Dream at 7B (Liu et al., 2025) — have demonstrated that MDLMs can approach and sometimes surpass autoregressive models of comparable scale on reasoning benchmarks, while Google's Gemini Diffusion has validated the paradigm at industrial scale.

A central question for the MDLM paradigm is whether inference-time compute scaling — the strategy of investing additional computation during generation to improve output quality — can yield systematic gains on reasoning tasks. For autoregressive models, inference-time scaling through techniques such as chain-of-thought prompting, best-of-N sampling, and tree search has produced dramatic improvements (Zhang et al., 2025; Ji et al., 2025). MDLMs present a natural opportunity: their iterative denoising process offers multiple intervention points where additional computation could refine predictions. Yet a growing body of evidence suggests that naive approaches to MDLM inference-time scaling fail on reasoning benchmarks, and the reasons for these failures have remained poorly understood.

We identify a fundamental limitation of standard MDLM denoising that we term the **information island problem**: at each denoising step, the model performs an independent forward pass on the current partially masked sequence and produces rich distributional predictions — full logit vectors and attention distributions — over all positions. However, once tokens are selected via argmax and unmasked, this distributional information is entirely discarded. The next step operates on fresh mask embeddings for remaining positions, with no memory of the previous step's continuous predictions. Each denoising step is thus an "information island," isolated from the distributional knowledge accumulated by prior steps. This problem has been independently identified by Xia et al. (2026), who address it through a trained GRU memory module (MetaState). We pursue a training-free alternative.

The information island problem explains why several existing inference-time scaling approaches have systematically failed on reasoning benchmarks. Pure remasking methods — which re-mask previously revealed tokens and re-predict them — do not accumulate cross-step information; on our primary benchmark (Countdown-500 with Dream-7B), ReMDM-conf achieves only 4.4% accuracy and RCR 5.7%, compared to vanilla denoising at 4.7% (Arriaga et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2025). Parameter-space adaptation via test-time training faces a different barrier: the masked language modeling loss in well-trained MDLMs is already extremely low (0.005--0.032), leaving insufficient gradient signal for meaningful online adaptation. Our experiments with Denoising-Time Adaptation (DTA) using LoRA confirm this: DTA achieves 4.8% on Countdown-500, statistically indistinguishable from vanilla. Best-of-N sampling incurs perplexity degradation without sharing information across independent trajectories.

A critical insight emerged from our prior iteration: **Diffusion Memory Injection (DMI)**, a simple technique that injects the previous step's logit-weighted embedding mixture into mask positions, yields a substantial improvement — 9.3% accuracy on Countdown-500 versus 4.7% for vanilla denoising (3-seed validated, $p < 0.05$). This near-2$\times$ improvement comes with negligible computational overhead ($\sim$1.05$\times$ FLOPs) and demonstrates that the bottleneck lies specifically in the **representation poverty of mask embeddings**, not in the denoising schedule or the model's capacity. The success of DMI, combined with independent results from related work on continuous token representations — LRD achieving GSM8K +2.9 and MATH500 +3.8 (Zhu et al., 2025), ReMix demonstrating 2--8$\times$ speedup without quality loss (Ye et al., 2026), and EvoToken-DLM's gradual token evolution (arXiv 2601.07351) — points to a unified insight: replacing hard mask embeddings with semantically rich continuous representations is the most promising direction for MDLM inference-time improvement.

Building on this insight, we propose two orthogonal, training-free inference-time scaling methods that address the information island problem at different levels of the denoising pipeline:

1. **Belief-State Diffusion (BSD)** operates at the *representation layer*. It replaces mask embeddings entirely with "belief states" — probability-weighted embedding mixtures derived from model predictions — that evolve continuously via exponential moving average (EMA) accumulation across denoising steps. Unlike DMI's fixed-ratio mixing, BSD maintains full distributional representations throughout a dedicated belief-refinement phase, committing to hard tokens only in the final steps. BSD generalizes DMI: DMI is the special case where the belief phase is empty and the mixing ratio is fixed.

2. **Adaptive Classifier-Free Guidance (A-CFG)** operates at the *prediction layer*. Inspired by the remarkable success of A-CFG on LLaDA-8B — where it boosted GSM8K accuracy to 73.5, surpassing LLaMA3-8B's 53.1 (arXiv 2505.20199) — we apply confidence-based re-masking and dual forward passes to Dream-7B. A-CFG identifies the least-confident token positions, temporarily re-masks them to construct an unconditional input, and amplifies the difference between conditional and unconditional predictions to enhance reasoning signals.

We evaluate both methods on Dream-v0-Instruct-7B across Countdown (structured arithmetic reasoning) and GSM8K (free-form mathematical reasoning). On the Countdown-16 pilot, A-CFG with guidance weight $w = 1.5$ achieves 12.5% accuracy — matching DMI and substantially outperforming vanilla (0--6.2%). More notably, A-CFG is the **only method demonstrating cross-benchmark generalization**: on GSM8K-16, it achieves 37.5% versus vanilla's 25.0% (+12.5 percentage points), while DMI shows no benefit (25.0%) and BSD slightly degrades performance (18.8%). BSD provides complementary information-theoretic validation: belief entropy decreases near-monotonically during denoising (Spearman $\rho = -0.95$, 15/16 samples monotonic), terminal belief entropy is lower than vanilla (0.001 vs. 0.002), and entropy convergence quality predicts task correctness ($r = 0.78$, $p < 0.001$).

Equally important are our extensive **negative results**, which map the design space of MDLM inference-time scaling and provide actionable constraints for future work:

- **Cross-step JSD stability fails as a re-masking signal**: Dream-7B produces remarkably stable cross-step distributions (JSD stability $\sim$0.997), rendering stability-based methods uninformative. Our RACFG variant using JSD-guided re-masking achieves 0% accuracy versus A-CFG's 12.5%.
- **CFG temporal scheduling fails for masked diffusion**: Despite theoretical predictions from continuous diffusion suggesting that early guidance is harmful (Rojas et al., 2025), all four temporal scheduling variants (linear, cosine, threshold) yield 0% accuracy on Countdown, while fixed guidance weight dominates. Masked diffusion's discrete dynamics create a fundamentally different information geometry.
- **BSD and A-CFG do not compose**: Their combination achieves only 6.2% on Countdown — below either method individually — falsifying the hypothesis that representation-layer and prediction-layer improvements are synergistic. BSD's smooth beliefs optimized for refinement conflict with A-CFG's need for sharp, discriminable distributions.
- **Compute-fair analysis reveals a narrow advantage**: When controlling for FLOPs, vanilla denoising with proportionally more steps is competitive at most compute budgets. The methods' value lies in information quality at the low-compute frontier, not in unbounded scaling behavior.

These findings collectively advance our understanding of what does and does not work for inference-time reasoning in MDLMs. We contribute: (1) BSD, the first training-free method to fully replace mask embeddings with continuously evolving distributional representations, with information-theoretic validation of belief convergence; (2) the first application of A-CFG to Dream-7B, establishing classifier-free guidance as the most promising general tool for MDLM reasoning enhancement; (3) DMI as a practical near-zero-cost method providing $\sim$2$\times$ improvement on Countdown-500; and (4) a systematic mapping of failure modes — DTA, JSD-based re-masking, temporal scheduling, and method combination — that constrains the design space for future MDLM inference-time methods.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 surveys related work on masked diffusion models, inference-time scaling, continuous token representations, and classifier-free guidance. Section 3 presents the BSD and A-CFG methods with information-theoretic analysis. Section 4 reports experimental results including main comparisons, ablation studies, and cross-benchmark generalization. Section 5 discusses failure modes and their implications. Section 6 concludes with limitations and future directions.
