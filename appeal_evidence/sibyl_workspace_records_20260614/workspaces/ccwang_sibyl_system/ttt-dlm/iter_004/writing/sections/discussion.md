# 5. Discussion

The experimental results paint a nuanced picture: BSD and A-CFG each yield meaningful improvements over vanilla Dream-7B denoising, yet several of our pre-registered hypotheses were decisively falsified. We analyze five key findings that constrain the design space for future MDLM inference-time methods.

## 5.1 Why BSD + A-CFG Combination Fails

Perhaps our most surprising negative result is that combining BSD and A-CFG yields *lower* accuracy (6.2%) than either method alone (BSD 6.2--12.5%, A-CFG 12.5%). This falsifies H7, which predicted synergistic gains from orthogonal layer optimization.

The root cause lies in a fundamental tension between the representational properties each method requires. During Phase 1, BSD evolves belief vectors via EMA accumulation, producing *smooth*, high-entropy distributional representations designed for gradual refinement. The resulting belief states encode uncertainty uniformly across the vocabulary, which is precisely the property that enables monotonic entropy decrease ($\rho = -0.95$). However, A-CFG's re-masking mechanism in Phase 2 relies on *sharp*, discriminable confidence scores to identify uncertain positions. When A-CFG operates on BSD's belief-conditioned predictions rather than standard mask-conditioned predictions, the confidence landscape is fundamentally altered: positions that BSD has partially resolved present a different uncertainty profile than positions emerging from fresh mask embeddings.

More concretely, BSD's Phase 1 produces predictions that are already informed by accumulated cross-step information. When Phase 2 applies A-CFG, the confidence-based re-masking selects positions based on beliefs that have been smoothed by EMA---these are not the same "uncertain" positions that single-step confidence would identify from scratch. The guidance signal $\ell^+ - \ell^-$ is therefore computed over a mismatched distribution, producing interference rather than amplification. This failure mode suggests a general principle: **methods that modify the representation space and methods that modify the prediction space may not compose freely**, even when they operate at nominally orthogonal layers.

## 5.2 Why JSD Stability Fails as a Re-masking Signal

Our proposed RACFG method, which selects re-masking positions via cross-step JSD instability, achieved 0.0% accuracy across all configurations---decisively falsifying H5 (JSD $\geq$ confidence + 2pp). The root cause is architectural rather than algorithmic: Dream-7B produces remarkably stable cross-step probability distributions, with JSD stability scores clustered at $\sim$0.997 across all positions (Figure 6a). This near-degenerate distribution eliminates any meaningful "hesitation signal" that RACFG relies on to identify reasoning-critical tokens.

This finding has broader implications. High cross-step prediction consistency is arguably a *desirable* property of a well-trained denoising model---it indicates that the model's predictions are robust to the specific mask configuration at each step. However, this very robustness makes stability-based position selection generically inapplicable to well-trained MDLMs. In contrast, single-step confidence directly measures current uncertainty without requiring inter-step variation, explaining why A-CFG's simpler signal (12.5%) dramatically outperforms RACFG's theoretically motivated alternative (0.0%).

This result also challenges the broader assumption that cross-step dynamics in MDLMs provide useful signals for inference-time interventions. Unlike autoregressive models where token-level uncertainty accumulates sequentially, MDLMs resolve uncertainty in parallel across all positions, and well-trained models do so consistently. Future work on MDLM inference-time scaling should therefore focus on *within-step* signals (confidence, entropy, attention patterns) rather than *across-step* signals (stability, trajectory divergence).

## 5.3 Why CFG Temporal Scheduling Fails for Masked Diffusion

The theoretical prediction from continuous diffusion CFG scheduling (Rojas et al., 2025) suggests that guidance should be suppressed early (high noise/mask rate) and maximized late (low noise/mask rate). Applied to A-CFG on Dream-7B, all four temporal schedules (linear, cosine, threshold at 70/30 mask rate, and fixed) were tested. Fixed guidance ($w = 1.5$) achieved 12.5%, while *every* scheduled variant achieved 0.0%---falsifying H6.

The theory-practice gap can be attributed to a fundamental mismatch in information geometry. Continuous diffusion operates over smooth noise-to-signal transitions where the signal-to-noise ratio varies continuously; the theoretical analysis assumes this smoothness to derive optimal scheduling. Masked diffusion's discrete mask/unmask dynamics create a qualitatively different landscape: at each step, a position is either fully masked (no information) or fully revealed (complete information), with no intermediate states. This binary structure means that the model has access to *sufficient context for guidance* at any step where some tokens are revealed, unlike the gradual context emergence in continuous diffusion.

Additionally, scheduled variants that suppress early guidance (e.g., threshold at 70% mask rate) sacrifice exactly the steps where A-CFG has the most positions available for re-masking---and therefore the greatest opportunity to construct an informative unconditional input. By the time guidance is allowed to activate under these schedules, most positions are already committed, leaving too few re-maskable positions for effective CFG. This suggests that **CFG scheduling theory developed for continuous diffusion does not transfer to discrete masked diffusion**, and that constant guidance is the appropriate default for MDLMs.

## 5.4 The Representation Bottleneck: Real but Narrow

Both BSD and A-CFG provide evidence that the information island problem---the discarding of distributional predictions between denoising steps---is a genuine bottleneck in MDLM reasoning performance. BSD's information-theoretic validation is particularly compelling: belief entropy decreases near-monotonically during denoising ($\rho = -0.95$, 15/16 samples monotonic), terminal entropy is lower than vanilla (0.001 vs. 0.002), and entropy convergence quality strongly predicts task correctness ($r = 0.78$, $p < 0.001$). These results confirm that continuous belief evolution genuinely accumulates information across denoising steps, validating the core thesis of representation-level improvement.

However, compute-fair comparisons temper this conclusion. When vanilla Dream-7B is given equivalent FLOPs (e.g., 256 steps for $\sim$2.0x compute), it achieves 12.5%---matching A-CFG's accuracy at the same compute budget. BSD at $\sim$1.1x FLOPs (6.2%) is outperformed by vanilla at 1.1x steps (12.5%). This means that the methods' value lies primarily at the *low-compute frontier*: they extract more information per forward pass than vanilla, but simple step-scaling is a competitive alternative when additional compute is available.

This compute-fairness result does not invalidate the methods---in practice, the low-compute regime is often the operationally relevant one, and methods that improve per-step information quality may compose better with future architectural advances. DMI, in particular, remains the most efficient method discovered: its near-zero overhead ($\sim$1.05x FLOPs) delivers a validated $\sim$2x improvement on Countdown-500 (9.3% vs. 4.7%, 3-seed validated), making it immediately deployable in any MDLM pipeline without meaningful cost.

## 5.5 Negative Results as Design Space Constraints

Across four iterations of experimentation, this work has systematically mapped what does and does not work for MDLM inference-time scaling. We summarize these constraints in Table 3.

The pattern that emerges is that *parameter-space* interventions (TTT, DTA) fail because gradient signals are too weak in the already-converged MDLM loss landscape (MLM loss at 0.005--0.032). *Structural* interventions that modify the denoising schedule (ReMDM, RCR, temporal scheduling) fail because they do not address the underlying representation quality. *Cross-step signal* methods (RACFG/JSD) fail because well-trained MDLMs are too stable across steps. The methods that succeed---DMI, BSD, and A-CFG---all operate at the *within-step representation or prediction level*, modifying the information content of individual denoising steps rather than the schedule or parameters.

A-CFG stands out as the most promising general tool: it is the only method demonstrating cross-benchmark generalization, with consistent +12.5pp improvements on both Countdown-16 and GSM8K-16. This generalization, combined with its conceptual simplicity (confidence-based re-masking + fixed guidance weight), makes it the strongest candidate for broader adoption. The failure of our proposed enhancements (cross-step stability, temporal scheduling) over the baseline A-CFG method is itself an informative result: it suggests that A-CFG's effectiveness derives from fundamental properties of CFG in discrete diffusion, not from the specific position selection heuristic, and that these properties are robust to---and potentially degraded by---additional complexity.

### Limitations

Several important limitations constrain the conclusions drawn from this work. First, the pilot-scale evaluation (n=16) for BSD, A-CFG, and their ablations yields wide confidence intervals (Bootstrap 95% CIs of $\pm$12--31pp) and insufficient statistical power---no McNemar comparison reached significance. Full-scale 3-seed validation on Countdown-500 is required to confirm the pilot trends. Second, the Countdown-500 results with 3-seed validation are available only for DMI and remasking baselines; BSD and A-CFG results at this scale remain pending. Third, GSM8K evaluation used only 16 samples, and the +12.5pp improvement for A-CFG, while encouraging, has a 95% CI of [$-$12.5%, +37.5%] that includes zero. Fourth, our experiments are restricted to Dream-7B; the failure of JSD stability may not generalize to other MDLMs (e.g., LLaDA-8B, where A-CFG has demonstrated stronger results). Fifth, the compute-fair analysis suggests that methods may not dominate vanilla step-scaling at all compute budgets, and the Pareto frontier analysis should be extended to larger-scale evaluations.
