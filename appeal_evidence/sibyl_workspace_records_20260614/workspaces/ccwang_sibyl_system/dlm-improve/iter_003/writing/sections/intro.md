# Introduction

Training-free revision in diffusion language models (DLMs) regularly produces small empirical gains that are tempting to narrate as genuine controller progress. The scientific risk is not merely that such gains may disappear under replication. It is that a weak control structure can make a narrow gain look far more methodologically meaningful than the evidence deserves. In the current workspace, the apparent success case was an entropy-guided revision arm, `CARD-84`, that improved GSM8K accuracy over a compute-matched active control, `DNB-84`. That contrast alone would have supported a familiar positive story: a localized observer signal can be converted into better revision behavior without retraining. The stronger audit in this iteration shows why that story should not be the paper.

Our paper instead reports an audited negative case. The central observation is straightforward. On the 50 audited GSM8K examples, `CARD-84` reaches 0.32 accuracy while `DNB-84` reaches 0.18, yielding a net repaired margin of +7. However, the same audited intervention reaches only a +1 net repaired margin against the budget-matched sham control `RAND-84`, which reaches 0.30 accuracy on the same slice. Once the sham control is introduced, the positive controller interpretation no longer survives. The remaining evidence is still informative, but it supports a narrower conclusion: the entropy signal is, at most, a risk marker for where revision may matter, not a validated targeting rule.

This reframing matters because DLM inference papers are increasingly judged in regimes where gains are small, compute is flexible, and implementation details can move the headline. In such settings, a compute-matched baseline is necessary but not sufficient. It rules out the trivial claim that more denoising budget alone produced the gain, yet it does not rule out the possibility that a generic or weakly structured perturbation would have done nearly as well. The stronger negative control changes the interpretation of the same empirical slice from "controller works" to "signal exists but cannot be upgraded into efficacy."

The contribution of this paper is therefore interpretive rather than algorithmic. We do not introduce a new DLM revision family, and we do not argue that `CARD-84` is a winning inference method. Instead, we document a minimal audit template that kept a small-gain story from being overstated. The template combines four ingredients: a compute-matched active control, a budget-matched sham control, sample-level repair and harm accounting, and current-only artifact closure. Together these ingredients let us say exactly what the present evidence supports and, equally importantly, what it does not.

Concretely, the current evidence supports three claims. First, there is a localized reasoning-side signal against `DNB-84` on the audited GSM8K slice. Second, that signal does not cleanly separate from budget-matched random targeting. Third, MBPP should be read as a harm-profile boundary rather than as mechanism validation, because `CARD-84` and `RAND-84` both remain at 0.04 accuracy on the audited code slice even though their failure distributions differ. These claims are modest, but they are more credible than the positive method narrative they replace.

The rest of the paper proceeds accordingly. Section 2 formalizes the audited slice and the minimal audit template. Section 3 situates that template against recent DLM inference, uncertainty, and evaluation work. Section 4 reports the audited results, emphasizing how the stronger control rewrites the GSM8K story and how MBPP constrains any mechanism-forward interpretation. Section 5 states the claim ceiling and the implications for future DLM revision studies. Section 6 concludes with the durable lesson: in a small-gain regime, compute-matched controls alone are not enough when a budget-matched sham control can still rewrite the story.
