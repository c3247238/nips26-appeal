# Independent Review: A Strong Diagnostic Thesis Still Searching for Its Final Paper Form

**Review time**: 2026-03-12  
**Model**: Codex (GPT-5)

## Review

This submission is most compelling when read not as another diffusion-LM decoding paper, but as a paper about research hygiene in diffusion-LM revision. Its real contribution is not that one more controller works on one benchmark. Its real contribution is that nominal step counts, uncertainty signals, and intervention policies should not be collapsed into one story. In that sense, the paper has a serious and worthwhile thesis.

The manuscript is already stronger than many empirical papers in one important way: it is trying to tell the truth about its own limits. The central evidence is coherent. On GSM8K, the shortlist does show a meaningful compute-accounting story: `CORE-proxy-64` is best on raw accuracy (`0.46`), but it is also dramatically slower and less deployment-friendly than the revision baselines, while `Entropy-Revise-64+3` and `TIGER-Instability-64+3` both land at `0.39` under much lower latency. The signal audit also supports the paper’s main philosophical claim: calibration has the strongest diagnostic score (`0.6225`), entropy is practically useful (`0.4140`), and instability is weak as an observer (`0.0555`) despite being tied to a more intervention-heavy story. The boundary evidence is also interesting rather than merely negative: on HumanEval, gated TIGER lowers syntax failure from `0.48` to `0.28`, but runtime failure worsens to `0.68` and `pass@1` remains below Standard (`0.04` vs. `0.06`). That is a real result, and it is more intellectually useful than a shallow “method failed on code” summary.

My main concern is that the paper’s identity is still not fully resolved. The proposal clearly aims for a compute-normalized diagnostic benchmark with a revision phase diagram, benefit buckets, and a failure taxonomy. The current paper, however, still looks structurally like a compact results paper with four tables and a modest discussion. That mismatch matters. If the paper wants to be a diagnostic paper, then the decisive payload should be diagnostic objects: case buckets, failure transitions, intervention benefit regimes, and explicit examples of conclusion reversals caused by honest compute. Right now, the paper states that those are the high-value next steps rather than actually delivering them. As a result, the manuscript undersells its own best idea and oversells its current completeness.

A second concern is that the observer-controller audit is conceptually promising but not yet methodologically clean enough. The paper presents calibration, entropy, and instability on one common “diagnostic vs. control” table, but the underlying quantities are heterogeneous. Calibration uses a held-out absolute entropy-error correlation; entropy and instability use screen-level correlations; control effectiveness is defined through deltas against random revision or the lack of a direct control policy. I understand the intuition, but a reviewer may reasonably ask whether these numbers are commensurate enough to justify the strong “gap” framing. At the moment, the argument is suggestive and insightful, but it still feels partly like an internal synthesis layer built on top of several experiments rather than a single crisp experimental object.

A third concern is evidence-chain clarity. The proposal, the paper, and the results directory all broadly agree on the narrative direction, but they still carry traces of pipeline logic rather than submission-ready scientific framing. For example, the manuscript uses MATH500 as transfer-boundary evidence, while the results directory also contains a separate `math500_transfer` artifact marked as skipped because TIGER did not justify scale-out. I can infer that the paper is relying on the diagnostic shortlist artifact rather than the canceled method-scaling task, but this kind of artifact lineage should never be left for a reviewer to reconstruct. The same issue appears in the signal story: the paper’s framing is defensible, but the provenance and normalization of the scores need to be made explicit enough that the reader never wonders whether the paper is comparing quantities that were engineered post hoc for synthesis.

My fourth concern is that the honest-compute claim, while real, is not yet maximally persuasive. The paper correctly says that honest compute changes “key comparisons” rather than proving a universal ranking rewrite, and I appreciate that restraint. But for the paper to become memorable, it needs one level more specificity: where exactly would a nominal-step reading lead the community to the wrong scientific conclusion? One pairwise reorder is a start; a sharper conclusion would be something like “method X appears preferable under nominal NFE, but becomes clearly inferior once latency, batchability, and compileability are included.” The current evidence is close to that standard, but it does not yet crystallize into a field-shifting benchmark lesson.

What I like most, and what I think should be leaned into much harder, is that this paper has the seeds of a norms paper for DLM inference research. The strongest line here is not “we found the best revision rule.” It is “the field is currently mixing observer quality, controller quality, and compute accounting in ways that make comparisons scientifically sloppy.” That is a publishable idea if the paper is brave enough to become fully about that idea. In other words, the right revision is probably not to add more algorithmic decoration. The right revision is to make the paper more diagnostic, more forensic, and more explicit about what kinds of claims it is refusing to make.

My recommendation is therefore a borderline weak reject for a top-tier conference in its current form, but with unusually high upside. I do not think the paper is failing because the underlying idea is weak. I think it is failing because the manuscript has not yet fully become the paper it is already trying to be. If the authors tighten the evidence chain, turn the missing “benefit buckets / phase diagram / asset-lineage” pieces into actual deliverables, and reframe the contribution as a protocol-and-failure-analysis paper rather than a lightly empirical results paper, the work would become much sharper and more distinctive.

## Score

**5/10**

Borderline weak reject. The paper contains a genuinely valuable independent thesis about honest compute and observer-controller mismatch, and the negative code evidence is intellectually useful rather than merely unfortunate. But the manuscript still feels one revision away from its real form: the framing is stronger than the delivered diagnostic payload, the signal-gap construction needs cleaner methodological grounding, and the paper’s most distinctive promised artifacts are not yet actually in the paper.
