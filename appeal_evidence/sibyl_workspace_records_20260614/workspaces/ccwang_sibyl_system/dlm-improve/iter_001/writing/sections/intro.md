# 1. Introduction

Training-free inference has become the most active improvement path for diffusion language models (DLMs). Recent systems such as LLaDA and Dream show that masked diffusion can scale to competitive open-weight language models, but their decoding cost remains high and their behavior under intervention remains poorly understood. In response, a growing family of inference-time methods has emerged: some try to stop or skip computation, some choose which positions to revise, and some probe trajectories for fragile tokens. Yet the literature still mixes together three different questions: how much compute a method really uses, whether a signal is a good observer of error, and whether that same signal becomes a good controller when turned into an intervention policy.

This paper argues that those questions should be separated. Our central claim is not that we have found another winning revision policy. Instead, across a focused six-method shortlist and three benchmark roles, we show that a credible study of revision in DLMs must simultaneously report honest compute, diagnose observer-controller mismatch, and test whether revision response transfers across task structure. We focus on a shortlist of representative training-free methods: `Standard-64`, `DNB-84`, `Prophet-64`, `CORE-proxy-64`, `Entropy-Revise-64+3`, and `TIGER-Instability-64+3`. These methods cover pure denoising, compute-matched standard denoising, lightweight stopping, probe-based revision, entropy-guided revision, and instability-guided revision. Studying them together reveals a problem with many headline comparisons in the current literature: nominal method labels obscure runtime asymmetries. On GSM8K, for example, `CORE-proxy-64` achieves the best raw accuracy (`0.46`) but requires much higher latency (`482.95` seconds) than `Entropy-Revise-64+3` (`210.67` seconds) and `TIGER-Instability-64+3` (`213.81` seconds), while also shifting position once actual forward evaluations are counted.

The second problem is conceptual rather than computational. Signals such as calibration, entropy, and instability are often used as if they simultaneously answer two questions: where the model is fragile, and how the system should intervene. Our evidence suggests that these roles should not be conflated. Calibration is the strongest signal for identifying likely errors, yet it does not support a deployed control gain in our shortlist. Entropy remains informative, but it adds little over random revision in the lightweight screen. Instability motivates the TIGER controller, yet shows only weak evidence that it is the right signal to control on. Under the tested policies, stronger observation does not reliably imply stronger control.

The third problem is over-generalization from favorable tasks. GSM8K supports a superficially optimistic revision story: `Entropy-Revise-64+3` and `TIGER-Instability-64+3` both improve over `Standard-64`, reaching `0.39` accuracy on the shortlist. But the story changes on MATH500, where `Standard-64` leads the same comparison set at `0.23`, ahead of `CORE-proxy-64` (`0.21`), `TIGER-Instability-64+3` (`0.20`), and `Entropy-Revise-64+3` (`0.19`). It changes again on HumanEval, where gating reduces syntax failure by `20` points yet still fails to recover the standard baseline on `pass@1` (`0.04` vs. `0.06`) and worsens runtime failure. These results motivate a task-dependent view of revision: what matters is not only whether a token looks uncertain, but whether the task structure tolerates local repair.

Based on these findings, we position the paper as a compute-normalized diagnostic study rather than a method paper. Our contributions are:

1. We introduce an honest-compute comparison protocol for training-free DLM revision methods, reporting actual NFE, latency, throughput, batch size, backend, and compile status alongside accuracy.
2. We show that honest compute changes key pairwise comparisons and Pareto conclusions on GSM8K, including a concrete reorder between `CORE-proxy-64` and `Entropy-Revise-64+3`.
3. We disentangle observer quality from controller quality, showing that calibration and entropy remain diagnostically informative without yielding proportional intervention gains under the tested policies.
4. We provide task-dependent boundary evidence across GSM8K, MATH500, and HumanEval, showing that revision behavior does not transfer cleanly across reasoning regimes and can fail on structured code generation even when it repairs shallow syntax.

The rest of the paper follows this narrower but more defensible structure. Section 2 positions the paper relative to prior DLM decoding work. Section 3 defines the honest-compute protocol and diagnostic quantities. Section 4 reports the main empirical results on compute shifts, observer-controller mismatch, and task dependence. Section 5 interprets what these results do and do not support. Section 6 concludes with practical recommendations for future DLM revision studies.
