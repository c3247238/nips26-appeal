\begin{abstract}
This position paper argues that autonomous research needs Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses, not paper generators. Large language models (LLMs) can now propose ideas, write code, run tools, and draft papers, but those abilities do not by themselves reproduce the research intuition that human scientists acquire through years of failed experiments, reviewer feedback, metric skepticism, and compute habits. We operationalize research intuition as \emph{trial-to-behavior conversion}: the observable ability of past trials to change future research behavior, including planning, experiment design, evidence validation, claim maturity, resource allocation, critique, and writing boundaries. We propose a Scientific Trial-and-Error Harness: a system environment that makes trials controlled, evidence-bounded, memorable, multi-perspective, compute-aware, and auditable. We present \sibyl{} as a concrete research harness with a file-backed state machine, decision gates, routed memory, role-separated agents, graphics processing unit (GPU) scheduling, and artifact traces. Across \sibyl{} workspaces, we use process evidence to show how trial signals can become algorithm repair, claim calibration, validation-first policy, experiment-first recovery, and narrative restraint. The paper does not claim that autonomous systems can replace scientists. It argues that the community should evaluate whether such systems can turn controlled trial-and-error into auditable research behavior updates.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Human researchers do not only know facts about a field. They accumulate a practical sense of what is worth trying, which metrics are fragile, which negative results are real signals, which baselines are suspicious, and when a polished story is outrunning the evidence. We call this practical sense \emph{research intuition}. The phrase is informal, but the underlying phenomenon is not mysterious: intuition is shaped by repeated trials, failed experiments, advice, reviews, lab notebooks, and long exposure to a domain. \placeholdercite{human research intuition / expertise acquisition}

Current autonomous research systems expose a gap between fluent research activity and durable research judgment. End-to-end AI scientist systems can move from idea generation to experiments and drafts \citep{lu2024aiscientist,yamada2025aiscientistv2}. Research assistant systems and co-scientist systems can help formulate hypotheses, synthesize literature, and rank candidates \citep{schmidgall2025agentlaboratory,gottweis2025aicoscientist,ghareeb2025robin}. Metric-driven loops can repeatedly modify code to optimize a visible score \citep{karpathy2026autoresearch}. These systems are important. Yet a system can produce a plausible paper while failing to remember that an earlier metric was broken, that a pilot result was underpowered, or that a reviewer objection should have blocked a claim.

\textbf{Autonomous research needs a Scientific Trial-and-Error Harness, not a paper generator.} By a \emph{Scientific Trial-and-Error Harness}, we mean the external system around research agents that organizes trials, constrains evidence, preserves memory, separates perspectives, manages compute, and records artifacts. A paper is then an expression of an evidence state, not the primary unit of research capability.

The key bridge in this paper is \emph{trial-to-behavior conversion}: past controlled trials should visibly change future research behavior. A system has learned from a failed pilot only if later plans ask for stronger controls, later experiment agents run validation first, later critics check the same metric failure, later supervisors downgrade claims, later schedulers prefer cheap sanity checks, or later writers narrow the narrative. Figure~\ref{fig:intuition} gives the framing. It intentionally does not claim that agents already possess human intuition. It says that a harness is the substrate through which an autonomous system can externalize and audit the kind of experience that human researchers internalize.

We make three contributions. First, we propose trial-to-behavior conversion as an evaluation target for autonomous research systems. Second, we define the components of a Scientific Trial-and-Error Harness. Third, we describe \sibyl{}, a concrete file-backed research harness, and use workspace traces as process evidence for behavior updates. These traces are not presented as final domain-level scientific findings.

\section{Field landscape}

The field is moving quickly, but several lines of work have a common limitation for open-ended science. End-to-end AI scientist systems demonstrate that LLM agents can generate ideas, execute experiments, and draft papers \citep{lu2024aiscientist,yamada2025aiscientistv2}. This is a useful stress test, but paper completion can reward rhetorical closure: a fluent draft may hide immature evidence.

Metric-driven autoresearch loops show the power of repeated autonomous experimentation when the objective is clear \citep{karpathy2026autoresearch,analemma2026fars}. They are closest in spirit to trial-and-error, but many scientific questions do not have a single trustworthy scalar objective. A visible metric can become a proxy target rather than evidence for understanding.

Research assistants and co-scientist systems emphasize human collaboration, hypothesis generation, and candidate prioritization \citep{schmidgall2025agentlaboratory,gottweis2025aicoscientist,ghareeb2025robin}. They are valuable because human scientists remain responsible for judgment. The gap is that many such systems are optimized for local assistance rather than durable autonomous trial memory.

Verifier-rich domains provide another contrast. AlphaEvolve-style systems can search algorithmic spaces when automatic evaluators are strong \citep{novikov2025alphaevolve}. PaperBench evaluates whether agents can reproduce existing AI research \citep{starace2025paperbench}. These works are complementary. Our focus is the messier setting where the evaluator is a chain of artifacts, baselines, assumptions, negative evidence, and human audit.

Finally, engineering practice around long-running agents already emphasizes harnesses, tools, tracing, and guardrails \citep{anthropic2025harnesses,anthropic2025tools,openai2026tracing,openai2026guardrails}. We extend this view to scientific method itself: the research harness should turn failures into future behavior changes, not only keep a long-running task alive.

\section{Missing behavioral updates}

Many failures in autonomous research can be understood as missing behavior updates.

\paragraph{Paper-generation bias.} If success is defined as producing a complete manuscript, a system can overvalue rhetorical completion. The right update is not ``write a better caveat'' but ``return the unsupported claim to planning, validation, or experiment execution.''

\paragraph{Pilot-to-claim collapse.} A pilot is a small trial used to check whether a direction is worth further investment. It is not a mature scientific result. Without a hard boundary, a single seed, small sample, or short run can be promoted into a claim. The correct update is to label the result as a pilot signal and require a full gate before claim-ready writing.

\paragraph{Proxy and metric gaming.} Systems can optimize visible quantities while missing whether those quantities measure the intended phenomenon. A bad quality score, unstable speedup, or mismatched baseline should trigger metric redesign, source-field anchoring, null controls, or baseline audit.

\paragraph{Memory discontinuity.} A long context window is not the same as research memory. Institutional memory must be retrievable, routed to the right role, updated over time, and able to change prompts, plans, reviews, and gates. Otherwise each iteration behaves like a new research assistant with no lab history.

\paragraph{Unsafe or inefficient trial-and-error.} Agents often need more trials than humans because they lack tacit judgment. But unbounded trials can generate bad data stories, and inefficient trials can consume GPUs, tokens, and human review attention without evidence maturity gain. A \texttt{NO\_GO} outcome, meaning a trial should not proceed under current evidence or engineering conditions, should change the next scheduler and planning policy.

A hypothesis leads to a trial; a trial produces evidence; evidence triggers reflection and memory; memory changes behavior; changed behavior leads to a new trial. \paragraph{Trial orchestration.} A trial is a bounded attempt to learn something: a pilot run, ablation, baseline audit, validation script, or full experiment. Orchestration means each trial has a hypothesis, a task plan, expected artifact outputs, dependencies, and stop conditions. The observable update is that earlier evidence changes later branches and priorities.

\paragraph{Evidence boundary.} Boundary mechanisms prevent immature or corrupted evidence from becoming prose. They include pilot/full gates, claim maturity labels, validation checks, stale-artifact checks, and supervisor decisions. A \texttt{REFINE} gate means ``continue, but fix the evidence or design before advancing.'' An \texttt{ADVANCE} gate means ``proceed with scoped claims and explicit risks.''

\paragraph{Research memory.} Memory is not a transcript. It is a structured layer that turns failures, negative results, review objections, and lessons into routed prompts or checklists for planners, experimenters, critics, supervisors, and writers. The success criterion is behavior change, not memory volume.

\paragraph{Perspective separation.} Research needs different cognitive roles: optimistic exploration, skeptical critique, methodological review, experiment execution, supervision, and writing. Multi-agent debate is useful only when objections become actions: new trials, downgraded claims, or validation tasks.

\paragraph{Resource efficiency.} Compute orchestration is part of scientific method. GPU scheduling, cheap sanity checks, dependency-aware execution, monitoring, and recovery determine how quickly a system reaches useful evidence. Efficiency should be measured as evidence value per unit compute, time, and human audit effort, not as raw throughput.

\paragraph{Artifact trace and claim-evidence graph.} An \emph{artifact} is a persistent research file, such as a plan, script, configuration, log, result table, review, or draft. An \emph{artifact trace} is the record connecting those files over time. A \emph{claim-evidence graph} links paper claims to supporting and contradicting artifacts. Without this substrate, a system can only assert that it learned from trials.

For readers outside autonomous research, it is useful to separate \emph{execution state} from \emph{evidence maturity}. A task can finish while the claim remains immature. Conversely, a negative result can increase maturity by ruling out a tempting story. We use an informal maturity ladder: runnable code or pilot signal is not the same as analysis-ready evidence, and analysis-ready evidence is not the same as paper-ready evidence. A harness should make these distinctions explicit so that ``the system ran'' is not confused with ``the system knows.''

\section{\sibyl{} as a concrete harness}

\sibyl{} is a concrete system contribution: a file-backed, multi-stage autonomous research harness. It realizes the above components as a research operating system, or Research OS, but the operating-system metaphor is secondary. The purpose is to make trial-to-behavior conversion observable. A state machine records which research stage is active and which decision allows the next transition; file-backed artifacts record why the transition happened.

The control plane contains explicit decision and quality gates. The evidence plane stores workspace artifacts, event logs, reviews, and claim traces. The memory plane turns reflection into issue categories and prompt overlays. The role plane separates innovators, skeptics, methodologists, critics, supervisors, and writers. The compute plane treats experiments as asynchronous workloads with GPU scheduling, dependencies, leases, monitoring, and recovery. Table~\ref{tab:sibylmapping} maps these mechanisms to the harness functions.

This structure matters because writing and research should not have the same permissions. A writer can synthesize validated claims, but it should not silently upgrade a pilot result. A supervisor can allow advancement, but only with scoped risks. A critic can object, but the objection should become a task or boundary condition. A scheduler can run long experiments, but should first exploit cheap checks when the likely evidence value is low. These are ordinary engineering choices, but in autonomous research they become scientific integrity mechanisms.

The system can be read as two coupled loops. The inner loop coordinates a single project: debate an idea, plan trials, run experiments, analyze evidence, write scoped claims, review, reflect, and pass a quality gate. The outer loop converts recurring failures into lessons that later prompts can retrieve. This distinction matters because many agent systems already have an inner loop, but autonomous research also needs an institutional layer that remembers why the previous loop failed. Without the outer loop, the system can repeat the same underpowered pilot, baseline mismatch, or stale-table error in a cleaner form.

The design also clarifies what \sibyl{} does \emph{not} claim. A file-backed trace does not make every experiment reproducible. A self-heal substrate does not guarantee arbitrary code repair. A debate among agents does not guarantee independent judgment. A memory overlay does not prove genuine scientific understanding. The claim is narrower and more useful: \sibyl{} exposes the places where trial history can change future action, and therefore makes those changes available for human audit.

\paragraph{\texttt{dynamic-wd}: repair before advancement.} The \texttt{dynamic-wd} workspace studied dynamic weight decay, including a candidate algorithm called Universal Dynamic Weight Decay Control (UDWDC). Its value here is not a final UDWDC performance claim. The process signal was that stability concerns, budget confounds, raw-log mismatch, and weakened hypotheses made the original story unsafe. A weaker system would have had two tempting options: continue writing with caveats, or abandon the direction as failed. \sibyl{} instead took a third path. A supervisor issued \texttt{REFINE}; the plan inserted a repaired UDWDC variant, validation tasks, budget assertions, raw-log cross-checks, and narrower taxonomy claims. After verified repair and explicit residual risks, the system issued calibrated \texttt{ADVANCE}. The important point is not that the algorithm was ``fixed'' once and for all; it is that the evidence challenge changed the algorithm, the plan, the validation checklist, and the claim boundary before expensive full experiments. This is the cleanest positive case because the behavior update crossed role boundaries: supervisor judgment changed planning, planning changed experiment contracts, experiment repair changed claim scope, and reflection changed future checking habits.

\paragraph{\texttt{dlm-acceleration}: negative results calibrate belief.} Diffusion language models (DLMs) generate text through iterative denoising rather than ordinary left-to-right decoding. In \texttt{dlm-acceleration}, unsupported statistics, baseline mismatch, metric confusion, and \texttt{NO\_GO} engineering outcomes forced the system to recalibrate. The useful output was not a stronger speedup claim. It was the deletion or downgrading of unsupported statistics, a shift toward composition-interference analysis, and a lesson that cheap sanity checks should precede expensive pilots. This case illustrates why negative evidence must be first-class: it can protect the research budget, lower claim maturity, and prevent a coherent but false acceleration narrative. It also shows why metric-driven loops need boundary mechanisms. If the visible score is misaligned with the scientific question, optimizing it faster can make the system worse.

\paragraph{\texttt{sae-absorption}: writing is not progress.} Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are models used in mechanistic interpretability to represent neural activations with sparse features. The \texttt{sae-absorption} workspace showed a long-horizon failure mode: repeated writing and polishing did not resolve data hygiene, stale numbers, or claim drift. The process improvement was an experiment-first turn: targeted experiments, negative results, and validation pressure changed the claim hierarchy and made a weaker but more defensible story possible. The companion \texttt{sae-absorption-kimi} workspace is a boundary case: measurement crisis and duplicate or inconsistent artifacts show why validation must precede narrative generation. Its component-level numbers remain audit targets rather than main evidence. Together, these traces show that a research harness must treat ``make the paper cleaner'' and ``make the evidence cleaner'' as different operations. For autonomous systems, this distinction is critical because fluent writing can mask the absence of new evidence.

\paragraph{Ablations and auxiliary cases.} Supporting workspaces make the argument less dependent on a single success story. \texttt{augmentation-order} distinguishes pilot completion from claim maturity: the workflow can visibly complete while the evidence remains too weak for general claims. \texttt{lewm-generalization} shows that real autonomous research quickly becomes a GPU scheduling and recovery problem. The \texttt{ablation-*} workspaces are process diagnostics rather than complete system-effect evidence, but they are still useful because they show what future evaluation should measure: not whether a workflow runs, but whether removing debate, validation, memory, or revision changes overclaim rate, validation failures caught, human audit burden, and maturity gain.

\section{Evaluation, governance, and alternative views}

If the unit of progress is trial-to-behavior conversion, evaluation should change. Figure~\ref{fig:evaluation} gives a compact view.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth,height=0.24\textheight,keepaspectratio]{fig6_evaluation_and_boundary.png}
  \caption{Evaluation should distinguish task completion from claim maturity. A claim-evidence gate can allow, downgrade, or block claims based on artifacts, validation checks, negative evidence, and reviewer objections.}
  \label{fig:evaluation}
\end{figure}

We propose six evaluation dimensions. \emph{Conversion} asks whether a trial changed a later plan, experiment, claim, validation step, scheduler, critic, supervisor, or writer. \emph{Maturity gain} asks whether claims moved from runnable code or pilot signal toward analysis-ready or paper-ready evidence. \emph{Trace fidelity} asks whether claims link to artifacts, scripts, configurations, seeds, and reviews. \emph{Boundary effectiveness} asks whether pilot overclaim, bad data, unsupported statistics, and stale artifacts are blocked. \emph{Resource efficiency} asks how much useful evidence is produced per GPU-hour, token budget, wall-clock time, and human review effort. \emph{Audit burden} asks how long a human needs to verify the paper's core claims.

These dimensions imply concrete protocols. A retrospective audit can reconstruct trial signals, behavior updates, maturity changes, and evidence paths from a completed workspace. A prospective fixed-budget study can compare harness designs under the same research question and compute budget. An injected-failure stress test can add duplicate data, missing results, stale tables, or unsupported statistics and check whether the system blocks narrative generation. A memory test can ask whether a failure in one project changes behavior in a later project.

The strongest near-term protocol is the injected-failure stress test because it is controlled and directly tied to scientific integrity. A harness can be given a workspace with a stale table, missing raw output, duplicated replicate, or unsupported statistic. The evaluation then asks whether the system blocks the claim, routes the issue to the right role, and preserves the negative evidence for future use. This moves evaluation away from whether an agent can write a good-looking paper and toward whether it can defend the paper's evidential boundary.

There are serious counterarguments. One view is that paper generation is a sufficient benchmark because a good paper implicitly requires good evidence. We disagree: a fluent draft can hide weak claims unless the evidence boundary is explicit. A second view is that metric-driven loops are enough. They are enough when the metric is a reliable evaluator, but open-ended science often asks whether the metric itself is meaningful. A third view is that humans should remain in the loop and no long-term autonomous memory is needed. We agree that human responsibility is non-negotiable, but human oversight is easier when the system exposes an auditable trace. A fourth view is that long context can replace memory. Long context helps, but memory must be routed, decayed, revised, and measured by its effect on future behavior.

Governance follows from the same logic. A research harness can make autonomous trial-and-error safer and more auditable, but it can also become a more efficient machine for producing plausible weak science. Risks include scientific spam, rhetorical overclaiming, data integrity failures, metric gaming, unsafe trial-and-error in high-risk domains, negative memory suppressing valid exploration, and multi-agent debate becoming theater. Mitigations include claim-evidence gates, AI-use disclosure, human accountability, negative evidence reporting, domain-specific safety gates, hidden injected failures, and protected integrity constraints.

The governance stance is therefore neither automation enthusiasm nor blanket rejection. The goal is constrained autonomy: allow agents to explore, fail, and repair inside a system that preserves evidence boundaries and leaves a human-auditable trace. In low-risk computational domains, this may permit substantial autonomous iteration. In high-risk domains, the same harness logic should become stricter, with human approval gates before real-world actions and domain-specific safety constraints before trials begin.

This draft also follows the NeurIPS 2026 Position Paper constraint that final position papers should be substantially written by human authors \citep{neurips2026positioncall}.

\section{Conclusion}

Autonomous research should not be judged mainly by whether a system can generate a complete paper. A paper is an expression of an evidence state. The harder capability is whether a system can conduct controlled trials, preserve what went wrong, revise its future behavior, and make the whole process auditable.

We argued that research intuition can be operationalized as trial-to-behavior conversion. We proposed Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses as the system design target for autonomous research. We described \sibyl{} as one concrete implementation, with decision gates, artifact traces, role-separated agents, routed memory, and GPU-aware experiment control. The central claim is deliberately modest but demanding: the next generation of autonomous research systems should compete on how safely, efficiently, and honestly they convert trial history into future research behavior.

\subsection{State machine and typed actions}

\sibyl{} uses an explicit multi-stage artifact-backed state machine. The current stage list includes initialization, literature search, idea debate, planning, pilot experiments, idea validation, experiment cycles, result debate, experiment decisions, outline writing, section writing, integration, final writing review, LaTeX generation, review, reflection, quality gate, and completion. We avoid using a brittle stage count in the main text because the scientific claim is not the number of stages; it is that stage transitions are file-backed and gate-controlled.

The orchestrator also returns typed actions rather than only natural-language instructions. Examples include single-skill calls, parallel skill calls, team actions, parallel agents, shell execution, GPU polling, experiment waiting, completion, and stopped states. This matters because a typed action can be inspected, retried, or blocked. A free-form instruction is harder to audit and harder to connect to later behavior.

\subsection{GPU scheduler and recovery}

The compute plane treats experiments as asynchronous workloads. A task plan is validated, converted into dependency layers, assigned resource needs, and dispatched under GPU leases. The scheduler polls device availability, tracks running tasks, records progress files, and supports recovery when an experiment is interrupted. This does not make scientific results automatically correct. It makes trials cheaper to monitor and easier to resume, which affects how quickly evidence can mature.

\subsection{Reflection and evolution memory}

The memory layer starts from reflection artifacts, but it should not end there. Reflection outputs are normalized into issue categories such as system, experiment, writing, analysis, planning, pipeline, ideation, and efficiency. The evolution layer maintains digests and lessons, computes relevance, and injects selected lessons into later prompts. This is the mechanism behind the paper's distinction between long context and research memory. Long context can expose past text; routed memory can change which checks a role performs.

Important future work is to measure memory effectiveness rather than memory volume. A lesson is useful only if it reduces repeated failures, changes a plan, adds a validation step, or changes a claim boundary. A lesson can also be harmful if it overgeneralizes from a weak failure and suppresses a valid direction. This is why memory requires source links, uncertainty, decay, and re-opening criteria.

\subsection{Decision gates, self-heal, and writing boundaries}

The current system exposes idea validation, experiment decisions, quality gates, review stages, and a structured self-heal substrate. The self-heal layer includes error collection, routing, protected-file constraints, deterministic fixers for some failure classes, and repair-task generation. The safe claim is that these mechanisms improve workflow reliability and create places where failures can become tasks. They do not guarantee general autonomous repair.

For future versions, the most important boundary is a validated claim registry. Writing agents should consume only claims that have maturity labels, artifact links, and validation status. Pilot signals should remain usable, but only as pilot signals. A paper should be generated from the claim registry, not from whatever narrative is most fluent after the latest experiment.

\section{Extended workspace case notes}

The following case notes expand Section~6 while preserving the evidence boundary. Each case is written as a sequence: trial signal, harness mechanism, behavior update, and remaining audit limit.

\subsection{\texttt{dynamic-wd}}

\textbf{Trial signal.} The workspace investigated dynamic weight decay and a candidate UDWDC control rule. The important process event was not a final performance number. It was that stability concerns, budget confounds, raw-log mismatch, and weakened hypotheses made the initial claim package unsafe.

\textbf{Harness mechanism.} Supervisor review issued \texttt{REFINE}. The plan then added algorithm repair, budget assertions, raw-log cross-checks, unit tests, and narrowed taxonomy claims. Reflection lessons also emphasized data-first ordering and control-result sanity checks.

\textbf{Behavior update.} The system changed the algorithm, the task plan, the validation list, and the claim boundary before moving onward. This is a strong example of trial-to-behavior conversion because the earlier evidence challenge changed multiple future roles: planner, experimenter, supervisor, and writer.

\textbf{Audit limit.} Exact UDWDC metrics, pilot accuracies, and full-run results are excluded from this draft until independently audited. The case is used for process evidence only.

\subsection{\texttt{dlm-acceleration}}

\textbf{Trial signal.} The workspace studied acceleration methods for diffusion language models. It encountered unsupported statistics, metric and baseline mismatch, no-op or \texttt{NO\_GO} outcomes, and overhead signals that made a strong acceleration story unsafe.

\textbf{Harness mechanism.} Reflection, lessons, supervisor analysis, and result debate downgraded unsupported novelty, removed weak statistical claims, and reframed the work around composition interference. The process also produced a practical scheduling lesson: cheap sanity checks should precede expensive pilots when a method may be a no-op or add overhead.

\textbf{Behavior update.} Negative results became calibrated belief. Instead of producing a stronger speedup narrative, the system changed the claim hierarchy, metric language, and future experiment policy.

\textbf{Audit limit.} Exact speedups, overheads, runtime estimates, and external baseline comparisons remain excluded from main claims.

\subsection{\texttt{sae-absorption} and \texttt{sae-absorption-kimi}}

\textbf{Trial signal.} The SAE absorption line exposed a long-horizon failure mode: writing-centered iteration can repeatedly polish a narrative while leaving stale artifacts, inconsistent tables, data hygiene issues, and claim drift unresolved. The Kimi companion workspace added a sharper boundary signal: compromised measurement artifacts can look like evidence if narrative generation starts too early.

\textbf{Harness mechanism.} Reflection repeatedly surfaced validation needs, while later experiment-first work changed the contribution hierarchy. The Kimi workspace made validation-before-narrative the central lesson: duplicate detection, feature-count consistency, control completeness, and metric sanity checks must occur before component-level conclusions are promoted.

\textbf{Behavior update.} The research behavior shifted from paper polishing toward targeted experiments, negative-result integration, and weaker but better-supported claims. This is the main example for why memory, boundary, and efficiency must work together. Memory alone can remember the problem; boundary prevents the bad story; efficient trials create the new evidence needed to escape stagnation.

\textbf{Audit limit.} SAE domain conclusions, component-level comparisons, and headline ratios are not treated as settled in this draft.

\subsection{Auxiliary cases}

\texttt{augmentation-order} is a pilot/full boundary case. The workspace shows that an autonomous research loop can execute visible tasks while still lacking claim-ready evidence. The harness lesson is that completion state and evidence maturity must be separate fields.

\texttt{lewm-generalization} is a compute-orchestration case. It is not used as mature domain evidence; it shows that real autonomous research quickly depends on scheduling, monitoring, running-task state, stopping, and recovery.

\texttt{sae-absorption-minimax} is a hypothesis-reversal case. Its value is methodological: a rejected or reversed hypothesis can redirect the project into a new framing rather than being discarded as mere failure. The domain claim remains audit-limited.

\subsection{Ablation diagnostics}

The current \texttt{ablation-*} workspaces should not be read as completed causal evidence about \sibyl{} components. They are diagnostics for future evaluation design. \texttt{ablation-no-debate} suggests that a workflow can run without debate while leaving narrower evidence and exposed measurement sensitivity. \texttt{ablation-mem-positive} shows that completed pilots can still reveal a broken proxy and should trigger measurement redesign. \section{Evaluation protocols}

\paragraph{Retrospective trial-to-behavior audit.} Given a completed workspace, reconstruct the project goal, trial signals, harness mechanisms triggered, behavior updates, maturity labels, negative evidence, and evidence paths. The audit fails if the claimed behavior update cannot be tied to artifacts.

\paragraph{Prospective fixed-budget study.} Give several harness designs the same research question, compute budget, and human review budget. Compare maturity gain, trace fidelity, unsupported claims, negative-result handling, compute reliability, and human audit burden.

\paragraph{Injected-failure stress test.} Insert controlled failures such as duplicate result files, missing outputs, stale tables, inconsistent feature counts, unsupported statistics, or pilot/full mismatches. A strong harness should block or downgrade narrative generation and point to the offending artifact.

\paragraph{Cross-project memory test.} Let one project expose a failure mode, then start another project where the same failure could recur. Measure whether the lesson is retrieved, routed to the right role, and converted into a changed plan or validation check.

\paragraph{Perspective and efficiency ablations.} Remove or weaken skeptic, methodologist, supervisor, validation, memory, or scheduling components under controlled budgets. The outcome should not be only task completion. It should include overclaim rate, missed validation failures, disagreement-to-action conversion, maturity gain, and audit burden.

\section{Governance details}

A research harness should make autonomous trial-and-error safer and more auditable, but it does not remove human responsibility. If optimized poorly, it can become a more efficient machine for plausible weak science. The main risks are scientific spam, rhetorical overclaiming, data integrity failures, metric gaming, unsafe trial-and-error in high-risk domains, negative memory suppressing valid exploration, perspective theater, and self-evolution drift.

The corresponding mitigations are claim-evidence gates, AI-use disclosure, human accountability, negative-evidence reporting, domain-specific safety gates, hidden injected failures, protected integrity constraints, and periodic audit of memory overlays. The most important policy point is simple: human authors must be able to understand, defend, and rewrite the final submission. Autonomous systems can help create evidence traces and drafts; they should not be treated as accountable authors.
