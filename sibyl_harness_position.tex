\title{Autonomous Research Needs Self-Evolving Trial-and-Error Harnesses, Not Paper Generators}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Autonomous research systems should be judged by how they learn from research, not by how smoothly they move from idea to manuscript. Existing systems give agents tools, roles, scaffolds, and research pipelines. These components help agents complete research tasks, but they do not by themselves create research judgment: the experience-backed ability to distrust weak signals, choose better controls, narrow claims, and stop unproductive lines of work. We propose Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses as the missing design target. A harness lets agents run bounded trials in real research environments, preserves positive and negative outcomes, routes lessons to the roles that must act on them, and changes both future research behavior and the harness itself. We make this target auditable through two conversion units. \emph{Trial-to-behavior conversion} links a trial signal to a later change in planning, validation, claim scope, scheduling, critique, or writing. \emph{Trial-to-harness-behavior conversion} links recurring process failures to new gates, prompt overlays, telemetry checks, scheduler policies, repair tasks, or protected constraints. We illustrate the framework with \sibyl{}, a file-backed autonomous research harness. A retrospective audit of its workspaces identifies 8 high-confidence conversion events with a median latency of 3 iterations. A recovered-failure registry further shows how five naturally occurring failure classes, including duplicate results, confidence-interval inversion, stale numbers, feature-count mismatch, and unsupported statistics, were blocked, downgraded, or routed into later repair.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

Autonomous research is becoming a concrete systems problem. LLM-driven agents can already read papers, write code, call tools, run experiments, revise drafts, and keep long contexts. The harder question is no longer only whether these agents can assist a human researcher. It is whether an agent can improve as a researcher across attempts: form better priors, notice fragile evidence earlier, stop bad stories before they become claims, and carry hard lessons into the next project.

Human researchers improve this way. A researcher who has spent months with a benchmark often knows which metric is brittle, which baseline result is suspicious, which negative result is useful, and which pilot result is not ready for a paper. This ability is often called intuition or taste. We call it \emph{research judgment}: experience-backed behavior that changes what the researcher does next. The important point is not that judgment is mysterious. The important point is that judgment is produced by many concrete trials, mistakes, repairs, and reviews.

Recent autonomous research systems automate many parts of the research loop. End-to-end AI scientist systems move from idea generation to draft papers \citep{lu2024aiscientist,yamada2025aiscientistv2}. Research assistant and co-scientist systems help form hypotheses, synthesize literature, and rank candidates \citep{schmidgall2025agentlaboratory,gottweis2025aicoscientist,ghareeb2025robin}. Metric-driven loops can search over code or methods when the score is visible \citep{karpathy2026autoresearch}. These systems show that agents can participate in scientific work. They also make a deeper design gap visible: completing research stages is not the same as accumulating research judgment.

The gap appears when a system observes a useful signal but fails to change future behavior. A pilot exposes a broken metric, yet the next plan keeps the metric. A reviewer objection identifies an unsupported claim, yet the writer keeps the claim polished. A failed GPU run reveals a wasteful experiment order, yet the scheduler repeats the order. A reflection file states the right lesson, yet the planner, critic, or supervisor never sees it. These are not just bugs. They are missing update paths from trial history to later action.

\textbf{Autonomous research needs Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses.} A harness is not a paper generator. It is the research environment around the agent: the state, tools, roles, memory, gates, artifact contracts, compute policy, and repair mechanisms that let agents try ideas under bounded controls and then use what happened. In a strong harness, past trials change future research behavior. Recurring process failures also change the harness itself. These two loops form agent-harness co-evolution.

We operationalize this position with two auditable conversion units. \emph{Trial-to-behavior conversion} asks whether a trial signal at iteration $t$ changes an action at iteration $t+k$, such as a plan, validation, claim boundary, schedule, critique, or writing scope. \emph{Trial-to-harness-behavior conversion} asks whether repeated process failures change gates, prompt overlays, telemetry requirements, scheduler policies, repair tasks, or protected constraints. The unit is small on purpose: a signal, a trace path, and a later behavior change. Figure~\ref{fig:hook} shows the audit unit inside the adaptive harness loop.

We make three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A judgment-centered framing for autonomous research.} We argue that autonomous research systems should be evaluated by whether controlled trials change future research behavior, not only by whether a pipeline produces a complete paper.
  \item \textbf{Two auditable conversion targets.} We define trial-to-behavior conversion and trial-to-harness-behavior conversion as small, inspectable units that link trial signals to later behavior updates and harness updates.
  \item \textbf{A Scientific Trial-and-Error Harness framework.} We give seven harness functions and observable commitments for building systems that preserve evidence, route memory, separate perspectives, manage compute, and repair their own failure paths. We illustrate the framework through \sibyl{}, a file-backed autonomous research harness, and its workspace traces.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{End-to-end autonomous research systems.}
AI scientist systems show that LLM agents can generate ideas, run experiments, interpret results, and draft manuscripts \citep{lu2024aiscientist,yamada2025aiscientistv2}. Agent laboratory and co-scientist systems emphasize human collaboration, literature synthesis, hypothesis generation, and candidate ranking \citep{schmidgall2025agentlaboratory,gottweis2025aicoscientist,ghareeb2025robin}. These systems make more of the scientific workflow executable. Our focus is different. We ask whether trial history changes later research behavior and whether repeated process failures change the harness that hosts later trials.

\paragraph{Metric-driven search and verifier-rich discovery.}
Autoresearch loops, FARS-style systems, AlphaEvolve-style systems, and PaperBench-like evaluations show the value of repeated trials when feedback is clear \citep{karpathy2026autoresearch,analemma2026fars,novikov2025alphaevolve,starace2025paperbench}. This line of work is closest to classical AutoML, neural architecture search, and black-box optimization: the system searches over candidates under an explicit objective \citep{hutter2019automl,zoph2017nas}. The difference is that open-ended research often lacks a single trusted objective. A harness must therefore route measurement failures and negative results into revised metrics, controls, and claim boundaries, not only into another search step.

\paragraph{Agent memory and reflection.}
Reflection systems show that verbal lessons from failed trials can improve later agent behavior \citep{shinn2023reflexion}. Memory systems show how agents can store and retrieve information across long interactions \citep{packer2023memgpt}. Self-Refine, Voyager, and Generative Agents also show how critique, skill libraries, and memory can shape future actions \citep{madaan2023selfrefine,wang2023voyager,park2023generativeagents}. Trial-to-behavior conversion is more specific than memory reuse. A lesson counts only when it reaches the role that must act on it and appears in a later plan, validation, claim boundary, schedule, critique, or writing restriction.

\paragraph{Harnesses, tools, provenance, and reproducibility.}
Long-running agents require harnesses, tools, tracing, and guardrails to make progress inspectable and safe \citep{anthropic2025harnesses,anthropic2025tools,openai2026tracing,openai2026guardrails}. Scientific provenance and reproducibility work gives complementary tools for connecting claims to artifacts \citep{lebo2013provo,gundersen2018reproducibility}. Work on weak evidence and publication incentives warns that polished claims can outrun the evidence base \citep{ioannidis2005why}. We connect these threads by treating a harness as a memory-bearing research environment with explicit evidence boundaries.

\paragraph{Expertise and research judgment.}
The idea that expertise grows from repeated, feedback-rich experience is old \citep{ericsson1996expert}. Research judgment in this paper is the system-level analogue of that process. We do not claim that agents acquire human expertise in the psychological sense. We make a narrower systems claim: if an autonomous research system has learned from a trial, later behavior should expose that learning.

\section{Failure modes: where autonomous research loses experience}

Autonomous research systems already make many research actions executable. The core failure is subtler. A system can propose hypotheses, run code, optimize a metric, preserve logs, and write a draft while still losing the experience that those actions should have produced. We call the missing route an \emph{update path}: the path by which a trial signal becomes a later constraint on planning, validation, claim scope, resource allocation, critique, writing, or harness behavior.

\paragraph{F1: Paper completion hides evidence immaturity.}
A pipeline can finish a paper even when the evidence is weak or corrupted. The needed update path is from weak evidence to a claim boundary: the writer should downgrade or remove the claim, and the planner should receive a validation task.

\paragraph{F2: Pilot signals collapse into paper claims.}
A cheap pilot can be useful because it points to a direction. It does not by itself support a broad scientific claim. The needed update path is from pilot signal to maturity state: the system should mark the result as exploratory and require a stronger full-scale gate before writing general conclusions.

\paragraph{F3: Visible objectives become bad objectives.}
Metric-driven loops work well when the objective is trusted. Research metrics often are not. A broken metric should trigger measurement critique, control design, and baseline audit. If the system simply optimizes the same metric again, the trial did not become research judgment.

\paragraph{F4: Memory stays textual instead of routed.}
Long context can preserve a lesson in text while failing to route it to the role that needs it. The needed update path is from lesson to role-specific behavior: planner checks, critic objections, supervisor gates, scheduler policy, or writer restrictions.

\paragraph{F5: More trials do not improve trial policy.}
Many-trial systems can increase experiment volume without improving the order, cost, stopping rules, or sanity checks. A failed or wasteful run should change resource allocation, early-stop policy, or cheap-check ordering.

\paragraph{F6: Process failures recur because the harness does not change.}
Missing artifacts, stale tables, incomplete telemetry, and paper/evidence synchronization errors are not only project-local mistakes. Repeated process failures should change the harness: new gates, prompt overlays, artifact contracts, telemetry requirements, repair tasks, or protected constraints.

These failure modes appear directly in workspace traces. In \texttt{sae-absorption-kimi}, paper-facing quality reached 8/10 exactly when verification exposed byte-identical Matryoshka and MultiScale replicates, a 1024 vs. 16{,}384 feature-count mismatch, missing L0 control, negative explained variance, and 81.6\% dead TopK latents. In \texttt{augmentation-order}, a CIFAR-10/ResNet18 pilot produced a 2.68 percentage-point spread and a \texttt{GO} signal, but reflection scored the project 4.0/10 and blocked full-scale claims because Tier-1 experiments remained unrun. In \texttt{dlm-acceleration}, unsupported statistics and metric failures changed a polished speedup story into an interference taxonomy. In \texttt{dynamic-wd}, a \texttt{REFINE} decision became UDWDC-v2 repair, budget assertions, 9/9 stability tests, and scoped \texttt{ADVANCE}. Otherwise, these signals would have been recorded without changing research behavior.

\section{Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses}
\label{sec:harness}

The diagnosis above changes the unit of analysis. The central unit of autonomous research is the \emph{trial}: a bounded encounter with a real research environment that produces a signal about a hypothesis, method, measurement, baseline, validation check, resource policy, or process. A trial is valuable when the signal changes later behavior. A failed trial can be especially useful because it rules out a tempting story, exposes a fragile metric, or reveals a weakness in the harness.

A Scientific Trial-and-Error Harness is the environment that makes those updates possible. It is a set of harness functions around the agent: state, tools, roles, memory, gates, artifact contracts, compute control, and repair mechanisms. These functions do not guarantee good science. They make research behavior inspectable at the places where judgment should appear.

\paragraph{H1: Trial orchestration.}
Each trial should have a question, expected evidence, dependencies, outputs, and stop conditions. The observable commitment is that earlier evidence changes the next trial plan, branch priority, or task dependency.

\paragraph{H2: Evidence maturity.}
Execution completion, pilot signal, analysis-ready evidence, paper-ready evidence, and audited claim are different states. The observable commitment is that a claim advances only after validation, scope control, and artifact links. Negative evidence can increase maturity by ruling out a false story.

\paragraph{H3: Traceability.}
A behavior update should be tied to artifacts: plans, configs, logs, tables, reviews, negative evidence, and writing changes. The observable commitment is that a reader can reconstruct why a later action changed.

\paragraph{H4: Routed research memory.}
Reflection should not remain as a free-form note. Lessons must be routed to planners, experimenters, critics, supervisors, schedulers, or writers. The observable commitment is that a past lesson changes a later role-specific check or decision.

\paragraph{H5: Perspective separation.}
Optimistic, skeptical, methodological, supervisory, and writing roles should have different authority. The observable commitment is that objections become validation tasks, plan mutations, stopped branches, or claim downgrades rather than free-form disagreement.

\paragraph{H6: Resource-aware trial policy.}
Research trial-and-error is bounded by GPU time, token budget, and human review attention. The observable commitment is that failed or wasteful trials change sanity-check policy, allocation, monitoring, or recovery behavior.

\paragraph{H7: Harness self-evolution with protected constraints.}
Some trial signals are about the research question. Others are about the harness that produced the research. Repeated missing artifacts, stale outputs, telemetry gaps, or gate failures should change prompt overlays, validation gates, artifact contracts, scheduler policies, or repair tasks. The observable commitment is that self-evolution strengthens evidence integrity rather than optimizing polish, pass rate, or reviewer gaming.

\textbf{Trial-to-behavior conversion} is the agent-side audit unit. A signal at iteration $t$ must alter an action at iteration $t+k$: which direction to try, which evidence to distrust, which validation to run earlier, how strongly to state a claim, when to stop a branch, how to allocate GPU budget, or when to delay writing. A conversion links three artifacts: a trial signal, a trace path, and a later behavior change. Figure~\ref{fig:conversionevent} shows this unit.

\textbf{Trial-to-harness-behavior conversion} is the harness-side audit unit. A recurring process failure must alter a harness function: a gate, prompt overlay, telemetry requirement, scheduler policy, repair task, artifact contract, or protected constraint. These two conversion units define agent-harness co-evolution: the agent accumulates research judgment, and the harness learns how to make later research loops safer, cheaper, and more informative.

\section{\sibyl{} as an illustrative harness instance}
\label{sec:sibyl}

\sibyl{} is a file-backed autonomous research harness used here as an illustrative instance of the framework. It is not treated as a controlled benchmark against prior systems. Its value in this paper is that its workspaces preserve enough state, artifacts, reflections, reviews, and evolution records to inspect whether trial signals changed later behavior.

The implementation follows the seven harness functions in Section~\ref{sec:harness}. Trial orchestration is implemented through an artifact-backed state machine and task plans. Evidence maturity is controlled through idea decisions, experiment decisions, quality gates, validation requirements, and writing restrictions. Traceability comes from workspace artifacts, event logs, reviews, experiment state, and writing outputs. Routed memory is implemented through reflection postprocessing, evolution records, issue categories, and role-specific lesson overlays. Perspective separation is provided by planner, experimenter, critic, supervisor, skeptic, methodologist, writer, and editor roles. Resource-aware trial policy uses GPU scheduling, dependency layers, leases, monitoring, and recovery. Harness self-evolution uses global and per-project evolution records, repair tasks, self-heal mechanisms, and protected constraints.

This implementation detail matters because writing and research roles do not have the same authority. A writer can synthesize validated claims but cannot silently upgrade a pilot result. A supervisor can allow advancement only with scoped risks. A critic can object, but the objection must become a task or boundary condition. A scheduler can run expensive experiments, but low evidence value should trigger cheap sanity checks first. These boundaries are scientific integrity mechanisms, not only engineering choices.

\paragraph{Patterns across the audited cases.}
The detailed traces behind these conversion events are preserved in Appendix~\ref{sec:extended-cases}. The patterns are concrete. In \texttt{dynamic-wd}, factual paper errors, budget-equivalence concerns, corrupted controls, and hidden negative BEM results become UDWDC-v2 floor clipping, EMA smoothing, epoch budget assertions, and a 9-of-9 stability test before the next \texttt{ADVANCE}. In \texttt{dlm-acceleration}, fabricated Wilcoxon p-values, an $\alpha=0.52$ claim against a measured accept rate of 0.881, and an M1 no-op at 1.16\(\times\) move the paper from a multiplicative speedup story to an interference taxonomy. In \texttt{sae-absorption}, ten consecutive iterations recommend \texttt{validate\_integration.py} before iteration 11 implements it, fixes 51 of 53 checks, and reduces a stale 4.1\(\times\) headline to 2.7\(\times\). In \texttt{sae-absorption-kimi}, a writing score of 8/10 coexists with a supervisor score of 4.5/10 because four of five Matryoshka replicates are byte-identical to MultiScale and the run uses 1024 features against a 16{,}384 claim. Figure~\ref{fig:dynamicwdflow} traces the Dynamic-WD path in detail. These cases support the same claim: the useful signal is not the failure by itself, but the later change in algorithm, validation, claim boundary, writing permission, or harness policy.

\paragraph{Recovered-failure registry.}
The strongest controlled experiment would inject known failures into a held-out workspace. We do not report such a new run here. Instead, we report a recovered-failure registry from naturally occurring traces. This is weaker than an injected benchmark but stronger than anecdote because each row contains a concrete failure class, a source artifact, a catch mechanism, and a later update. Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-recovered-failures} lists the per-row source paths.


\paragraph{Harness-side evidence.}
The \texttt{.sibyl/evolution} records show trial-to-harness-behavior conversion. The central digest contains 416 recurring issue patterns: 212 experiment, 89 writing, 84 analysis, 20 system, 4 ideation, 3 pipeline, 3 planning, and 1 efficiency. Routing is explicit: experiment issues are assigned to experimenter/server-experimenter/planner roles, analysis issues to supervisor/critic/skeptic/reflection roles, and writing issues to section-writer/editor/codex-writer roles. The prompt loader then injects selected lessons as role-specific overlays. The d2Cache case also exposes a resource-policy update: the reflection records that a 54-minute pilot revealed a 15.2\(\times\) overhead failure that a 10-minute TPS sanity check could have caught earlier.

\section{Comparison with prior systems}

Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-system-comparison} compares AI Scientist-style systems, Agent Laboratory/co-scientist systems, AlphaEvolve-style verifier systems, Reflexion/memory agents, long-running agent harnesses, and \sibyl{} traces against H1--H7. The comparison is not a leaderboard. It shows that prior systems automate important parts of research, but they usually expose only partial update paths from trial signal to role behavior or harness behavior. Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses require these ingredients to be connected: trial signals must change role behavior, claim maturity, resource policy, and the harness path that allowed repeated failures.

\section{Evaluation, limitations, and governance}

\paragraph{Evaluation protocols.}
The commitments in Table~\ref{tab:evaluation} define what should be measured. Useful protocols include retrospective trace audits, fixed-budget comparisons, cross-project memory tests, and injected-failure benchmarks. The strongest benchmark inserts duplicate results, CI inversions, stale tables, feature-count mismatches, missing seeds, or unsupported statistics, then tests whether the harness blocks or downgrades narrative generation.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The evidence is retrospective and concentrated in one author-built harness for computational AI/ML workspaces. The conversion count is hand-marked, the recovered-failure registry uses natural failures rather than a new injected benchmark, and the ablations are process diagnostics rather than repeated statistical experiments. Thus, the paper supports a design position and an existence proof, not a claim that \sibyl{} outperforms other systems.

\paragraph{Alternative views.}
A good final paper does not imply good evidence: \texttt{sae-absorption-kimi} improved writing while the evidence base collapsed. Metric-driven loops and long context are also not enough: \texttt{dlm-acceleration} had to change the objective after unsupported statistics and M1 no-op evidence. Human responsibility remains central, but scalable oversight needs traces that show why claims changed, not only final prose.

\paragraph{Governance.}

Harness self-evolution should be versioned, testable, and constrained by protected integrity rules.

\section{Conclusion}

Autonomous research should not be judged mainly by whether a system can generate a complete paper. A paper is an expression of an evidence state. The harder capability is whether a system can turn trial history into research judgment: better plans, stronger validation, narrower claims, safer resource policy, and a harness that becomes harder to fool over time. Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses make this capability auditable by linking trial signals to later behavior updates and harness updates.

\subsection{State machine and typed actions}

\sibyl{} uses an explicit multi-stage artifact-backed state machine. The current stage list includes initialization, literature search, idea debate, planning, pilot experiments, idea validation, experiment cycles, result debate, experiment decisions, outline writing, section writing, integration, final writing review, LaTeX generation, review, reflection, quality gate, and completion. We avoid using a brittle stage count in the main text because the scientific claim is not the number of stages; it is that stage transitions are file-backed and gate-controlled.

The orchestrator also returns typed actions rather than only natural-language instructions. Examples include single-skill calls, parallel skill calls, team actions, parallel agents, shell execution, GPU polling, experiment waiting, completion, and stopped states. This matters because a typed action can be inspected, retried, or blocked. A free-form instruction is harder to audit and harder to connect to later behavior.

\subsection{GPU scheduler and recovery}

The compute mechanism treats experiments as asynchronous workloads. A task plan is validated, converted into dependency layers, assigned resource needs, and dispatched under GPU leases. The scheduler polls device availability, tracks running tasks, records progress files, and supports recovery when an experiment is interrupted. This makes trials cheaper to monitor and easier to resume, which affects how quickly evidence can mature.

\subsection{Reflection and evolution memory}

The memory layer starts from reflection artifacts, but it should not end there. Reflection outputs are normalized into issue categories such as system, experiment, writing, analysis, planning, pipeline, ideation, and efficiency. The evolution layer maintains digests and lessons, computes relevance, and injects selected lessons into later prompts. This is the mechanism behind the paper's distinction between long context and research memory. Long context can expose past text; routed memory can change which checks a role performs.

Memory effectiveness is measured by behavior, not volume. A lesson informs the system when it reduces repeated failures, changes a plan, adds a validation step, changes a claim boundary, or repairs the harness path that allowed the failure. The memory layer therefore stores source links, uncertainty, decay, and re-opening criteria alongside the lesson text.

\subsection{Decision gates, self-heal, and writing boundaries}

The current system exposes idea validation, experiment decisions, quality gates, review stages, and a structured self-heal substrate. The self-heal layer includes error collection, routing, protected-file constraints, deterministic fixers for recurring failure classes, and repair-task generation. These mechanisms improve workflow reliability and create places where failures become tasks before they recur in the next research loop.

The natural boundary mechanism is a validated claim registry. Writing agents consume claims with maturity labels, artifact links, and validation status. Pilot signals remain usable as pilot signals. A paper is generated from the claim registry, not from whatever narrative is most polished after the latest experiment.

The table above gives cross-project evidence. The following complete traces show how a reader should inspect one project end to end. Each trace follows the same chain: project goal, iteration state, trial signal, harness decision, next behavior, and claim effect.

\subsubsection{Complete trace I: \texttt{sae-absorption}, from writing stagnation to validation-first research}

\textbf{Project goal.} The workspace studies SAE feature absorption: when a parent feature absorbs child-feature behavior, how that effect should be measured, and which claims survive across layers, domains, and interventions. This is the best long-horizon case because it contains 11 iteration directories and full role artifacts across planning, experiments, supervision, writing, and reflection.

\textbf{Iteration map.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Iterations 1--5: initial measurement and narrative formation.} The system builds an initial absorption story, writes drafts, runs targeted probes, and accumulates reflection artifacts. These iterations establish the central research object but also start the pattern that later becomes important: the paper can become smoother while source-to-paper numeric consistency remains fragile.
  \item \textbf{Iterations 6--8: writing stagnation and missing validation.} The quality trajectory stalls around 6.5. Reflection repeatedly asks for \texttt{validate\_integration.py}, but the recommendation remains a lesson rather than a hard writing gate. Iteration 8 records the ninth recommendation of the script and finds a fabricated 12.3\% hedging number where raw data gives 0.0\%. The action plan turns this into Gate 0: a 1.5-hour, zero-GPU source-to-paper cross-check must run before further writing.
  \item \textbf{Iteration 9: experiment-first break from polishing.} The project breaks the writing-only loop by executing new empirical checks: activation patching, tightened hedging analysis, CMI replication, and threshold sensitivity. The score rises from 6.5 to 7.0 because the system has produced new evidence rather than only a cleaner narrative.
  \item \textbf{Iteration 10: scientific progress plus evidence-boundary failure.} The iteration produces a strong probe-degradation result ($R^2=0.777$, $\rho=-1.0$, $p=0.009$), decoder-magnitude evidence (6.16 nats for first-letter and 3.98 nats for city-continent), and rate-distortion rejection across 131 pairs. The score still regresses from 7.0 to 6.5 because the paper imports new integrity errors: Table 3 confidence-interval inversion, three incompatible first-letter rates, stale 4.1$\times$ headline language, layer-multiplier mismatch, and an unverified patching sign reversal.
  \item \textbf{Iteration 11: data integrity becomes the iteration objective.} The next plan explicitly makes the iteration about data integrity and verification. \texttt{validate\_integration.py} is implemented, 51/53 checks pass, CI inversions are fixed, per-token aggregation becomes canonical, the headline changes from 4.1$\times$ to 2.7$\times$, and the 21.6\%/27.1\%/34.5\% first-letter rates are traced to distinct experimental conditions. Probe degradation becomes contribution \#1, and a 20-entity city-continent spot-check confirms 62.7\% mean recovery versus 61.9\% expected ($d=2.04$, $p<0.001$).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{What the trace demonstrates.} \texttt{sae-absorption} shows the full agent-harness loop. Trial signals first change the agent's research behavior: writing gives way to experiments, then experiments give way to validation-first planning. They also change the harness boundary: source-to-paper numeric validation becomes a gate rather than an optional reflection note. The final contribution hierarchy is not the one the early drafts wanted; it is the one that survived repeated trial, critique, and validation failure mode.

\subsubsection{Complete trace II: \texttt{dynamic-wd}, from unstable control law to scoped advancement}

\textbf{Project goal.} The workspace studies dynamic weight decay and UDWDC-style control. It is the cleanest positive trace because a concrete algorithmic defect becomes a repaired method, explicit tests, a mutated task plan, and scoped advancement.

\textbf{Iteration map.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Iterations 0--7: idea formation, early pilots, and repeated evidence gaps.} The project builds a dynamic weight-decay story and accumulates experiments across small and medium settings. Quality moves upward and downward rather than monotonically: the quality log includes 5.5, 7.0, 5.0, 6.5, 6.75, 7.0, and later 6.5. This volatility is useful because it exposes the harness's claim-boundary function: better prose or a promising pilot does not erase unresolved controls.
  \item \textbf{Iterations 8--12: recurring control and generalization failure mode.} Reflection and evolution records keep surfacing ImageNet absence, equivalence-test weakness, budget confounds, and control reliability. The evolution outcome marks missing ImageNet evidence as recurring for 7+ iterations, records TOST equivalence passing only 6/12 comparisons, and proposes a Phase-0 smoke test before a 9-run ImageNet-100 replication plan.
  \item \textbf{Iteration 13: REFINE signal becomes unavoidable.} Reflection records raw-log mismatches, hidden negative BEM results, corrupted controls, higher-lambda control gaps, and a 90-epoch ImageNet need. The important system behavior is that these signals do not get absorbed as prose caveats. They become blockers for broad advancement.
  \item \textbf{Iteration 14: repair, validation, and scoped ADVANCE.} The supervisor path introduces UDWDC-v2 with floor clipping, EMA smoothing, and epoch budget assertions. The UDWDC-v2 fix passes 9/9 stability tests; the Kp-only budget changes from 0.0 to 90.61; ImageNet CSI reaches 0.987; H6 is no longer supported; and the ImageNet budget confound remains explicitly recorded. The plan mutates into a 14-task refinement and full-experiment program including UDWDC-v2 repair, diagnostic CIFAR-10, H1 unification fit, CIFAR-100 ablations, batch-size sweeps, temporal-gate tests, alignment informativeness, ImageNet main runs, and budget-matched ImageNet controls.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{What the trace demonstrates.} \texttt{dynamic-wd} shows trial-to-behavior conversion in its most direct form. A failed or unstable trial changes the algorithm, then the task plan, then the validation order, then the claim boundary. The final \texttt{ADVANCE} is stronger because it is scoped: H1 is narrowed to a three-tier taxonomy, H6 is treated as unsupported or uncertain, budget confounds remain visible, and the full-run plan inherits the repair obligations.

\subsubsection{Complete trace III: \texttt{dlm-acceleration}, from speedup narrative to interference taxonomy}

\textbf{Project goal.} The workspace studies acceleration methods for diffusion language models and asks whether methods compose multiplicatively. It is shorter than the previous two projects, but it is the sharpest negative-evidence trace: the harness converts unsupported speedup claims into metric repair, full-scale gates, and a new thesis about interference.

\textbf{Iteration map.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Iteration 1: paper and metric errors become experiment requirements.} Reflection fixes fabricated Wilcoxon claims, a tau$=0.0$ paradox, failure-atlas number mismatches, QAS formula inconsistency, a 6-pair overclaim where only 3 pairs are feasible, novelty overclaiming, and M3 speed-report mismatch. The same action plan finds a new alpha error: the draft claims $\alpha=0.52$, while raw results report avg accept rate 0.881 on GSM8K and 0.830 combined. Pairwise Ortho evidence is only a 2-seed, 200/1319-sample pilot with per-seed range [1.292, 1.478], so the plan calls for full benchmark replication on 1319 GSM8K plus 500 MATH500 with 3 seeds and bootstrap confidence intervals.
  \item \textbf{Iteration 2: the scientific story flips from multiplication to interference.} Result debate reports 15 experiment groups, M1 as a functional no-op around 1.16$\times$, destructive M1+M3 interference with ortho 0.41--0.52, M3+IGSD partial interference with ortho 0.61--0.84, and an autoregressive comparison where Qwen2.5-7B reaches 96\% GSM8K at 70.9--471.1 TPS. The old claim that speedups compose multiplicatively no longer matches the evidence.
  \item \textbf{Iteration 3: pilot/full maturity rules become explicit.} Later lessons encode the rule the earlier paper lacked: pairwise Ortho with $N<500$ must be labeled a pilot estimate with bootstrap intervals; core pairwise claims require full-scale $N\ge1319$ and 3 seeds. The system also separates per-token speed from output-length effects and standardizes baseline TPS before additional comparisons.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{What the trace demonstrates.} \texttt{dlm-acceleration} shows that negative results are not dead ends. They are belief-calibration events. The harness forces unsupported statistics out of the paper, splits metric definitions, blocks overbroad pairwise claims, and changes the research question from ``how do we multiply speedups?'' to ``which mechanisms interfere, and under what evidence maturity?''

\subsubsection{Boundary trace: \texttt{sae-absorption-kimi}, when writing quality rises as evidence collapses}

\texttt{sae-absorption-kimi} is not used as a clean success story. Its value is a boundary failure that exposes why data-validation gates must sit before narrative generation. Iteration 1 marks the first-letter proxy as degenerate: 26/27 GPT-2 checkpoints in one experiment and 9/10 in another return exactly 0.0, and the task-agnostic metric is negatively correlated with the first-letter benchmark ($r=-0.592$, $p=0.12$). The pilot quality is \texttt{NO-GO}, and the action plan marks the pilot/full escalation error as \texttt{requires\_system\_change=true}: if any pilot rating is \texttt{NO-GO}, the next stage must be metric or code repair rather than scale-up.

Later, the project shows the danger of writing improvement without evidence improvement. A full component run covers 7 variants by 5 replicates, but reflection finds that writing reaches 8/10 while supervisor and critic scores fall to 4.5/10 and 5/10. The evidence base contains byte-identical Matryoshka and MultiScale replicates across nine metrics, \texttt{NUM\_FEATURES=1024} while the paper claims 16,384 features, missing L0-matched ablation, 81.6\% dead TopK latents, negative explained variance, and a canonical summary with only 3/7 variants. This trace is the cleanest warning that polished writing can move in the opposite direction from scientific maturity.

\subsubsection{Reversal trace: \texttt{sae-absorption-minimax}, from falsified hypothesis to new framing}

\texttt{sae-absorption-minimax} shows how the system handles hypothesis reversal. Iteration 1 reverses H3: high-absorption features are more steerable, not less, with reported $r=+0.3548$ and $p=2.92e{-}04$. The paper reframes around ``Absorption as Steering Signature'' instead of discarding the result as a failure. Later validation complicates the new story: a controlled matched design gives $p=0.299$, the beta=20 reversal is uncorrected at $p=0.015$, UAS saturates at layer 8, and later steering-protocol summaries report all effects at 0.0. The valuable behavior is the sequence: falsification becomes a new framing, and later validation is still allowed to downgrade that framing and create pivot signal.

\texttt{augmentation-order} is a pilot/full boundary case. The experiment state records 20 tracked tasks, 18 completed and 2 failed. \texttt{lewm-generalization} is a compute-orchestration case. Its pilot reaches \texttt{GO} with 0.88 confidence and 48.8s training, while the workspace status records \texttt{stop\_requested=true}. \subsection{Ablation diagnostics}

The \texttt{ablation-*} workspaces are diagnostic stress tests for update paths. \texttt{ablation-no-debate} has a clean configuration knob, \texttt{debate\_rounds: 0}, and reaches a pilot \texttt{GO} while exposing metric sensitivity. \texttt{ablation-mem-positive} is best read as an evolution-follow-up case rather than a clean memory ablation: a recurring activation-patching lesson is later answered by a pilot with 9/9 patching checks and 67.3\% mean recovery. \texttt{ablation-mem-negative} is strongest as a boundary case because the quality gate hard-blocks a missing review score and rolls the workspace back. \texttt{ablation-no-validation} is config-only in this audit, and \texttt{ablation-no-revision} is a stagnation/escalation case rather than a clean revision-off component test.

\textbf{Details used in the main audit.} \texttt{ablation-no-debate} (\texttt{debate\_rounds: 0}) ran a synthetic absorption pilot to \texttt{GO}; the trained SAE showed higher absorption than the random baseline under the overlap method (0.50 versus 0.25), while the ablation method gave 1.00 for both, exposing a measurement-method sensitivity that debate should expose earlier. \texttt{ablation-mem-positive} records explicit validation against evolution lessons: activation patching succeeds on 9/9 checks with 67.3\% mean recovery, but later reflection still records multiple-comparison, baseline, figure, and circularity issues. \texttt{ablation-mem-negative} contains a runtime boundary artifact: \texttt{Quality gate HARD BLOCK: iteration 5 has no review score. \section{Evaluation protocols}
\label{sec:appendix-eval-protocols}

\paragraph{Retrospective trial-to-behavior audit.} Given a completed workspace, reconstruct the project goal, trial signals, harness mechanisms triggered, behavior updates, maturity labels, negative evidence, and evidence paths. The audit fails if the claimed behavior update cannot be tied to artifacts.

\paragraph{Prospective fixed-budget study.} Give several harness designs the same research question, compute budget, and human review budget. Compare maturity gain, trace fidelity, unsupported claims, negative-result handling, compute reliability, and human audit burden.

\paragraph{Injected-failure stress test.} Insert controlled failures such as duplicate result files, missing outputs, stale tables, inconsistent feature counts, unsupported statistics, or pilot/full mismatches. A strong harness should block or downgrade narrative generation and point to the offending artifact.

\paragraph{Cross-project memory test.} Let one project expose a failure mode, then start another project where the same failure could recur. Measure whether the lesson is retrieved, routed to the right role, and converted into a changed plan or validation check.

\paragraph{Harness-evolution test.} Let one project expose a process failure such as missing telemetry, stale figure assets, absolute artifact paths, or paper/LaTeX desynchronization. Start later projects where the same failure could recur. Measure whether the harness adds a gate, sentinel, repair task, artifact contract, or scheduler policy, and whether recurrence rate falls.

\paragraph{Perspective and efficiency ablations.} Remove or weaken skeptic, methodologist, supervisor, validation, memory, or scheduling components under controlled budgets. The outcome should not be only task completion. It should include overclaim rate, missed validation failures, disagreement-to-action conversion, maturity gain, and audit burden.

\section{Governance details}
\label{sec:appendix-governance-details}

A research harness makes autonomous trial-and-error safer and more auditable while preserving human responsibility. If optimized poorly, it becomes a more efficient machine for plausible weak science. The main risks are scientific spam, rhetorical overclaiming, data integrity failures, metric gaming, unsafe trial-and-error in high-risk domains, negative memory suppressing valid exploration, perspective theater, and self-evolution drift.

The corresponding mitigations are claim-evidence gates, AI-use disclosure, human accountability, negative-evidence reporting, domain-specific safety gates, hidden injected failures, protected integrity constraints, and periodic audit of memory overlays. The most important policy point is simple: human authors must be able to understand, defend, and rewrite the final submission. Autonomous systems can help create evidence traces and drafts; they should not be treated as accountable authors.
