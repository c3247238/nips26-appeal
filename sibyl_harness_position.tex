\title{Autonomous Research Needs Self-Evolving Trial-and-Error Harnesses, Not Paper Generators}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
As LLM-driven agents become more capable, fully autonomous research is becoming an active systems problem. Existing autoresearch frameworks mostly focus on scaffolds, tools, and pipelines for generating ideas, running experiments, and drafting papers. We argue that this is necessary but not sufficient: strong research also requires accumulated judgment, the compressed experience formed through repeated attempts, failures, successes, critique, and reflection. Autonomous research agents therefore need an experience substrate, not only static model knowledge and tool access.
We propose Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses: systems that let agents interact repeatedly with real research environments under bounded controls, preserve positive and negative outcomes, summarize lessons into long-term memory, and use those lessons to change future behavior. We operationalize this as \emph{trial-to-behavior conversion}: an auditable mapping from a trial signal at iteration $t$ to a future action at iteration $t+k$ across planning, validation, claim maturity, scheduling, critique, and writing scope. We also argue that the harness itself must evolve, turning recurring process failures into prompt overlays, validation gates, telemetry requirements, scheduler policies, repair tasks, and protected constraints.
We instantiate this agent-harness co-evolution in \sibyl{}, a file-backed autonomous research harness deployed across nine research workspaces with system-evolution records. Across these runs, \sibyl{} converts research traces and process failures into behavior changes: weak claims are sent back to validation, negative results influence future planning, recurring artifact failures become harness-level checks, and system weaknesses become repair targets. Autonomous research systems should be evaluated by whether accumulated trial experience changes both the research agent and the harness that hosts it.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

As LLMs and LLM-driven agents improve in reasoning, coding, tool use, and long-horizon execution, autonomous research is becoming a concrete systems problem. The question is no longer only how human researchers can use LLMs as assistants. It is whether an agent can conduct a full research cycle in a domain with minimal human intervention: search the literature, form hypotheses, choose methods, run experiments, interpret results, revise claims, and draft a paper. Recent systems already span large parts of this loop. End-to-end AI scientist systems move from idea generation to draft papers \citep{lu2024aiscientist,yamada2025aiscientistv2}. Research assistant and co-scientist systems help formulate hypotheses, synthesize literature, and rank candidates \citep{schmidgall2025agentlaboratory,gottweis2025aicoscientist,ghareeb2025robin}. Metric-driven loops modify code to optimize a visible score \citep{karpathy2026autoresearch}. These systems show impressive automation, but they leave open a deeper question: what would let an agent improve as a researcher over repeated research attempts?

The current design target is often a scaffold, tool interface, or research pipeline: give agents roles, tools, execution environments, and writing stages so they can complete research tasks. We argue that these are necessary but insufficient. A pipeline can produce a finished-looking paper while failing to carry forward the experience that an earlier metric was broken, a pilot was underpowered, a baseline was invalid, or a reviewer objection should have blocked a claim. Completing research stages is not the same as becoming better at research. The missing capability is accumulated judgment.

Human researchers produce good ideas, results, and papers not only because they know facts or can use tools. Effective researchers develop inspiration, taste, suspicion, and domain understanding through many concrete encounters with a field. They learn from implementation details, failed pilots, surprising successes, benchmark quirks, related work, reviewer objections, lab notes, and repeated reflection. We call this \emph{research judgment}: the practical form of what researchers often describe as intuition, taste, or domain understanding. It is compressed experience that tells a researcher what is worth trying, which metrics are fragile, which baselines are suspicious, when a negative result is informative, and when a polished story is running ahead of the evidence~\citep{ericsson1996expert}.

Autonomous research agents need an analogous experience substrate. Static model knowledge and tool access can start a research loop, but they cannot replace the process of trying different theoretical routes, exploring alternative methods, inspecting the details of datasets and benchmarks, comparing against related work, absorbing critique, and reflecting after each project. A research agent's past attempts should shape its future priors: what to try, what to distrust, what to validate, what to abandon, and how strongly to state a claim. Without this conversion, each project begins like a new researcher with no lab history.

\textbf{Autonomous research needs a Scientific Trial-and-Error Harness, not a paper generator.} A finished-looking paper can hide immature evidence. A harness is not merely a pipeline that moves an agent from idea to manuscript. It is the external research environment that lets agents interact with real research tasks under bounded controls, fail without corrupting the evidence base, preserve positive and negative outcomes, summarize lessons into long-term memory, and use those lessons to guide future behavior. A paper is then an expression of an evidence state, not the primary unit of research capability.

The bridge in this paper is \emph{trial-to-behavior conversion}: accumulated trial history visibly changes future research behavior. This is the observable footprint of accumulated research judgment in an autonomous system. A system has learned from a failed pilot when later plans ask for stronger controls, experiment agents run validation first, critics check the same metric failure, supervisors downgrade claims, schedulers prefer cheap sanity checks, or writers narrow the narrative. We also introduce \emph{trial-to-harness-behavior conversion}: recurring failures change the harness itself through new checks, prompt overlays, scheduler policies, telemetry requirements, repair tasks, or protected constraints. Together these two conversions define \emph{agent-harness co-evolution}: the agent accumulates domain judgment, while the harness learns how to make future research loops safer, cheaper, and more informative. Figure~\ref{fig:hook} gives the framing.

This position came from building \sibyl{}, a file-backed autonomous research harness, deploying it across nine research workspaces, and inspecting system-evolution records from a working autonomous research stack. The harness functions below were not invented top-down. Each was a response to failures encountered while running real autonomous research: missing figures across rounds, stale artifacts, underpowered experiments, broken proxy metrics, unsupported claims, GPU telemetry gaps, and synchronization failures between planning, evidence, and writing artifacts. In \sibyl{}, these traces become design pressure: weak claims are sent back to validation, negative results influence future planning, recurring artifact failures become harness-level checks, and system weaknesses become repair targets.

We make four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A judgment-centered framing for autonomous research.} We argue that autonomous research systems should be designed to accumulate \emph{research judgment}: the experience-backed ability to choose directions, distrust weak signals, interpret negative results, and calibrate claims across repeated research attempts.
  \item \textbf{Auditable conversion targets.} We introduce \emph{trial-to-behavior conversion} and \emph{trial-to-harness-behavior conversion} as concrete targets for measuring whether trial history changes future research behavior and future harness behavior.
  \item \textbf{Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses.} We define a framework for bounded, memory-bearing research trial-and-error in which trial experience changes both future research behavior and future harness behavior.
  \item \textbf{A concrete instantiation in \sibyl{}.} We instantiate this design in a file-backed autonomous research harness and audit it through nine research workspaces, controlled ablations, and system-evolution records that expose how research traces become behavior updates and harness repairs.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}

Autonomous research draws on several lines of work that make parts of the research process executable. End-to-end AI scientist systems show that LLM agents can generate ideas, run experiments, interpret results, and draft manuscripts \citep{lu2024aiscientist,yamada2025aiscientistv2}. Research assistant and co-scientist systems emphasize hypothesis generation, literature synthesis, human collaboration, and candidate prioritization \citep{schmidgall2025agentlaboratory,gottweis2025aicoscientist,ghareeb2025robin}. These systems establish that agents can participate in large portions of the scientific workflow.

Metric-driven autoresearch and verifier-rich systems demonstrate the value of repeated autonomous trials when feedback is clear. Autoresearch loops can search over code or methods against a visible objective \citep{karpathy2026autoresearch,analemma2026fars}, while AlphaEvolve-style systems exploit strong automatic evaluators in algorithmic spaces \citep{novikov2025alphaevolve}. PaperBench evaluates whether agents can reproduce existing AI research \citep{starace2025paperbench}. These settings clarify what becomes possible when a system can try, measure, and revise.

Long-running agent engineering contributes the infrastructure needed to keep such processes reliable: harnesses, tools, tracing, and guardrails \citep{anthropic2025harnesses,anthropic2025tools,openai2026tracing,openai2026guardrails}. Memory, reflection, provenance, and reproducibility work provide complementary ingredients for learning from previous attempts and grounding claims in artifacts \citep{shinn2023reflexion,packer2023memgpt,lebo2013provo,gundersen2018reproducibility,ioannidis2005why}. Our paper builds on these ingredients, but asks a narrower systems question: whether trial experience is converted into durable research behavior and durable harness behavior.

\section{Autonomous research systems and missing update paths}

Existing systems have made many research actions executable: agents can propose hypotheses, search literature, run code, optimize metrics, use tools, write drafts, preserve context, and expose traces. The remaining bottleneck is not whether an autonomous system can perform these actions once. It is whether the system gets better because of what happened. A weak claim, failed metric, negative result, reviewer objection, or process failure should become a constraint on future behavior.

We call these constraints \emph{update paths}. An update path is the route by which a trial signal changes what the agent or harness does later. Missing update paths explain why a system can look productive while failing to accumulate research judgment. A paper can become smoother without the evidence becoming stronger; a metric can improve while drifting away from the scientific question; a lesson can remain in context without changing the next role that needs it; a harness can log the same process failure without making that failure harder to repeat.

Table~\ref{tab:failuremodes} summarizes the main architectural pressures. The diagnostic claim is not that prior systems lack value. It is that completing research actions, preserving context, or running more trials does not by itself create the experience substrate described in the introduction. Experience becomes useful only when it changes future research behavior or the environment in which future research happens.

\section{Scientific Trial-and-Error as Agent-Harness Co-Evolution}
\label{sec:harness}

The diagnosis above suggests a different unit of analysis for autonomous research. The central unit is not the paper, the prompt, or the agent role. It is the \emph{trial}: a bounded encounter with a real research environment that produces a signal about a hypothesis, method, measurement, baseline, validation check, or process. A trial is valuable when it changes future behavior. A failed trial can be especially valuable because it rules out a tempting story, exposes a fragile metric, or reveals a weakness in the surrounding research environment.

This is how human researchers develop research judgment. They do not acquire taste only by reading facts or using tools. They accumulate compressed experience from attempts that succeeded, attempts that failed, objections that changed their standards, and details that taught them what to distrust. Autonomous research agents need an analogous experience substrate. Static model knowledge can start a project, and tools can execute actions, but repeated trials are what let the agent form priors over what is promising, fragile, suspicious, or ready to claim.

\textbf{Trial-to-behavior conversion} is the agent-side loop. A trial signal changes future research behavior: which direction to try, which evidence to distrust, which validation to run earlier, how strongly to state a claim, when to stop a branch, and when to delay writing. The conversion must be more than a verbal summary. It must appear in a later plan, experiment, review, schedule, or claim boundary.

\textbf{Trial-to-harness-behavior conversion} is the harness-side loop. Some trial signals are not only about the research question; they are about the environment that produced the trial. Repeated missing artifacts, incomplete telemetry, stale outputs, synchronization errors, or gates that admit weak evidence are signals that the harness itself should change. The future trial should be collected under better constraints.

Together these loops define \emph{agent-harness co-evolution}. The research loop lets the agent accumulate domain judgment from trials. The harness loop improves the conditions under which later trials are proposed, executed, checked, remembered, and audited. The key claim is that autonomous research should not only search within a fixed harness. It should also use its own failures to improve the harness that shapes the next search.

Evidence maturity is the boundary between trial-and-error and paper generation. Execution completion, pilot signal, analysis-ready evidence, paper-ready evidence, and audited claim are different states. Moving between them requires validation, scope control, and auditability. This distinction matters because a completed run can still support only a weak signal, while a negative result can raise maturity by ruling out a false story. A Scientific Trial-and-Error Harness is therefore any research environment that makes these maturity changes explicit and converts trial history into future agent behavior and future harness behavior.

\section{\sibyl{}: how the framework is implemented}
\label{sec:sibyl}

\sibyl{} instantiates the framework above as a file-backed, multi-stage autonomous research harness. The purpose is to make experience accumulation observable: a state machine records which research stage is active, which decision allows the next transition, which trial signals changed future research behavior, and which recurring process failures changed the harness itself.

\sibyl{} is organized as six planes. The control plane contains explicit decision and quality gates. The evidence plane stores workspace artifacts, event logs, reviews, and claim traces. The memory plane turns reflection into issue categories and prompt overlays. The role plane separates innovators, skeptics, methodologists, critics, supervisors, and writers. The compute plane treats experiments as asynchronous workloads with GPU scheduling, dependencies, leases, monitoring, and recovery. The evolution plane accumulates cross-project lessons, routes issues to affected roles or system components, and creates repair tasks when the harness itself must change.

These planes implement seven surfaces. \textbf{Trial orchestration} gives each trial a contract: question, expected outputs, dependencies, and stop conditions. \textbf{Evidence boundary} keeps execution state separate from claim maturity through gates and scoped advancement. \textbf{Research memory} stores obligations rather than transcripts, routing lessons to the roles that must act on them. \textbf{Perspective separation} makes objections actionable by turning critique into tasks, claim changes, or stopped branches. \textbf{Resource efficiency} makes scheduling and stopping responsive to evidence quality. \textbf{Artifact trace and claim-evidence graphs} make learning externally inspectable. \textbf{Harness self-evolution} turns recurring process failures into system changes. In \sibyl{}, writing and research do not have the same permissions. A writer can synthesize validated claims, but cannot silently upgrade a pilot result. A supervisor can allow advancement, but only with scoped risks. A critic can object, but the objection must become a task or boundary condition. A scheduler can run long experiments, but should first exploit cheap checks when the expected evidence value is low. These are implementation choices, but in autonomous research they become scientific integrity mechanisms.

The implementation runs as two coupled loops. The research loop coordinates a single project: debate an idea, plan trials, run experiments, analyze evidence, write scoped claims, review, reflect, and pass a quality gate. The harness loop converts recurring failures into lessons, prompt overlays, artifact checks, telemetry requirements, and repair tasks that later projects inherit. These design choices make \sibyl{} auditable at the point where experience should matter: a failed trial can be traced to a later plan mutation, a reviewer objection to a validation task, and a repeated infrastructure failure to a new gate or repair request.

\section{Process evidence from \sibyl{} workspaces and evolution records}

The evidence is process-level rather than outcome-only: workspace artifacts and evolution records show how trial signals translated, or failed to translate, into research behavior and harness behavior changes. \paragraph{\texttt{dynamic-wd}: repair before advancement.} The workspace studied dynamic weight decay, including a candidate algorithm called Universal Dynamic Weight Decay Control (UDWDC). Stability concerns, budget confounds, raw-log mismatch, and weakened hypotheses made the original story unsafe. A weaker system would have either continued writing with caveats or abandoned the direction as failed. \sibyl{} took a third path: a supervisor issued \texttt{REFINE}, and the plan then inserted a repaired UDWDC variant, validation tasks, budget assertions, raw-log cross-checks, and narrower taxonomy claims. After verified repair and a residual-risk audit, the system issued a calibrated \texttt{ADVANCE}. The behavior update crossed role boundaries: supervisor judgment changed planning, planning changed experiment contracts, experiment repair changed claim scope, and reflection changed future checking habits. The counterfactual is observable in \texttt{ablation-mem-negative}, where without routed refinement signals the system cycled on a flawed framework for four iterations.

\paragraph{\texttt{dlm-acceleration}: negative results calibrate belief.} Diffusion language models (DLMs) generate text through iterative denoising rather than ordinary left-to-right decoding. The workspace encountered unsupported statistics, baseline mismatch, metric confusion, and \texttt{NO\_GO} engineering outcomes. The useful output was not a stronger speedup claim, but the deletion of unsupported statistics, a shift toward composition-interference analysis, and a lesson that cheap sanity checks should precede expensive pilots. Negative evidence here protected the research budget and prevented a coherent but false acceleration narrative: when the visible score is misaligned with the scientific question, optimizing it faster makes the system worse. Compare \texttt{ablation-mem-weakened}, where the absence of routed sanity-check lessons let an under-powered study proceed without flagging.

\paragraph{\texttt{sae-absorption}: writing is not progress.} Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are interpretability models that represent neural activations with sparse features. The workspace exhibited a long-horizon failure mode in which repeated writing and polishing did not resolve data hygiene, stale numbers, or claim drift. The improvement was an experiment-first turn: targeted experiments, negative results, and validation pressure changed the claim hierarchy and made a weaker but more defensible story possible. The companion \texttt{sae-absorption-kimi} workspace adds a measurement-crisis boundary case that shows why validation must precede narrative generation. A research harness must treat ``make the paper cleaner'' and ``make the evidence cleaner'' as different operations, because fluent writing masks the absence of new evidence. In \texttt{ablation-no-debate}, where critique was structurally absent, similar writing-first patterns persisted: the trained SAE looked geometrically indistinguishable from a shuffled control, and no role pushed back.

\paragraph{System-evolution trace: the harness learns from its own failures.} The \texttt{.sibyl/evolution} records in the running system normalize reflection outputs into issue categories, severities, affected agents, suggestions, statuses, and success patterns. The records capture repeated writing and experiment failures such as missing rendered figures, placeholder references, paper-length overflow, absent code-release plans, underpowered experiments, stale artifacts, and paper/LaTeX desynchronization. They also mark infrastructure issues that require system changes, including incomplete GPU telemetry with empty timing fields, runtime-lineage artifacts that reviewers would otherwise have to reconstruct manually, non-self-contained evidence bundles with absolute paths, and external synchronization failures. This is trial-to-harness-behavior conversion: the harness learns not only that a project should change its claim, but that the research environment should add a gate, telemetry field, artifact contract, repair task, or role-specific prompt overlay before the next project repeats the failure.

\paragraph{Controlled ablations as counterfactual evidence.} Three ablations disable individual harness components and expose the failure that a fully configured run would have prevented (Table~\ref{tab:ablations}). \texttt{ablation-no-debate} (\texttt{debate\_rounds: 0}) produced a strong primary signal of Cohen's $d=8.94$ on the absorption hypothesis, but conflated signal strength with methodological soundness: the steering validation showed zero improvement for both absorbed and non-absorbed features, and the shuffled baseline nearly matched the trained SAE, indicating a geometric artifact rather than a learned effect, with no role pushing back. \texttt{ablation-mem-negative} cycled on a circular ground-truth framework for four iterations, each iteration reformulating the same proxy-metric definition rather than questioning the framework itself, with final F1 near random ($\approx 5\times 10^{-4}$); long context preserved the prior trials in tokens, but routed memory would have blocked the recurrence structurally. \texttt{ablation-mem-weakened} accepted an under-powered study ($n=26$, power $\approx 65\%$ for $|r|\geq 0.5$) without flagging the issue: three controls (random baseline, alternative metric, cross-model replication) were relegated to ``limitations'' rather than executed as planner-level prerequisites.

\section{Evaluation, governance, and alternative views}

If the unit of progress is accumulated experience converted into better research behavior and better harness behavior, evaluation should change. Figure~\ref{fig:evaluation} gives a compact view.

We propose ten evaluation dimensions, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:evaluation}. The first six extend standard benchmarks: conversion, maturity gain, trace fidelity, boundary effectiveness, resource efficiency, and audit burden. Four dimensions capture the main argument of this paper. \emph{Experience accumulation} asks whether repeated trials create a better prior over what to try, distrust, validate, or stop. \emph{Memory reuse} asks whether a lesson identified in one project changes the prior of a planner, critic, or supervisor in a later project, not just whether the lesson is restated in context. \emph{Harness self-evolution} asks whether repeated process failures change gates, artifact contracts, scheduler policies, telemetry, or repair tasks. \emph{Self-evolution safety} asks whether those changes preserve scientific integrity rather than drifting toward fluent paper production.

These dimensions imply concrete protocols. A retrospective audit can reconstruct trial signals, behavior updates, maturity changes, and evidence paths from a completed workspace. A prospective fixed-budget study can compare harness designs under the same research question and compute budget. An injected-failure stress test can add duplicate data, missing results, stale tables, or unsupported statistics and check whether the system blocks narrative generation. A memory test can ask whether a failure in one project changes behavior in a later project. A harness-evolution test can deliberately repeat a process failure across projects and measure whether it becomes a gate, artifact contract, telemetry requirement, or repair task before the third occurrence.

The strongest near-term protocol is the injected-failure stress test because it is controlled and directly tied to scientific integrity. Concretely, the harness should be probed against duplicate result files, confidence-interval inversion, stale tables, feature-count mismatch between the manifest and the run output, missing seeds, and unsupported significance claims (e.g., reported p-values without a corresponding test statistic). A passing system blocks narrative generation, routes the issue to the role responsible for the affected artifact, and preserves the negative evidence for future memory. This moves evaluation away from whether an agent can write a good-looking paper and toward whether it can defend the paper's evidential boundary.

There are serious counterarguments. One view is that paper generation is a sufficient benchmark, because a good paper implicitly requires good evidence. We disagree: a fluent draft hides weak claims unless the evidence boundary is explicit. In \texttt{sae-absorption}, multiple writing iterations polished the same stale tables, and only the experiment-first redirection identified the data-integrity gap. A second view is that metric-driven loops are enough. They are enough when the metric is a reliable evaluator, but open-ended science often asks whether the metric itself is meaningful: when the metric is questionable, optimizing it faster makes the system worse, as \texttt{dlm-acceleration} showed when a fluent acceleration narrative formed around an unstable speedup metric. A third view is that humans should remain in the loop, so no long-term autonomous memory is needed. Human responsibility is non-negotiable, but human oversight is feasible at scale only when the system exposes an artifact trace, otherwise audit cost grows with every claim. A fourth view is that long context replaces memory. \texttt{ablation-mem-negative} demonstrated otherwise: tokens recorded the prior failure, but without routing the lesson the system reformulated the same broken framework four times. A fifth view is that harness self-evolution is merely engineering hygiene. In autonomous research, engineering hygiene is part of scientific method: missing telemetry, stale artifacts, and invalid claim gates determine what evidence the agent can learn from.

The harness is also allowed to improve, but only through versioned changes, protected constraints, tests, and human review for safety-relevant surfaces. In low-risk computational domains, this permits substantial autonomous iteration. In high-risk domains, the same harness logic should become stricter, with human approval gates before real-world actions and domain-specific safety constraints before trials begin.

\section{Conclusion}

Autonomous research should not be judged mainly by whether a system can generate a complete paper. A paper is an expression of an evidence state. The harder capability is whether a system can accumulate research experience: conduct controlled trials, preserve what went right and wrong, revise its future behavior, improve the harness that hosts it, and make the whole process auditable.

We argued that research judgment can be operationalized as accumulated trial experience whose effects appear as trial-to-behavior conversion. We proposed Scientific Trial-and-Error Harnesses as the design target for autonomous research. We described \sibyl{} as one concrete implementation, with decision gates, artifact traces, role-separated agents, routed memory, GPU-aware experiment control, and system-evolution records. The central claim is that the next generation of autonomous research systems should compete on how safely, efficiently, and honestly they convert trial history into future research behavior and future harness behavior.

\subsection{State machine and typed actions}

\sibyl{} uses an explicit multi-stage artifact-backed state machine. The current stage list includes initialization, literature search, idea debate, planning, pilot experiments, idea validation, experiment cycles, result debate, experiment decisions, outline writing, section writing, integration, final writing review, LaTeX generation, review, reflection, quality gate, and completion. We avoid using a brittle stage count in the main text because the scientific claim is not the number of stages; it is that stage transitions are file-backed and gate-controlled.

The orchestrator also returns typed actions rather than only natural-language instructions. Examples include single-skill calls, parallel skill calls, team actions, parallel agents, shell execution, GPU polling, experiment waiting, completion, and stopped states. This matters because a typed action can be inspected, retried, or blocked. A free-form instruction is harder to audit and harder to connect to later behavior.

\subsection{GPU scheduler and recovery}

The compute plane treats experiments as asynchronous workloads. A task plan is validated, converted into dependency layers, assigned resource needs, and dispatched under GPU leases. The scheduler polls device availability, tracks running tasks, records progress files, and supports recovery when an experiment is interrupted. This makes trials cheaper to monitor and easier to resume, which affects how quickly evidence can mature.

\subsection{Reflection and evolution memory}

The memory layer starts from reflection artifacts, but it should not end there. Reflection outputs are normalized into issue categories such as system, experiment, writing, analysis, planning, pipeline, ideation, and efficiency. The evolution layer maintains digests and lessons, computes relevance, and injects selected lessons into later prompts. This is the mechanism behind the paper's distinction between long context and research memory. Long context can expose past text; routed memory can change which checks a role performs.

Important future work is to measure memory effectiveness rather than memory volume. A lesson informs the system only if it reduces repeated failures, changes a plan, adds a validation step, changes a claim boundary, or repairs the harness path that allowed the failure. A lesson can also be harmful if it overgeneralizes from a weak failure and suppresses a valid direction. This is why memory requires source links, uncertainty, decay, and re-opening criteria.

\subsection{Decision gates, self-heal, and writing boundaries}

The current system exposes idea validation, experiment decisions, quality gates, review stages, and a structured self-heal substrate. The self-heal layer includes error collection, routing, protected-file constraints, deterministic fixers for recurring failure classes, and repair-task generation. These mechanisms improve workflow reliability and create places where failures become tasks before they recur in the next research loop.

For future versions, the most important boundary is a validated claim registry. Writing agents should consume only claims that have maturity labels, artifact links, and validation status. Pilot signals should remain usable, but only as pilot signals. A paper should be generated from the claim registry, not from whatever narrative is most fluent after the latest experiment.

\section{Evaluation protocols}

\paragraph{Retrospective trial-to-behavior audit.} Given a completed workspace, reconstruct the project goal, trial signals, harness mechanisms triggered, behavior updates, maturity labels, negative evidence, and evidence paths. The audit fails if the claimed behavior update cannot be tied to artifacts.

\paragraph{Prospective fixed-budget study.} Give several harness designs the same research question, compute budget, and human review budget. Compare maturity gain, trace fidelity, unsupported claims, negative-result handling, compute reliability, and human audit burden.

\paragraph{Injected-failure stress test.} Insert controlled failures such as duplicate result files, missing outputs, stale tables, inconsistent feature counts, unsupported statistics, or pilot/full mismatches. A strong harness should block or downgrade narrative generation and point to the offending artifact.

\paragraph{Cross-project memory test.} Let one project expose a failure mode, then start another project where the same failure could recur. Measure whether the lesson is retrieved, routed to the right role, and converted into a changed plan or validation check.

\paragraph{Harness-evolution test.} Let one project expose a process failure such as missing telemetry, stale figure assets, absolute artifact paths, or paper/LaTeX desynchronization. Start later projects where the same failure could recur. Measure whether the harness adds a gate, sentinel, repair task, artifact contract, or scheduler policy, and whether recurrence rate falls.

\paragraph{Perspective and efficiency ablations.} Remove or weaken skeptic, methodologist, supervisor, validation, memory, or scheduling components under controlled budgets. The outcome should not be only task completion. It should include overclaim rate, missed validation failures, disagreement-to-action conversion, maturity gain, and audit burden.

\section{Governance details}

A research harness makes autonomous trial-and-error safer and more auditable while preserving human responsibility. If optimized poorly, it becomes a more efficient machine for plausible weak science. The main risks are scientific spam, rhetorical overclaiming, data integrity failures, metric gaming, unsafe trial-and-error in high-risk domains, negative memory suppressing valid exploration, perspective theater, and self-evolution drift.

The corresponding mitigations are claim-evidence gates, AI-use disclosure, human accountability, negative-evidence reporting, domain-specific safety gates, hidden injected failures, protected integrity constraints, and periodic audit of memory overlays. The most important policy point is simple: human authors must be able to understand, defend, and rewrite the final submission. Autonomous systems can help create evidence traces and drafts; they should not be treated as accountable authors.
